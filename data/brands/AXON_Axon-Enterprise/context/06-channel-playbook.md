# 06 — Channel Playbook
## Axon Enterprise (AXON)

---

## 1. Channel Philosophy

Axon's channel strategy must serve three distinct buyer journeys simultaneously: the government procurement cycle (12–36 months, committee-driven), the officer end-user adoption cycle (immediate, peer-influenced), and the policy/advocacy cycle (relationship-driven, non-linear). No single channel owns all three. The playbook assigns each channel a primary job and a secondary audience, and governs how they interact.

The governing principle: **meet buyers where authority lives**. For chiefs, that is peer networks and conferences. For commissioners, that is written evidence and peer officials. For officers, that is word-of-mouth and on-the-job demonstration. Digital channels support and amplify — they rarely initiate.

## 3. Channel Deep Dives

### Channel 1: Direct Sales (Field Representatives)

**Role:** The primary revenue channel. Axon's sales model is relationship-driven, agency-by-agency, with named account reps for agencies above a threshold size.

**Cadence:**
- Annual agency business reviews (for existing customers)
- Quarterly check-ins with IT directors on adoption metrics
- Pre-budget-cycle outreach (typically 6–9 months before agency fiscal year close)
- Event-triggered outreach: new legislation, high-profile incidents, federal grant announcements

**Enablement tools:**
- ROI calculator (litigation cost avoidance model)
- RFP response kit (pre-built procurement language)
- Reference agency program (warm peer introductions)
- Battle cards (per competitive landscape file)
- Grant support team handoff

**Key behavior:** Sales reps are trained to sell to the ecosystem, not to the product. Every conversation starts with the agency's current evidence workflow pain — not with TASER or camera specs. The product conversation follows the problem diagnosis.

### Channel 2: Conference & Events

**Priority events:**

| Event | Audience | Axon Role | Objective |
|---|---|---|---|
| IACP Annual Conference | Chiefs, commissioners, federal | Keynote + exhibit hall | Pipeline + brand dominance |
| SHOT Show | Officers, procurement, media | Exhibit + product launch | Product awareness |
| PERF Annual Summit | Chiefs, policy researchers | Speaking + roundtable | Thought leadership |
| National Sheriffs' Association | Sheriffs, undersheriffs | Exhibit + networking | Tier 2 agency pipeline |
| NDAA (prosecutors) | DAs, ADAs | Sponsorship + new audience | Evidence.com expansion |
| CES / tech conferences | Investors, media, recruits | Innovation positioning | Investor + talent narrative |

**Conference content formula:**
- 60% officer/chief testimonials (peer voices carry more weight than vendor claims)
- 25% live product demonstration (evidence upload speed, Draft One AI demo)
- 15% policy/data presentation (use-of-force reduction metrics, ROI data)

**Never at conferences:** Feature-list slide decks, pricing discussions in public sessions, any competitive attack by name.

### Channel 3: LinkedIn

**Primary use:** Thought leadership for chiefs, IT directors, and policy stakeholders. Secondary use: recruiting (engineering, policy, sales talent).

**Content mix:**
- 40% officer/chief testimonials (video clips, quote cards)
- 25% data/impact posts (use-of-force reduction numbers, case closure metrics)
- 20% policy commentary (legislative developments, grant announcements)
- 15% product/feature updates (framed as officer safety improvements, never spec-sheet posts)

**Posting cadence:** 4–5 times per week (company page); CEO Rick Smith personal page posts 2–3 times per week with higher-level narrative content.

**Tone on LinkedIn:** Authoritative but not corporate. First-person officer voices in quotes. Data always cited. No self-congratulation without a supporting outcome metric.

**Paid LinkedIn:** Retargeting campaigns timed to budget cycles; job title targeting (Chief of Police, IT Director, County Commissioner); sponsored content amplifying case studies and ROI calculator.

### Channel 4: YouTube

**Primary use:** Product demonstrations and officer testimonials for end-user awareness and consideration.

**Content series:**

| Series | Format | Length | Audience |
|---|---|---|---|
| "In the Field" | Officer testimonial documentary | 3–6 min | Chiefs, officers |
| "How It Works" | Product walkthrough / demo | 60–90 sec | IT directors, officers |
| "The Evidence Chain" | Evidence.com workflow demo | 2–4 min | DAs, IT directors |
| "Draft One" | AI report writing demo | 60–90 sec | Officers, chiefs |
| "Public Safety Futures" | Policy/thought leadership interview | 8–12 min | Commissioners, press |

**SEO strategy on YouTube:** Target search terms like "body camera evidence management demo," "AI police report writing," "TASER 10 review," and "less-lethal weapons training."

**What not to do on YouTube:** Anything that looks like a weapons advertisement without context. Every TASER video must show the de-escalation framing — the goal of avoiding a firearm, not the spectacle of the device.

### Channel 5: Email (Agency Newsletters & Lifecycle)

**List segments:**
- Chiefs / administrators (decision-makers): monthly digest, policy-forward
- IT directors: bi-weekly product update, integration news
- Officers (end-users via agency distribution): monthly tips, Draft One updates, training reminders
- Commissioners / budget officers: quarterly ROI/impact summary
- Grant prospects: triggered by federal grant announcement cycles

**Email content rules:**
- Subject lines lead with outcomes, not features: "How Riverside PD cut report time by 40%" not "New Draft One Feature Release"
- Every email has one primary CTA: demo request, case study download, grant worksheet, or reference call request
- Unsubscribe respect is a brand value — officers who opt out of marketing still receive safety/compliance communications through their agency admin

**Lifecycle triggers:**
- New agency signed: onboarding sequence (30/60/90 day adoption milestones)
- Renewal approaching: 6-month ROI summary + expansion offer
- Incident in coverage area: empathy-first outreach with relevant resources (not a sales pitch)
- Federal grant announcement: immediate grant support offer

### Channel 6: Axon Academy (LMS)

**Role:** The highest-retention channel. Agencies that complete Axon Academy onboarding have significantly higher product adoption and renewal rates.

**Content:**
- Officer certification courses: TASER deployment, body camera policy, Evidence.com upload procedures
- Administrator courses: policy configuration, supervisory review workflows, compliance reporting
- AI literacy: Draft One accuracy expectations, supervisor review requirements, legal admissibility guidance
- Optional: Community policing integration (how body camera data supports community relations)

**Format:** Video-led modules (under 10 minutes each), interactive scenario simulations, certification assessments with passing scores recorded in agency compliance dashboard.

**Business impact:** Axon Academy completion is correlated with contract renewal. Agencies with 80%+ officer certification rates renew at 96%+ (estimated internal benchmark).

### Channel 7: Earned Media & PR

**Target outlets:**
- Law enforcement trade: Police Chief Magazine, Law Officer, Law Enforcement Today
- Policy/governance: Government Executive, Route Fifty, Governing Magazine
- National press: New York Times (technology/policing beat), Washington Post, The Atlantic
- Tech press: Wired, MIT Technology Review, TechCrunch (for AI/innovation stories)

**PR narrative priorities:**
1. Use-of-force reduction data (lead with outcomes, not technology)
2. AI transparency and bias auditing (preempt critical coverage)
3. Officer exoneration by body camera (accountability works both ways)
4. Federal compliance and grant success (policy-relevant business outcomes)

**Crisis communications protocol:**
- Any incident involving a Axon-equipped officer: factual, restrained statement within 4 hours
- Any TASER-related in-custody death: immediate medical context statement; never lead with product defense
- Any data breach or privacy concern (Evidence.com): immediate disclosure, mitigation steps, third-party audit announcement
- Rule: never let a critic define the narrative for more than 24 hours without a factual response

### Channel 8: Federal & State Policy Channels

**Audiences:** Congressional staffers, DOJ, BJA, state legislators, state AG offices, police oversight boards.

**Channels used:**
- Direct lobbying (Axon Government Affairs team in Washington, DC)
- Coalition membership: Major Cities Chiefs Association, PERF, IACP legislative committees
- Testimony: Congressional hearings on police reform, AI in law enforcement, federal grant programs
- Comment letters: NIST AI standards, DOJ consent decree monitoring, FTC privacy rulemaking

**Content produced for policy channels:**
- Technical comment letters (AI bias, privacy standards)
- Policy white papers ("A Framework for Responsible Body Camera Deployment")
- Grant program guides (distributed to agency budget offices before federal application cycles)

**Tone in policy channels:** Deferential to process, data-driven, bipartisan-framed. Axon does not take partisan positions. The frame is always: "What does the evidence show?" not "What does Axon believe?"

## 4. Battle Cards — Channel Layer

### Battle Card 1: When Motorola's Field Team Gets There First

**Situation:** Axon learns a Motorola rep has already briefed a target agency's chief and framed the narrative as "you already trust us for radios."

**Channel response:**
- Activate reference agency program immediately: warm introduction from a peer chief in the same state who switched from Motorola to Axon
- Request a separate meeting with the IT director (not the chief) — Motorola rarely has strong IT relationships
- Send a targeted email to the chief's inbox: "What agencies in [state] are saying about Evidence.com" — peer voice, no Axon branding in subject line
- If budget cycle is 3+ months away, offer a free 90-day Evidence.com pilot to a subset of the agency before any camera commitment

**Never:** Counter Motorola's pitch in the same meeting. Win the next meeting.

---

### Battle Card 2: When a Civil Rights Group Is Lobbying Against Axon Adoption

**Situation:** A local ACLU chapter or police reform coalition is actively lobbying a city council to reject or delay an Axon contract vote.

**Channel response:**
- Do not engage the opposition directly in public. Do not rebut in press.
- Brief the chief privately with talking points on data privacy protections and data deletion policies — arm them, don't replace them
- Submit an op-ed from a reform-aligned law enforcement voice (not Axon) to the local paper on how body cameras protect community members, not just officers
- Offer a public briefing for city council members with Axon's policy team (not sales) — invite the opposition to ask questions directly
- Activate Ethics Advisory Board statement if needed

**Never:** Attack the opposition's credibility. Engage their concerns as legitimate and answer them with data.

---

### Battle Card 3: When a Budget Freeze Delays a Decision

**Situation:** A target agency has completed procurement evaluation and selected Axon, but a city council budget freeze delays the vote by 6–12 months.

**Channel response:**
- Immediately connect agency to Axon's grant support team — federal grant cycle may solve the budget problem entirely
- Offer an extended evidence.com pilot at no cost during the freeze period: creates adoption before contract, increases switching cost if they reconsider
- Send quarterly impact reports to the budget office during the freeze: document what the pilot is saving in report-writing time and what incidents have been recorded
- Brief the city council liaison with a one-page ROI document framed as "cost of waiting" — litigation exposure per month without coverage

**Never:** Pressure the agency publicly or reduce price to solve a political problem. Price integrity protects the long-term SaaS model.

## 5. Steal Sheets — Channel Layer

### Steal Sheet 1: Steal the Prosecutor Relationship Channel

**Opportunity:** No competitor has a dedicated channel strategy for prosecutorial offices. DA offices depend on Axon Evidence.com daily but receive no Axon marketing. The result: Evidence.com is treated as a utility, not a competitive differentiator. If Axon builds a prosecutor-facing channel, DAs begin requesting Axon-compatible evidence formats from agencies — creating a demand-pull dynamic that competitors cannot replicate.

**Channel actions:**
- Join NDAA (National District Attorneys Association) as a supporting partner
- Launch "Evidence.com for Prosecutors" — dedicated landing page, email series, conference presence at state DA conferences
- Co-author white paper with state DA association on digital evidence best practices
- Create a DA advisory council (3–5 DAs) that provides formal input on Evidence.com roadmap

**Metric to track:** Percentage of Evidence.com agency contracts where the adjacent DA office is an active Evidence.com user.

---

### Steal Sheet 2: Steal the Small Agency Channel from Non-Adoption

**Opportunity:** Axon's sales model is optimized for mid-to-large agencies (50+ officers). Agencies under 25 officers — the majority of US law enforcement by count — are served poorly by the current model. Competitors (or non-adoption) win by default in this segment. A low-touch digital channel — grant-first, self-serve onboarding, peer cohort support — can capture this segment without dedicated field reps.

**Channel actions:**
- "Axon for Small Agencies" landing page: grant eligibility calculator as primary CTA
- Self-serve Evidence.com trial (no sales rep required for agencies under 25 officers)
- Peer cohort program: small agency chiefs connected in a private Slack/community for shared best practices
- Monthly webinar: "Modernizing on a Small Budget" — federal grant focus, small agency case studies

**Metric to track:** Net new agencies under 25 officers per quarter; grant-assisted closes as a percentage of small agency deals.

---

### Steal Sheet 3: Steal the Officer Influencer Channel from Word-of-Mouth

**Opportunity:** Officer adoption is the most powerful sales lever Axon has — and it is largely unmanaged. Officers who love Draft One or Axon Body cameras evangelize informally. A structured officer influencer program would convert informal advocacy into a documented channel with measurable pipeline impact.

**Channel actions:**
- "Axon Officer Ambassador" program: 50 officers across diverse agencies, geographies, and demographics
- Ambassadors receive early access to product features, a quarterly call with the Axon product team, and a co-branded testimonial asset
- Ambassador content: short LinkedIn and YouTube videos, conference appearances, peer-to-peer reference calls for agencies in evaluation
- Compensation: non-monetary (recognition, access, early features) — avoids FTC disclosure complexity and preserves authenticity

**Metric to track:** Ambassador-influenced pipeline (tracked via UTM and reference call attribution); ambassador content engagement rate vs. brand-produced content.
