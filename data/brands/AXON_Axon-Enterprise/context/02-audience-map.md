# 02 — Audience Map
## Axon Enterprise (AXON)

---

## 1. Audience Overview

Axon operates in a B2G (business-to-government) primary market with secondary B2B (enterprise security) and B2C (consumer TASER) segments. Purchase decisions are layered: end-users (officers) influence, administrators approve, procurement departments execute, and city councils or county boards ultimately fund.

---

## 2. Primary Audience Segments

### Segment A: Law Enforcement Decision-Makers
- Chiefs of Police, Sheriffs, Deputy Commissioners
- IT Directors and Technology Officers within agencies
- Budget and procurement officers
- **Buying motivation:** Liability reduction, officer safety, community trust, federal compliance
- **Pain points:** Budget cycles, union pushback on new tech, public scrutiny of surveillance tools

### Segment B: Frontline Officers (End Users / Influencers)
- Patrol officers, detectives, corrections officers
- **Buying motivation:** Tools that make their job safer and less paperwork-heavy
- **Pain points:** Bulky equipment, complicated evidence upload processes, slow report writing

### Segment C: Government & Policy Stakeholders
- City councils, county commissioners, state legislators
- DOJ / federal oversight bodies
- **Buying motivation:** Accountability mandates, federal funding eligibility, community pressure
- **Pain points:** Civil liberties concerns, cost justification to constituents

### Segment D: Civil Rights & Community Advocates
- ACLU, NAACP, police reform coalitions
- **Role:** Not buyers — but blockers or enablers of agency adoption
- **Axon approach:** Transparency initiatives, ethics advisory board, published use-of-force data

### Segment E: Consumer (TASER Pulse+)
- Personal protection buyers, predominantly suburban/rural
- **Buying motivation:** Self-defense without lethal firearm
- **Pain points:** Legal patchwork by state, price sensitivity

## 3. Personas

### Persona 1: Chief Darnell Washington

**Age:** 54
**Race/Ethnicity:** Black / African American
**Occupation:** Chief of Police, mid-size Southern city (pop. ~180,000)
**Location:** Birmingham, Alabama

**Background:**
Chief Washington rose through the ranks over 28 years, starting as a patrol officer in a city with significant poverty and racial tension. He was one of the first Black chiefs in his department's history. His tenure has been defined by a push to modernize while maintaining community trust after a high-profile use-of-force incident in 2021 put his department under a federal consent decree.

**Goals:**
- Demonstrate measurable reductions in use-of-force incidents to the DOJ oversight committee
- Retain officers in a competitive labor market while managing a flat budget
- Rebuild credibility with Black community leaders who are skeptical of any new surveillance technology

**Frustrations:**
- Vendors who show up with flashy demos but can't answer hard questions about data privacy
- Officers who resist body camera policies because of discipline fears
- City council members who demand accountability tools but vote against the budget to fund them

**Axon Relationship:**
Currently on a legacy body camera contract. Evaluating Axon Body 4 + Evidence.com bundle. His IT director flagged the AI report-writing (Draft One) as a potential officer retention tool. Skeptical of the price but intrigued by the consent decree compliance dashboard.

**Quote:** "I don't need a tech company to tell me policing is broken. I need one that can help me prove to the DOJ — and to this community — that we're fixing it."

**Content that resonates:** Case studies from comparable cities under consent decrees; data on use-of-force reduction; peer testimonials from Black police leadership; ACLU co-authored transparency reports.

### Persona 2: Officer Marisol Reyes-Gutierrez

**Age:** 31
**Race/Ethnicity:** Latina / Mexican-American
**Occupation:** Patrol Officer (4 years), currently training for Detective track
**Location:** Phoenix, Arizona (suburban precinct)

**Background:**
Second-generation immigrant, grew up in a family where distrust of police was common. Joined the force partly to change it from within. Works a high-volume patrol route and spends roughly 2.5 hours per shift on incident reports — time she considers stolen from actual community work. Has a 6-year-old at home. Fatigue and paperwork are her top complaints.

**Goals:**
- Complete detective training and move off patrol within 18 months
- Cut report-writing time so she can actually build community relationships on shift
- Not get burned by body camera footage being used against her unfairly in disciplinary proceedings

**Frustrations:**
- Body camera upload process is slow and she regularly gets compliance warnings
- Previous incident report software crashed mid-entry twice in one month
- Feels like new technology is announced at command level without officer input

**Axon Relationship:**
Uses Axon Body 3 camera daily. Has heard about Draft One AI report writing from a colleague in another precinct and informally asked her sergeant about it. Would be an internal champion if given a trial.

**Quote:** "If this thing can write the first draft while I'm still at the scene, I'll actually be able to eat dinner with my kid."

**Content that resonates:** Short video demos (under 90 seconds); officer-to-officer testimonials; time-savings data; workflow screenshots; Spanish-language materials for family-facing communications.

### Persona 3: Commissioner Priya Nair-Johansson

**Age:** 47
**Race/Ethnicity:** South Asian / Indian-American (married name Johansson)
**Occupation:** County Commissioner, suburban Pacific Northwest county (pop. ~420,000)
**Location:** Snohomish County, Washington State

**Background:**
Former tech attorney turned elected official. Won her seat on a platform of fiscal responsibility and evidence-based governance. Her county's sheriff has requested a full Axon ecosystem upgrade — TASER 10, body cameras, Fleet cameras, and Evidence.com — at a total 5-year cost of $4.2M. She is the swing vote on the budget committee.

**Goals:**
- Justify the $4.2M to constituents who are skeptical of "surveillance expansion"
- Ensure the contract includes data privacy protections that survive a future ACLU challenge
- Demonstrate ROI — either in reduced litigation costs or in federal grant eligibility

**Frustrations:**
- Vendor sales teams that talk to the sheriff but not to her office
- Contracts with opaque data-sharing clauses (she reads the fine print)
- Constituents on both sides: law-and-order voters who want more tech, civil libertarians who want less

**Axon Relationship:**
Has received the full Axon sales deck. Her chief of staff flagged that three neighboring counties adopted Axon and two received DOJ community policing grants afterward. She has requested a meeting with Axon's policy team — not sales — before any vote.

**Quote:** "I need to be able to stand at a town hall in front of 300 people and explain exactly what data this system collects, who can access it, and what happens when the contract ends."

**Content that resonates:** White papers; independent audit reports; data retention and deletion policy documentation; ROI calculators with litigation-cost comparisons; peer commissioner testimonials.

## 4. Audience Journey Map

| Stage | Chief Washington | Officer Reyes-Gutierrez | Commissioner Nair-Johansson |
|---|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Federal mandate, peer network | Colleague word-of-mouth | Budget request from sheriff |
