# 04 — Content DNA
## Bristol Myers Squibb (BMY)
**Sector:** Health Care — Pharmaceuticals
**Key Products:** Opdivo (nivolumab), Eliquis (apixaban)

---

## 1. Content Philosophy

BMY's content operates across two distinct registers that must coexist without contradiction:

1. **Scientific Register:** Peer-to-peer, data-first, mechanism-driven — for HCP audiences
2. **Human Register:** Patient-centric, emotionally resonant, survivorship-anchored — for patient/caregiver audiences

The unifying thread is **credibility** — every piece of content, regardless of audience, must be traceable to evidence. Emotion without evidence is marketing. Evidence without emotion is noise. BMY content aims for the intersection.

## 3. Banned Words

1. **Cure** — Overclaims; violates FDA labeling standards; triggers legal review
2. **Miracle** — Sensationalist; undermines scientific positioning
3. **Revolutionary** — Overused superlative; triggers regulatory scrutiny in claims
4. **Best** — Unsupported comparative unless head-to-head data exists
5. **Guaranteed** — False promise; violates FTC and FDA promotional standards
6. **Magic** — Trivializes disease complexity; incompatible with scientific voice
7. **Cheap** / **Affordable** (unsupported) — Contradicts access program nuance; requires substantiation
8. **Experimental** (for approved indications) — Misrepresents regulatory approval status
9. **Safe** (absolute) — No drug is absolutely safe; "favorable safety profile" is preferred
10. **Simple** (for complex diseases) — Minimizes patient experience and clinical complexity

## 5. Narrative Pillars

### Pillar 1: Scientific Pioneer
**Core Idea:** BMY did not enter the IO space — BMY invented it.
**Content Forms:** Origin story content, CheckMate trial retrospectives, mechanism-of-action animations, "How PD-1 was discovered" editorial content
**Tone:** Authoritative, historical, confident
**Key Proof Points:** First PD-1 inhibitor approval (2014, melanoma); 40+ Opdivo indications; CheckMate trial library spanning 50+ trials

### Pillar 2: Patient Prevailment
**Core Idea:** Patients are not defined by their diagnosis — they prevail.
**Content Forms:** Patient testimonial videos, survivorship campaigns, "Prevail" brand advertising, patient ambassador programs
**Tone:** Hopeful, human, grounded in real stories
**Key Proof Points:** Long-term survivor data in melanoma; durable response rates in RCC and NSCLC; Opdivo 5-year survival data

### Pillar 3: Partnership in Care
**Core Idea:** BMY does not sell drugs — BMY partners with HCPs, patients, and caregivers in a shared mission.
**Content Forms:** Caregiver guides, BMS Together copay program content, HCP patient support tools, community health partnerships
**Tone:** Warm, collaborative, accountable
**Key Proof Points:** BMS Together program; patient navigation services; health equity initiatives; 340B hospital partnerships

### Pillar 4: Pipeline Promise
**Core Idea:** Today's innovation is only the beginning — BMY's pipeline represents the next decade of medicine.
**Content Forms:** Pipeline overview content, mechanism platform explainers, investor-facing R&D narratives, congress pipeline presentations
**Tone:** Forward-looking, confident, ambitious
**Key Proof Points:** 50+ compounds in clinical development; cell therapy platform; I-O + novel mechanisms combinations

## 7. Tone-of-Voice Guidelines by Context

### Corporate / Investor Context
- Measured, formal, data-anchored
- Forward-looking without overclaiming
- Use: Annual Report, Earnings Call, Corporate Website
- Example: "Our science-first strategy continues to yield results — with more than 50 compounds in clinical development and a track record of transformative approvals."

### HCP / Medical Context
- Precise, collegial, evidence-forward
- Peer-to-peer register — not condescending or overly promotional
- Use: Detail aids, CME, MSL dialogues, congress content
- Example: "CheckMate 9LA demonstrated that Opdivo plus Yervoy with two cycles of chemotherapy significantly improved overall survival versus chemotherapy alone in patients with Stage IV or recurrent NSCLC."

### Patient / DTC Context
- Warm, accessible, empowering without overclaiming
- Use plain language; avoid jargon; acknowledge fear and uncertainty
- Use: TV ads, patient guides, social media, patient advocacy events
- Example: "Living with advanced lung cancer is hard. But patients who've been treated with Opdivo are still living their lives — going further than they thought possible."

### Social / Digital Context
- Conversational, community-building, health literacy-focused
- No promotional claims on unbranded platforms
- Use: Twitter/X (corporate), LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram
- Example: "This #LungCancerAwarenessMonth, we're sharing the stories of patients who chose to fight — and prevail. #Opdivo #CancerResearch"

## 10. Steal Sheets — Content DNA

### Steal Sheet 1: Compound Claim Construction
**What to steal:** BMY builds content claims that address benefit AND fear simultaneously ("reduces stroke AND major bleeding"). This compound structure preempts the competitor counterargument before it can be made. Any brand with dual superiority data should architect content around compound claims — they are harder to rebut and more memorable.

### Steal Sheet 2: Trial Library as Content Moat
**What to steal:** The CheckMate trial library — 50+ trials bearing the same name — creates a recognizable content brand within the clinical community. "CheckMate" has become synonymous with Opdivo evidence in oncology. Brands with large trial programs should brand their trial families and build a consistent editorial identity around them.

### Steal Sheet 3: Survivorship Content as Brand Equity
**What to steal:** BMY's "patients who prevailed" content series turns clinical outcomes into brand equity. Long-term survivors are the living proof of a drug's promise. Brands in serious disease categories should invest in longitudinal patient story content — filming the same patient at diagnosis, at one year, at five years. The arc IS the brand story.
