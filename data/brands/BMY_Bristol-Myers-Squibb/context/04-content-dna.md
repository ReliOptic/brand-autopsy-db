It does not constitute investment, legal, or strategic advice. All interpretations are labeled as such. Estimates are explicitly marked `(estimated)`.

## Content Mission

Bristol-Myers Squibb's content mission, as expressed on the company's website and in its corporate communications, is: "To share science and stories that demonstrate our commitment to transforming patients' lives through innovative medicines." (official, bms.com) This dual mandate — science and stories — defines the content DNA: every piece of content is either advancing clinical understanding (science) or humanizing the brand's impact (stories). Content that does neither is structurally absent from BMS's communication.

## Hook Patterns (Minimum 4)

**Hook 1 — The Survival Milestone**
Open with a clinical outcome that reframes what is possible for patients:
> "In a Phase III trial, Opdivo + Yervoy demonstrated a 5-year overall survival rate of 24% in previously untreated metastatic NSCLC patients — representing a meaningful advance over historical benchmarks." (official, BMS clinical data communications)

Structure: State the endpoint → State the result → Contextualize against prior standard of care. Used in HCP-directed communications, press releases, and investor presentations.

**Hook 2 — The Patient Voice**
Open with a first-person statement from a patient that reorients the scientific discussion to human impact:
> "Because of Opdivo, I'm still here to see my daughter graduate." (official, BMS.com patient story format)

Structure: Patient statement → Disease context → Treatment narrative → Outcome. Used on bms.com patient stories, social media, and the BMS Annual Report.

**Hook 3 — The "Because Patients" Purpose Frame**
Open by anchoring a business or scientific decision in patient need:
> "Why do we invest in CAR-T manufacturing infrastructure? Because patients with relapsed multiple myeloma have limited options — and we believe they deserve more." (T4_INFERRED from BMS corporate communications style)

Structure: Rhetorical question about business decision → Patient need → BMS response. Used in corporate communications, earnings call framing, and internal culture content.

**Hook 4 — The Science Milestone**
Open with a discovery or approval that creates a historical reference point:
> "In 2014, Opdivo became the first PD-1 inhibitor approved by the FDA — beginning a new era in cancer immunotherapy." (official, FDA approval records, BMS communications)

Structure: Date → First-mover status → Category framing. Used in heritage communications, investor presentations, and brand credibility establishment.

**Hook 5 — The Pipeline Forward Look**
Open by orienting audiences toward the future therapeutic opportunity:
> "Our pipeline includes more than 50 clinical programs in oncology, hematology, and cardiovascular disease — each one representing a patient population with significant unmet need." (official, BMS 2023 Annual Report)

Structure: Pipeline scale → Therapeutic scope → Patient need. Used in investor communications, annual reports, and R&D narrative content.

## CTA Patterns (Minimum 3)

**CTA 1 — HCP Information Request**
> "Talk to your BMS Medical Science Liaison for a full data package."
Used in HCP-facing digital and print materials. Drives relationship with the field medical team rather than direct conversion. (observed on BMS professional portal)

**CTA 2 — Patient Assistance Enrollment**
> "You may qualify for the BMS Patient Assistance Foundation. Talk to your doctor or pharmacist, or visit bms.com/patients."
Used in DTC advertising required fair balance sections and on bms.com. (official, FDA-regulated DTC advertising format)

**CTA 3 — Pipeline Subscribe / Investor**
> "Sign up for BMS news alerts to stay current on clinical milestones, regulatory decisions, and pipeline updates."
Used on IR pages and press release subscriptions. (observed on bms.com investor relations)

**CTA 4 — Disease Awareness Engagement**
> "Learn more about [cancer type] and how to talk to your doctor about your treatment options."
Used in patient education campaigns; drives information seeking rather than direct product engagement, consistent with fair balance requirements. (observed on BMS disease awareness microsites)

## Content Prohibitions (Minimum 5)

1. **No unqualified efficacy superlatives**: "The best cancer treatment" or "the most effective drug" are prohibited without specific, head-to-head trial data basis and proper FDA claim substantiation. (T4_INFERRED from FDA promotional guidelines)
2. **No off-label promotion**: BMS content must restrict efficacy claims to FDA-approved indications. Off-label use discussions are permitted only in specific, legally structured medical education contexts. (T4_INFERRED from FDA regulations)
3. **No comparative safety claims without trial data**: Stating that a BMS drug is "safer than" a competitor drug requires head-to-head safety trial data; casual comparative claims are prohibited. (T4_INFERRED from FDA promotional guidelines)
4. **No patient identification without consent**: Patient stories require documented written consent and cannot identify individuals who have not agreed to participation. (T4_INFERRED from standard healthcare privacy and consent protocols)
5. **No clinical data publication without proper peer review**: BMS does not make first public disclosure of clinical trial data in promotional materials before peer-reviewed publication or presentation at a major medical conference. (T4_INFERRED from industry standards for data integrity)
6. **No financial guidance in non-investor channels**: Revenue projections and financial guidance belong only in SEC-regulated investor communications; patient or HCP content does not include financial performance information. (T4_INFERRED from SEC Regulation FD requirements)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Build a dual-register content engine: science for authority, stories for humanity.**
BMS's content infrastructure systematically produces two distinct content types — clinical data communications that build HCP authority and patient narratives that build consumer trust. Neither type alone is sufficient; together they create a brand that is simultaneously credible and humane. Transferable structure: identify your equivalent "proof register" (data, case studies, specifications) and "human register" (customer stories, testimonials, community narratives), then build a content calendar that balances both rather than defaulting to one.

**2. Use disease awareness content to build audience before product messaging is appropriate.**
BMS's disease awareness campaigns (lung cancer, blood cancer, cardiovascular disease) reach patients and caregivers at the point of diagnosis — before treatment decisions are made. This content builds brand familiarity and trust without promotional content, so that when a physician mentions Opdivo or Eliquis, the patient already has a positive BMS association. Transferable structure: create content that serves your audience's core information needs in your product category, not just content that promotes your product — audience built on genuine utility converts more reliably than audience built on advertising.

**3. Anchor every pipeline story in patient need, not scientific novelty.**
BMS pipeline communications do not lead with the molecular mechanism or the scientific ingenuity of CAR-T or CELMoDs — they lead with the patient population that has no current options. The science is the solution to the human problem, not the headline. Transferable structure: when communicating about new products or features in development, always frame the innovation as the answer to a named, specific problem affecting real people — this structure creates buy-in from investors, employees, and customers simultaneously.
