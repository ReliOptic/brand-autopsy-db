## Audience Tiers

### Tier 1 — Prescribing Physicians (HCPs)

Medical oncologists, hematologists, and cardiologists are the primary prescription decision-makers. In the US, approximately 13,000 oncologists practice across academic and community settings (estimated, based on American Society of Clinical Oncology membership data). BMS's Opdivo has over 35 approved indications globally as of the 2023 Annual Report (official, BMS 2023 Annual Report), creating a broad prescriber footprint across multiple tumor types.

**Core needs**: Peer-reviewed clinical efficacy and safety data (overall survival, PFS, response rate); clear patient selection guidance for biomarker-driven treatment (PD-L1, TMB, MSI-H status); reimbursement support and prior authorization navigation; and continuing medical education on evolving combination therapy protocols.

**Pain points**: Prior authorization burden from commercial payers; managing immune-related adverse events (irAEs) unique to checkpoint inhibitors; rapidly evolving NCCN guidelines requiring continuous education; patient affordability barriers that delay treatment initiation.

**Triggers for BMS product selection**: Phase III data demonstrating overall survival benefit; NCCN Category 1 guideline recommendation; positive prior patient experience; BMS medical science liaison (MSL) relationship providing timely clinical data access.

---

### Tier 2 — Patients and Caregivers

Adults diagnosed with advanced cancer, multiple myeloma, atrial fibrillation, or heart failure. Age profile for most BMS indications skews 55–75 years (estimated, based on published cancer incidence demographics, National Cancer Institute SEER database). Caregivers — spouses and adult children — are frequent co-decision-makers for complex oncology regimens.

**Core needs**: Plain-language treatment information; insurance coverage navigation; out-of-pocket cost support; connection to peer support networks; clinical trial information for disease progression scenarios.

**Pain points**: Complexity of specialty drug insurance coverage; anxiety about side effect profiles; information asymmetry in short physician appointments; financial toxicity from high-cost cancer drugs.

**Triggers for BMS engagement**: Physician recommendation of Opdivo or Revlimid-based regimen; BMS patient assistance program eligibility screening; online research following diagnosis; patient advocacy organization referral.

---

### Tier 3 — Payers and Formulary Decision-Makers

Medical and pharmacy directors at commercial health plans, PBMs (Express Scripts, CVS Caremark), and government programs (Medicare, Medicaid). In Europe and Japan, equivalent functions are performed by national HTA bodies whose reimbursement decisions determine market access.

**Core needs**: Cost-effectiveness data (cost per quality-adjusted life year); comparative effectiveness versus competing therapies; utilization management frameworks; rebate and contract terms aligned with population outcomes.

**Pain points**: High net cost of checkpoint inhibitors and CAR-T therapies; difficulty predicting patient population uptake; outcomes uncertainty for newer indications in real-world settings.

## Persona 1 — The Academic Oncologist

**Profile**: Dr. Sarah M., MD, PhD — Director of Thoracic Oncology, NCI-Designated Cancer Center, age 48.

**Pain**: Managing PD-L1 testing complexity across large patient volumes; staying current on combination regimen data (Opdivo + Yervoy, Opdivo + chemotherapy) while running an active research program; ensuring NCCN guideline updates are reflected in her tumor board protocols.

**Gain**: Phase III data that clearly segments which patient population benefits from a given regimen, enabling confident patient selection and treatment recommendation with documented evidence basis.

**Trigger**: New ASCO plenary session data on Opdivo survival outcomes in first-line NSCLC drives immediate reassessment of her treatment algorithm. BMS MSL provides pre-conference data briefing and post-conference slide deck.

**BMS touchpoints**: MSL relationship; advisory board participation; data package and journal reprints; clinical trial enrollment support.

## Persona 2 — The Myeloma Patient

**Profile**: Robert T., 67 — Retired engineer, relapsed/refractory multiple myeloma, suburban Ohio. Medicare Part D with supplemental coverage.

**Pain**: Monthly specialty pharmacy costs for Revlimid-based therapy; confusion about whether his Medicare plan covers oral specialty drugs; anxiety about disease progression after two prior treatment lines.

**Gain**: Clear explanation of treatment benefit in accessible language; financial assistance that eliminates or significantly reduces out-of-pocket burden; confidence that his oncologist is following evidence-based guidelines.

**Trigger**: Insurance denial for Revlimid refill triggers immediate engagement with BMS patient support line.

**BMS touchpoints**: BMS Together on Access program; patient navigator; pharmacist-initiated outreach; BMS.com patient resources.

## Persona 3 — The PBM Medical Director

**Profile**: Jennifer L., PharmD — VP Clinical Programs, national PBM. Manages formulary for 40M+ covered lives.

**Pain**: Balancing clinical evidence requirements with cost containment mandates from health plan clients; managing outcomes uncertainty for checkpoint inhibitor regimens across heterogeneous real-world populations.

**Gain**: Outcomes-based contract structure tying BMS drug cost to clinical response rates, reducing financial risk and enabling evidence-based formulary placement.

**Trigger**: Health plan client renewal negotiation triggers formulary position review for all oncology biologics.

**BMS touchpoints**: BMS contracting team; real-world evidence data package; outcomes contract negotiation.

## Anti-Persona

BMS does not serve patients with early-stage diseases managed by primary care with generic drugs; consumers seeking OTC or preventive wellness products; or healthcare organizations in markets where BMS lacks regulatory approval or distribution infrastructure. The company's stated focus on "serious diseases" explicitly excludes lifestyle conditions, preventive medicine, and primary care markets. (official, BMS corporate strategy, 2023 Annual Report)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Design your audience map around the full influence chain, not just the end buyer.**
In biopharmaceuticals, the patient is the end user, the physician is the prescriber, and the payer controls access. BMS builds separate relationship structures, value propositions, and engagement models for each link in this chain. Transferable structure: for any regulated or high-consideration product, map every decision-maker involved in the purchase and build distinct value propositions for each rather than treating "the customer" as a single entity.

**2. Convert access barriers into brand relationship opportunities.**
The financial complexity of specialty drug coverage is a pain point BMS converts into a relationship moment through patient support infrastructure. Every prior authorization challenge becomes evidence of the "Because Patients" commitment. Transferable structure: identify the most common friction point in your customer journey and build a dedicated service program around resolving it — the service becomes the most credible demonstration of your brand promise.

**3. Invest in peer physician influence as the highest-leverage prescriber acquisition channel.**
A single ASCO plenary presentation by a respected oncologist presenting Opdivo survival data reaches thousands of prescribers in one session. BMS's investment in investigator-initiated studies and speaker programs generates physician advocacy that promotional programs cannot replicate. Transferable structure: identify the ten most credible voices in your target professional community and build genuine relationships through research support, advisory participation, and early data access — before they have any product to endorse.
