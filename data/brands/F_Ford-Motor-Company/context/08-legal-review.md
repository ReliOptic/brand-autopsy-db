## 3. Major Litigation Categories

> All descriptions below are factual summaries of publicly disclosed or documented legal
> matters. Source citations are provided for each item. Nothing in this section constitutes
> an assertion of wrongdoing by Ford Motor Company.

### 3.1 Vehicle Defect and Product Liability Litigation

Ford, in common with all major automotive OEMs, faces ongoing product liability litigation
related to vehicle defects, accidents, and injury claims. Ford's 10-K, Part I, Item 3
(Legal Proceedings) discloses material legal proceedings. (SEC 10-K, annual)

Notable publicly documented matters include:

**Takata Airbag Recall**: Ford was among the OEMs required to participate in the massive
Takata airbag inflator recall, one of the largest automotive recalls in U.S. history. Ford
conducted recalls under NHTSA oversight for affected vehicles. (T1_OFFICIAL, NHTSA recall
database; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, automotive and financial press coverage)

**Transmission-related litigation**: Ford has faced class action claims related to
transmissions in certain model years of vehicles including Ford Fiesta and Ford Focus
(PowerShift dual-clutch transmission). Class action proceedings and settlement discussions
have been documented in press coverage. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, automotive press; SEC 10-K
disclosures for material proceedings)

**3.0L Power Stroke diesel engine litigation**: Litigation related to the 3.0L Power Stroke
diesel engine in certain Ford F-150 and Ranger trucks has been reported in automotive press.
(T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE; verify current status in SEC 10-K Item 3 for active proceedings)

### 3.2 Emissions and Regulatory Proceedings

**CARB / EPA emissions**: Ford, like all major OEMs, operates under EPA and California Air
Resources Board (CARB) emissions regulations. Any enforcement proceedings related to emissions
are disclosed in the 10-K Risk Factors and, if material, in Item 3. (SEC 10-K)

**FTC advertising claims**: The Federal Trade Commission oversees automotive advertising claims.
Ford's capability advertising (towing, payload figures) is subject to FTC standards for
substantiation. This project notes that Ford is required to substantiate all performance claims
in advertising with documented evidence; the brand's advertising history indicates awareness
of this obligation. (T4_INFERRED; T1_OFFICIAL, FTC advertising substantiation framework)

### 3.3 Labor Litigation — UAW Relations

Ford's October 2023 UAW strike (Ford was the last of the Detroit Three to reach agreement
with the UAW in the 2023 contract negotiations) resulted in a new four-year contract. The
strike and subsequent contract terms were disclosed by Ford through official press releases
and reflected in the FY2023 10-K's risk factor and labor relations disclosures.
(T2_PRIMARY_RELIABLE, Ford Motor Company official strike settlement announcement, October 2023;
SEC 10-K FY2023)

The UAW contract includes material wage increases and other benefits that Ford's management
has stated will add approximately $900 per vehicle in labor costs. (T2_PRIMARY_RELIABLE,
Jim Farley and CFO John Lawler post-settlement statements)

**Project implication**: Characterizations of the UAW strike or labor negotiations in any
project content must be factual and sourced, citing Ford's and UAW's official communications.
Neither party's negotiating positions should be characterized in pejorative terms.

### 3.4 Environmental Litigation and Regulatory Obligations

Ford's manufacturing operations are subject to EPA and state environmental regulations.
Environmental liabilities — including remediation obligations at historical manufacturing
sites — are disclosed in the 10-K notes under Environmental Matters.
(SEC 10-K FY2023, Environmental notes)

Ford has disclosed commitments to carbon neutrality and has published sustainability reports
addressing manufacturing emissions. (official, Ford Sustainability Report; SEC 10-K)

### 5.1 Litigation Posture Assessment

**Assessment**: Ford Motor Company is a large-cap public company operating in a heavily
regulated, high-liability industry. Its litigation profile — product liability, class actions
related to specific vehicle defects, labor proceedings, and environmental obligations — is
consistent with the profile of a major automotive OEM and does not suggest unusual or
disproportionate legal exposure relative to industry peers. (T4_INFERRED from comparison with
publicly disclosed legal proceedings at GM, Stellantis, and Toyota)

**Implication for this project**: The primary risks for this project are:
(a) **Trademark misuse** — Do not use Ford marks in ways that imply commercial endorsement

    figures that exceed Ford's published and EPA-certified specifications

### 5.4 Writing Risk Guide — Ford Motor Company

This section provides company-specific writing guidance for all content in this project
referencing Ford Motor Company.

#### SAFE TO WRITE

- Financial data with specific 10-K citation (e.g., "Ford reported $176.2B in revenue in
  FY2023, per its annual report filed with the SEC")
- NHTSA recall summaries with recall number and NHTSA database citation
- UAW strike facts with citation to Ford official press releases and union official statements
- F-Series annual sales leadership claims with citation to Ford press releases and Wards data
- Ford Pro segment financial performance with SEC 10-K segment disclosure citation
- Product specifications (towing, payload, range) with citation to Ford's published specs and
  EPA/SAE certification where applicable
- Trademark portfolio descriptions with USPTO citation
- Earnings call quotations attributed to named executives (Jim Farley, John Lawler) with date
  and transcript citation

#### REQUIRES CAREFUL HEDGING

| Topic | Required Approach |
|-------|-------------------|
| UAW strike characterization | "Ford and UAW reached agreement on [date] providing for [specific terms disclosed officially]" — cite official announcement; do not characterize negotiating positions or attribute motivations |
| Recall causation | "NHTSA records show [recall number] covering [scope]" — do not assert causation beyond officially attributed findings |
| EV financial losses | "Ford's Model e segment reported [specific EBIT figure] in [FY], per the company's 10-K" — do not project future profitability; cite management guidance with appropriate hedge |
| Competitor comparisons | Specific metric-based only (towing ratings, sales volumes from Wards, revenue from SEC filings) — no qualitative superiority assertions |
| Customer satisfaction / quality | Cite specific J.D. Power study name, year, and segment — do not generalize ("Ford has quality issues") without specific cited data |
| Environmental compliance | Cite EPA/NHTSA enforcement records only; do not assert violations not officially documented |
| Strategic intent (e.g., EV scale-back) | "Ford announced [specific change] in [date] via [source]" — cite the specific announcement; do not infer motivations beyond what management has stated |

#### DO NOT WRITE

- Assertions that Ford "knew" about a defect prior to a recall without citing specific court
  findings or regulatory determinations to that effect
- Characterizations of Ford's advertising as misleading or false without citing specific
  FTC or court findings
- Claims about internal deliberations, executive decision-making processes, or board decisions
  not in public filings
- Predictions of recall scope, settlement amounts, or litigation outcomes in ongoing proceedings
- Consumer sentiment generalizations without cited survey data ("Ford customers are unhappy")
- Assertions that Ford's EV scaling adjustments represent a failure without hedged language
  ("Ford indicated it was adjusting timelines" vs. "Ford's EV program failed")
- Any characterization of Ford's business practices using words from the prohibited list:
  deceptive, fraudulent, manipulative, predatory, exploitative, intentionally, deliberately

## 9. Specific Prohibitions for Ford Content in This Project

The following are specific to Ford's profile and supplement the project-wide prohibitions:

1. **Do not use "Mustang Mach-E" in contexts that assert it is the same product as the
   ICE Mustang.** The naming is commercially established; the distinction between the two
   products must be maintained.
2. **Do not characterize Ford's EV investment adjustments as a stated abandonment of EVs.**
   Ford has not officially exited EVs. Adjustments to timelines and capital allocation are
   disclosed; interpret them as such.
3. **Do not assert that the Blue Oval logo is a specific HEX value without citing an official
   Ford brand guidelines source.** The `#003478` value used in this project is based on
   observed brand materials and is noted as T4_INFERRED; it has not been verified against
   Ford's official brand guidelines.
4. **Do not cite the F-Series annual sales rank without referencing the source year.** "47+
   consecutive years" is accurate as of 2024 communications but requires a source and date.
5. **Do not attribute specific statements to Jim Farley or John Lawler without citing the
   specific earnings call date, press release, or transcript source.**

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Build a recall communication protocol before you need it — and treat it as a brand asset.**
Ford's established NHTSA coordination process, Newsroom press release cadence, and owner
notification workflow reflect decades of managing high-stakes vehicle safety communications.
The brand's ability to communicate a recall clearly and promptly — acknowledging scope, providing
a remedy, and directing owners to action — is itself a trust signal. Transferable structure:
draft your crisis communication protocol for product defects, data breaches, or service outages
before an incident occurs. The protocol should specify: who speaks, what channel leads, what the
acknowledgment-remedy-commitment sequence looks like, and what the escalation threshold for SEC
or regulatory disclosure is.

**2. Use dual-class share structures transparently — and document the governance implications.**
Ford's Class B shares give the Ford family disproportionate voting control, as disclosed in the
DEF 14A. This structure is a legal and governance fact that proxy advisors and institutional
investors evaluate. The key lesson is that dual-class structures are defensible when disclosed
fully and when the controlling family's long-term orientation is demonstrable through the
company's investment decisions. Transferable structure: if your governance structure includes
disproportionate founder or family control, ensure that the disclosure is explicit, the
rationale is articulated, and the investment track record supports the thesis that
long-term stewardship benefits minority shareholders.

**3. Treat the "DO NOT WRITE" list as a living document updated by each new legal development.**
This project's writing prohibitions for Ford content are derived from Ford's specific litigation
profile, regulatory environment, and trademark posture. As Ford's legal proceedings evolve —
new recalls, new regulatory actions, new class action filings — the writing risk profile
changes. Transferable structure: maintain a living "content risk register" for each major brand
or client you write about. Update it when material legal events occur. A writing risk register
is cheap to maintain and expensive to ignore.
