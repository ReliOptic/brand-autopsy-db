## Competitive Overview

Ford Motor Company competes in four overlapping arenas:

1. **Full-size pickup trucks** — The most important single market Ford participates in.
   F-Series is the volume and profit anchor of Ford Blue. Competitors: General Motors (Chevrolet
   Silverado, GMC Sierra), Stellantis (Ram 1500/Heavy Duty). This is a three-brand oligopoly
   with no material foreign-brand participant at scale in the U.S. full-size segment.
2. **Commercial vans and work vehicles** — Ford Pro (Transit, E-Transit, Super Duty) competes
   with Stellantis (Ram ProMaster), Mercedes-Benz Vans (Sprinter), and GM (Chevy Express /
   GMC Savana). Ford Transit has been the top-selling U.S. commercial van by volume for
   multiple consecutive years. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Wards Intelligence annual data)
3. **Electric vehicles** — F-150 Lightning and Mustang Mach-E compete with Tesla (Model Y,
   Cybertruck), Rivian (R1T), Chevrolet (Silverado EV, Blazer EV), and a growing field of
   EV entrants from legacy OEMs and startups.
4. **SUVs and crossovers** — Explorer, Escape, Bronco, and Expedition compete with GM
   (Tahoe, Equinox, Traverse), Toyota (4Runner, RAV4, Highlander), and Stellantis (Jeep
   Wrangler, Grand Cherokee).

Ford's competitive position is strongest in full-size trucks and commercial vans — both
segments with superior economics in Ford's FY2024 reporting. Ford Pro's adjusted EBIT margin
significantly exceeds the company average, as disclosed in segment reporting. (SEC 10-K FY2024,
segment disclosures; T4_INFERRED for relative margin assessment)

## Competitor Profiles

### Competitor 1 — General Motors (GM / NYSE: GM)

| Metric | GM | Ford (F) | Source |
|--------|-----|----------|--------|
| FY2024 Total Revenue | ~$187B (estimated) | ~$185B (estimated) | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, public earnings releases |
| Full-size pickup brands | Chevrolet Silverado, GMC Sierra | F-Series | (official, company websites) |
| U.S. vehicle sales (FY2024) | ~2.7M units (estimated) | ~2.1M units (estimated) | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Wards Intelligence estimates |
| EV platform | Ultium (Silverado EV, Blazer EV, Hummer EV) | Ford Model e (Lightning, Mach-E, E-Transit) | (official, GM and Ford IR) |
| Commercial fleet segment | GM Envolve | Ford Pro | (official, company product pages) |
| Ford Credit equivalent | GM Financial | Ford Motor Credit Company | (SEC 10-K, both companies) |

**Head-to-head assessment**: GM competes directly with Ford on the two most important product
lines — full-size pickup and commercial fleet. The Silverado has trailed the F-Series in annual
U.S. sales volume for decades, a gap that persists despite sustained product investment by GM.
(T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Wards Intelligence annual sales data)

GM's Ultium EV platform is broader in vehicle application than Ford's Model e program, which
is more concentrated in the F-150 Lightning and Mach-E. GM's full-size SUV lineup (Tahoe,
Suburban, Yukon) represents a segment where Ford does not have a directly equivalent product,
as Ford's full-size SUV is the Expedition. (T4_INFERRED from product portfolio comparison)

**Ford advantages vs. GM**: F-Series heritage and dealer loyalty accumulated over 47+ years;
Ford Pro's software integration maturity (Ford Pro Intelligence telematics); F-150 Lightning's
truck-first positioning in EV trucks. (T4_INFERRED)

**GM advantages vs. Ford**: Broader Ultium EV platform scope; GMC Sierra's premium truck
positioning; Chevrolet's stronger small-to-midsize SUV range. (T4_INFERRED)

### Competitor 2 — Toyota Motor Corporation (TM / NYSE: TM)

| Metric | Toyota | Ford (F) | Source |
|--------|--------|----------|--------|
| Global FY2024 Revenue | ~$307B (estimated, USD equivalent) | ~$185B (estimated) | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Toyota annual report (JPY basis) |
| U.S. truck/SUV strength | Tacoma (#1 midsize truck), Tundra, 4Runner, RAV4 | F-Series (#1 full-size truck), Explorer, Bronco | (official, company websites; Wards data) |
| Quality perception (J.D. Power IQS 2024) | Consistently above average | Ford has improved materially 2022–2024 | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, J.D. Power 2024 Initial Quality Study |
| EV approach | bZ4X, hybrid-first strategy (RAV4 Hybrid, Camry Hybrid) | Model e (BEV-focused, Lightning / Mach-E) | (official, Toyota and Ford IR) |
| Full-size truck U.S. rank | Tundra: typically #4 or below | F-Series: #1 (47+ consecutive years) | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Wards annual sales estimates |

**Head-to-head assessment**: Toyota and Ford compete most directly in the midsize truck segment
(Tacoma vs. Ford Ranger) and crossover SUVs (RAV4 vs. Escape/Explorer). Toyota does not
materially compete with F-Series in the full-size truck category — Tundra's U.S. sales volume
is a small fraction of F-Series. Toyota's hybrid powertrain leadership (particularly in the
RAV4 Hybrid and Camry Hybrid) represents competitive pressure in crossover segments where
Ford's offering is primarily ICE or BEV with limited hybrid depth. (T4_INFERRED)

Toyota's hybrid-first EV strategy contrasts with Ford's parallel BEV/ICE approach. As of
FY2024, Toyota's U.S. hybrid lineup continued to take market share in segments where Ford
competes. Ford does not currently offer a full-size F-Series hybrid truck configuration with
the same market depth as Toyota's Tundra Hybrid. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, automotive industry
sales data, 2024)

### Competitor 3 — Stellantis N.V. (STLA / NYSE: STLA)

| Metric | Stellantis | Ford (F) | Source |
|--------|------------|----------|--------|
| FY2024 Revenue | Declining significantly (CEO Carlos Tavares departed December 2024) | ~$185B (estimated) | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Stellantis earnings press releases |
| Full-size truck brand | Ram 1500, Ram Heavy Duty | F-Series | (official, company websites) |
| Commercial vans | Ram ProMaster, Dodge | Ford Transit, E-Transit | (official) |
| Off-road SUV direct competitor | Jeep Wrangler, Gladiator | Bronco | (official) |
| Key FY2024 development | U.S. sales volume decline; significant dealer inventory surplus; leadership change | Relatively stable market share in trucks and commercial | T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Reuters, Bloomberg automotive coverage 2024 |

**Head-to-head assessment**: Ram's full-size truck lineup occupies the third position in the
U.S. full-size truck oligopoly behind F-Series and Silverado/Sierra. Ram has made gains in
consumer preference ratings through interior quality improvements and ride comfort positioning
in the Ram 1500, which has received strong reviews in the premium cab segment. (T3_SECONDARY_
RELIABLE, J.D. Power consumer satisfaction surveys, 2022–2024)

Jeep Wrangler is the most direct competitor to Bronco in the off-road-capable body-on-frame
SUV segment. Wrangler has a continuous production history that predates the Bronco relaunch
(2021) by decades. Bronco's enthusiast relaunch generated significant consumer interest,
with reports of multi-year waiting lists in the early 2021–2022 period. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE,
automotive press coverage)

Stellantis's FY2024 operational challenges — which included substantial inventory build-up,
U.S. market share losses, and leadership disruption — may create competitive opportunity for
Ford Pro in the commercial van segment, where Transit competes with the ProMaster. (T4_INFERRED)

| OTA software update frequency | Industry-leading | Developing capability | T4_INFERRED; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE |

**Head-to-head assessment**: Tesla and Ford compete directly for the electric truck buyer
evaluating F-150 Lightning vs. Cybertruck. Tesla's software integration maturity, Supercharger
network scale, and direct-to-consumer sales model represent structural advantages that Ford's
dealer-based model does not replicate. (T4_INFERRED)

Ford's Lightning competes on truck-buyer familiarity (existing F-150 customer base is the
most accessible conversion opportunity), Ford Pro Power Onboard (bidirectional power
generation for jobsite use), and dealership accessibility in non-major metropolitan markets
where Tesla service is less dense. (official, Ford Lightning product pages; T4_INFERRED for
competitive assessment)

Ford's Model e segment reported approximately $4.7B in EBIT losses in FY2023 (SEC 10-K
FY2023) and continued material losses in FY2024, which Ford management has acknowledged
publicly as the cost of EV transition investment. CEO Jim Farley has stated publicly that
Ford will scale Model e investment selectively based on profitability milestones. (T2_PRIMARY_
RELIABLE, Jim Farley earnings call statements, 2023–2024)

commercial fleet EV capability that competes with Ford's E-Transit. This is among the most

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Own the category's most defensible quantitative leadership claim.**
"America's Best-Selling Truck — 47 years running" is a verifiable, time-accumulated claim
that forecloses competitors from claiming category leadership in the most important segment.
This single data point appears in virtually every F-Series advertising execution. Transferable
structure: identify the single most defensible quantitative leadership claim in your category
and embed it in every acquisition touchpoint. Duration-based leadership claims accumulate
value over time and are the hardest for competitors to counter.

**2. Build service network density as a moat against pure-play challengers.**
Rivian and Tesla entered commercial fleet without a dealer/service network. Ford Pro's moat
in fleet is not product specs — it is service network density and TCO reliability. A fleet
manager cannot afford downtime while waiting for a service appointment in a non-major metro
market. Transferable structure: invest in service and support infrastructure as a competitive
barrier. Challengers can replicate product features faster than they can replicate a
national service network built over decades.

**3. Use segment-level P&L transparency to attract the capital your growth segment needs.**
Ford's disclosed segment structure (Ford Blue, Model e, Ford Pro) allows Ford Pro's strong
margins and Model e's investment phase to be evaluated independently by investors. This
transparency allows Ford Pro to attract capital allocation without being obscured by EV losses,
and allows Model e to be evaluated as an investment rather than a drag. Transferable
structure: when managing portfolio businesses with different growth profiles, segment-level
financial transparency enables stakeholders to value each business on its own merits.
