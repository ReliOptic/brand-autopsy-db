## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

Ford Motor Company serves three structurally distinct customer segments, each aligned with a
separate reporting segment disclosed in the FY2024 10-K: Ford Blue (ICE consumer vehicles),
Ford Model e (electric vehicles), and Ford Pro (commercial fleet). These segments carry
different purchase triggers, demographics, and brand relationships. (SEC 10-K FY2024, segment
disclosures)

| Dimension | Ford Blue (ICE Consumer) | Ford Model e (EV) | Ford Pro (Commercial) |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| **Core Buyer** | Individual consumers, families, truck enthusiasts | EV-curious consumers, tech-forward buyers | Small business owners, fleet managers, contractors, government agencies |
| **Primary Products** | F-150, Mustang, Bronco, Explorer, Escape | F-150 Lightning, Mustang Mach-E, E-Transit | F-Series Super Duty, E-Transit, Transit, fleet configurations |
| **Purchase trigger** | New model cycle, truck need, brand loyalty renewal | EV adoption milestone, fuel cost, range anxiety resolution | Fleet replacement cycle, tax incentives (Section 179), uptime requirements |
| **Geography** | Primarily North America; strong rural and suburban presence | Coastal metros, high-income suburban markets | National; construction, agriculture, utility, government sectors |
| **Decision timeline** | 30–90 days (consumer) | 60–120 days (includes range evaluation) | 6–24 months (fleet procurement cycle) |

Source: T4_INFERRED from Ford segment structure (SEC 10-K FY2024) and observed marketing
positioning on ford.com and fordpro.com.

## Core Personas

### Persona 1 — The Working Truck Owner (Ford Blue Core Buyer)

- **Age / Occupation**: 42, self-employed contractor, construction foreman, or ranch operator
- **Geography**: Suburban or rural; Sun Belt, Midwest, Mountain West
- **Annual household income**: $75,000–$130,000 (estimated)
- **Vehicle need**: A full-size pickup that can haul materials, tow equipment trailers, and
  survive heavy daily use without unexpected downtime
- **Core pain**: Unexpected vehicle downtime costs money — a broken truck means a missed job
  site. Reliability and serviceability are non-negotiable.
- **Ford selection rationale**: F-Series brand trust built over generations; dealer network
  density; availability of work-specific configurations (payload, tow packages); parts
  availability nationally. (T4_INFERRED from Ford Pro and Blue market positioning)
- **Brand relationship**: Deep loyalty, often generational. Many Ford truck buyers report
  "we've always bought Ford" as a decision factor — suggesting that brand identity is inherited
  within families and trade networks. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Churn risk**: Low for brand loyalty holders. Elevated if Ford quality perception suffers or
  if a competitor (GM's Silverado/Sierra, Ram) consistently outperforms on a capability metric
  the buyer measures (e.g., tow rating, payload).
- **Media touchpoints**: YouTube (truck review channels, towing tests), country music radio,
  sports media (NFL, NASCAR), in-dealership, word-of-mouth from peers in trade

---

### Persona 2 — The Fleet Manager / Ford Pro Buyer

- **Age / Occupation**: 48, fleet manager for a mid-size HVAC company, utility contractor, or
  government agency fleet administrator
- **Fleet size**: 15–500 vehicles (estimated range for Ford Pro's core commercial segment)
- **Core pain**: Total cost of ownership (TCO) and uptime. A van off the road means a missed
  service call. Fleet managers optimize for parts availability, service turnaround, and
  vehicles-as-a-service economics.
- **Ford Pro selection rationale**: Ford Pro's market share in commercial vehicles (Transit,
  Super Duty) is substantial. Fleet managers value the Ford Pro Intelligence telematics
  platform, charging management tools (for E-Transit fleets), and dedicated commercial
  service lanes at Ford dealerships. (official, Ford Pro product pages)
- **Brand relationship**: Contractual and analytical. Fleet decisions are made against TCO
  models, not brand sentiment. However, Ford's proven commercial vehicle track record reduces
  risk in the procurement decision.
- **Churn risk**: Moderate — driven by competitive fleet programs from GM (Envolve), Stellantis
  (Ram ProMaster), and increasingly from Mercedes-Benz Vans and Stellantis's commercial van
  lineup. Large fleet operators run RFP processes periodically.
- **Media touchpoints**: Fleet trade publications (Fleet Owner, Work Truck Magazine), Ford Pro
  direct sales team, industry conferences (NTEA Work Truck Show), LinkedIn

---

### Persona 3 — The EV Early Adopter / Ford Model e Buyer

- **Age / Occupation**: 38, dual-income household professional; may own a home with garage
  charging capability; values sustainability alongside performance
- **Geography**: Predominantly coastal metros and high-income suburban markets; California,
  Texas, Florida, Pacific Northwest
- **Core pain**: Wants EV practicality (lower fuel cost, home charging convenience) without
  compromising on the truck or SUV capability they already use. Range anxiety remains a
  consideration for buyers evaluating the F-150 Lightning.
- **Ford selection rationale**: F-150 Lightning's truck-first positioning (Pro Power Onboard
  generator capability, towing capacity, familiar F-150 body style) differentiates it from
  EV-native competitors. Buyers already loyal to F-Series trucks represent the most accessible
  Lightning conversion target. (T4_INFERRED from Ford's Lightning marketing strategy)
- **Brand relationship**: Evaluative and performance-conscious. This buyer will compare Lightning
  specifications against Tesla Cybertruck and Rivian R1T before purchase. Brand heritage is a
  positive signal but insufficient alone.
- **Churn risk**: High — this segment is the most competitive in Ford's portfolio. Tesla,
  Rivian, and upcoming GM electric trucks are all competing for the same buyer. Ford's
  FY2024 10-K discloses significant losses in the Model e segment (see Layer 07), indicating
  the economics of this segment remain challenged. (SEC 10-K FY2024)
- **Media touchpoints**: EV review sites (Electrek, InsideEVs), YouTube EV channels, social
  media, Tesla owner communities, automotive press

---

### Persona 4 — The Mustang / Bronco Enthusiast

- **Age / Occupation**: 35, white-collar professional or skilled tradesperson; vehicle purchase
  is partly aspirational and identity-driven
- **Core pain**: Wants a vehicle with authentic heritage and driving character — not a
  generic crossover. Values the cultural meaning of the Mustang nameplate (American muscle,
  1964 heritage) or the Bronco's off-road identity (relaunch 2021 after 25-year absence).
- **Ford selection rationale**: These nameplates are irreplaceable within Ford's portfolio.
  No competitor offers an equivalent Mustang (Dodge Challenger is exiting the nameplate;
  Camaro is discontinued). Bronco fills a niche that Jeep Wrangler occupies, creating a
  direct two-brand competition. (T4_INFERRED from competitive landscape analysis)
- **Brand relationship**: Aspirational and community-driven. Mustang and Bronco owners form
  active enthusiast communities (Mustang Club of America, Bronco Nation) that function as
  organic brand advocates. (official, Ford-affiliated community platforms)
- **Churn risk**: Low for committed enthusiasts. Higher risk if Ford dilutes heritage — for
  example, the naming of the Mustang Mach-E (an electric crossover) as a "Mustang" generated
  significant enthusiast backlash upon announcement. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, multiple
  automotive press reports, 2019–2020)

## Anti-Persona — Who Ford Is Not For

| Type | Characteristics | Why Ford Is Not the Match |
|------|----------------|--------------------------|
| **Ultra-luxury buyer** | Prioritizes prestige badge, bespoke interior, and premium brand signaling above all else | Ford's core brand is democratic and functional. Lincoln is Ford's luxury sub-brand. The Ford badge does not carry the premium signaling of BMW, Mercedes-Benz, or Lexus. |
| **Pure EV-native early adopter** | Wants a vehicle built from the ground up as an EV, with no ICE heritage, and values Tesla or Rivian's software-first approach | Ford Model e vehicles are adapted from existing platforms in several cases. EV purists who prioritize OTA update frequency and software integration may prefer Tesla's software maturity. (T4_INFERRED) |
| **Urban micro-car buyer** | Needs the smallest, most fuel-efficient city vehicle; parking and fuel cost are primary criteria | Ford has exited most of the small car segment in North America (discontinued Focus, Fiesta in North America). The brand's North American product mix is overwhelmingly trucks, SUVs, and commercial vans. (official, Ford product strategy disclosures) |

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Match voice to the buyer's occupational identity, not their demographic.**
Ford's most effective truck advertising does not speak to a demographic segment (males, 35–55,
income $80K+). It speaks to an occupational identity: the contractor, the rancher, the
tradesperson. "Built Ford Tough" resonates because it mirrors the customer's self-image as a
tough professional. Transferable structure: in B2B and pro-consumer marketing, identify the
occupational or professional identity your core buyer holds and write copy that validates
that identity — not demographics.

**2. Build community infrastructure around heritage products.**
Bronco Nation and Mustang Club of America are not just enthusiast forums — they are brand
retention assets. Community members who invest in peer relationships around a product have
materially higher switching costs than those who do not. Transferable structure: invest in
customer community infrastructure (forums, owner events, social groups) for high-identity
products. The community converts customers into advocates, and advocates into retention assets.

**3. Separate your fleet and consumer acquisition funnels completely.**
Ford's Ford Pro segment has a dedicated website (fordpro.com), sales team, and service
infrastructure separate from the consumer dealer experience. A fleet manager buying 50
Transit vans has completely different decision criteria from a consumer buying one Explorer.
Transferable structure: if your product serves both individual consumers and business buyers,
consider complete funnel separation — different landing pages, sales teams, pricing structures,
and support channels. A unified funnel optimized for one will underperform for the other.
