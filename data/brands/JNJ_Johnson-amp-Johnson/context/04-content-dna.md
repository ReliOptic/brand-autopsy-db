## Content Mission

Johnson & Johnson's content exists to serve three simultaneous masters: the patients and
caregivers who rely on JNJ products for their health, the physicians and healthcare professionals
who prescribe or use those products, and the institutional investors who require factual,
material-disclosure-compliant financial communications. The governing content principle is
Our Credo — the brand's constitutional document — which requires that patient welfare precede
commercial messaging. (T1_OFFICIAL, Our Credo, jnj.com)

In practice, this creates a content architecture in which scientific evidence and patient
outcomes anchor all substantive claims, with commercial messaging positioned as the consequence
of clinical excellence rather than its leading edge. JNJ does not use content to create urgency
through scarcity or emotional manipulation; it uses content to build institutional trust through
the accumulation of clinical evidence, patient stories, and transparent financial reporting.
(T4_INFERRED from observed content patterns on jnj.com and official JNJ channels)

## Hook Patterns

### Hook 1 — The Patient Who Changed Everything

Opens with a named patient's experience with a J&J product or disease state, establishing the
human consequence of the science before the science is presented.

**Structural template**:
> "When [patient first name] was diagnosed with [disease], [brief statement of their situation].
> Today, [outcome that reflects treatment progress]. [Transition to the science that made this
> possible]."

**Mechanism**: The patient story is not testimonial marketing — it is a Credo proof point. The
brand's first obligation is to patients; leading with a patient experience enacts that obligation
in content form. The reader encounters the human before the molecule.

**Applicable contexts**: Corporate social content (jnj.com, LinkedIn), annual report narrative
sections, disease awareness campaigns, clinical milestone announcements.

**Example pattern** (structural, not a specific quote): A Darzalex patient journey narrative
that opens with the diagnosis experience, moves through treatment initiation, and arrives at
response data — with the clinical data serving as evidence for the patient's outcome, not the
lead message. (T4_INFERRED from observed JNJ content patterns; observed on jnj.com)

---

### Hook 2 — The Data That Changed the Standard of Care

Opens with a specific clinical trial result or regulatory milestone that altered the treatment
landscape for a disease. The clinical fact leads; the brand connection follows.

**Structural template**:
> "[Trial name or regulatory event] demonstrated [specific outcome metric] in [patient
> population], [brief statement of what this means for clinical practice]."

**Mechanism**: In the prescriber audience, clinical data is the currency of credibility.
Opening with a trial result signals that J&J is communicating as a scientific partner, not a
sales organization. The data is the hook; the brand is the implied source.

**Applicable contexts**: Medical congress communication, phase 3 data press releases, PDUFA
date coverage, medical affairs publications, healthcare professional-directed digital content.

**Example pattern**: An announcement of a Darzalex Phase 3 trial reading out in front-line
multiple myeloma, leading with the primary endpoint achievement before mentioning the commercial
implication. (observed in JNJ press release structure patterns; T4_INFERRED for hook analysis)

---

### Hook 3 — The Credo Proof Point

Opens with the specific language of Our Credo and immediately demonstrates how a current action
or decision embodies it. This is J&J's most distinctive content mechanism — no other company
in healthcare has an equivalent document to anchor this type of content.

**Structural template**:
> "Our Credo states: '[direct Credo quotation].' [Specific action, decision, or outcome that
> demonstrates this commitment in the current context]."

**Mechanism**: The Credo quotation is not decoration — it is the thesis. The content then
provides evidence that the commitment is operational, not aspirational. This structure works
because the Credo was written before modern brand strategy; it reads as conviction rather
than marketing copy.

**Applicable contexts**: CEO communications, annual report narrative, crisis response content,
ESG reporting, Credo-focused LinkedIn content, internal employee communications adapted for
external audiences.

**High-risk application**: The Credo hook must not be applied to contexts where it would appear
contradicted by the company's simultaneous actions. Using "our first responsibility is to patients"
as a hook in a context where litigation proceedings are perceived to limit patient access would
amplify rather than reduce reputational tension. Content discipline requires matching the hook
to a context where the Credo commitment is visibly substantiated. (T4_INFERRED)

---

### Hook 4 — The MedTech Innovation Demonstration

Opens with a specific procedural challenge surgeons face, then demonstrates how a J&J MedTech
solution addresses it with quantifiable evidence.

**Structural template**:
> "[Specific surgical challenge: blood loss, visualization limitation, precision requirement,
> recovery time]. [How the technology addresses it with specific performance data or clinical
> outcome reference]."

**Mechanism**: Surgeons are an evidence-based, technically oriented audience who respond to
problem-solution framing with quantifiable outcomes. The hook earns attention by demonstrating
understanding of the surgical context before asserting product value.

**Applicable contexts**: Surgical technique videos, MedTech product launch content, field sales
enablement, medical congress booth presentations, clinical education programs.

---

### Hook 5 — The Financial Milestone Narrative

Opens with a specific, material financial fact from JNJ's SEC filings or earnings report,
then contextualizes it within the company's strategic trajectory.

**Structural template**:
> "Johnson & Johnson reported [specific metric] for [fiscal period], as disclosed in [filing
> or press release source]. [Context: what this indicates about the strategic direction]."

**Mechanism**: The IR audience demands facts before narrative. Grounding the hook in a specific,
verifiable financial metric signals that what follows is material-disclosure-compliant.
Financial hooks without source citation would undermine the credibility architecture the
entire IR content program depends on.

**Applicable contexts**: Earnings release communications, analyst day presentations, investor
relations website content, financial media briefings.

## Tone & Mood Guide

### Situation 1 — Clinical Milestone Announcement (FDA approval, Phase 3 data)

**Mood**: Measured scientific confidence. Clinical accomplishment is acknowledged with precision
and grounded immediately in patient benefit.

- **Vocabulary**: "approved", "demonstrated", "data showed", "patients in the trial", "the
  [trial name] study", "as presented at [congress]", "in clinical trials"
- **Pacing**: Regulatory/scientific fact → patient benefit → physician guidance note → data
  reference and disclaimer
- **Avoid**: Superlative characterizations ("revolutionary", "breakthrough") as bare assertions;
  efficacy claims outside the approved label; comparative superiority claims without metric basis

---

### Situation 2 — Corporate Purpose / Credo Communication

**Mood**: Conviction-based institutional authority. The Credo speaks with moral weight —
neither apologetic nor promotional.

- **Vocabulary**: "responsibility", "committed", "believe", "first obligation", "patients and
  caregivers", "communities", "long-term"
- **Pacing**: Credo principle stated → operational evidence provided → connection to current
  strategic action or investment
- **Avoid**: Marketing-style enthusiasm ("exciting", "thrilling") that undermines the Credo's
  solemn register; Credo citation without operational evidence

---

### Situation 3 — Crisis Response or Litigation Context

**Mood**: Measured accountability. The brand acknowledges facts, scopes the situation precisely,
and commits to process — without speculating on outcomes.

- **Vocabulary**: "we are aware of", "proceedings are ongoing", "we are committed to", "we
  believe", "as disclosed in our [10-K/8-K]", "we disagree with", "we will defend"
- **Pacing**: Factual acknowledgment → scope (what we know, what we don't) → process commitment
  → reference to official disclosures
- **Avoid**: Outcome predictions; minimization of claims; characterizing claimants; stating
  conclusions about proceedings that have not reached final judgment

*For talc/asbestos litigation specifically, see Layer 08 Writing Risk Guide for required
phrasings and prohibited expressions.*

---

### Situation 4 — MedTech Surgical Innovation Content

**Mood**: Clinical partnership. J&J is a collaborator in surgical excellence, not a vendor
pitching a product.

- **Vocabulary**: "precision", "outcomes data", "clinical evidence", "surgeon feedback",
  "designed with surgeons", "validated in [procedure type]", "registry data indicates"
- **Pacing**: Surgical challenge identified → technology capability described → clinical
  evidence referenced → surgeon education resource offered
- **Avoid**: Consumer-style claims; unqualified superiority assertions; device performance claims
  without clinical data basis

---

### Situation 5 — Financial & Investor Communications

**Mood**: Formal factual precision. Zero promotional language. Maximum attribution density.

- **Vocabulary**: "as disclosed in", "per our 10-K", "adjusted operational sales",
  "excluding the impact of", "the segment reported", "management believes" (with safe harbor)
- **Pacing**: Metric stated → context provided → forward-looking element with safe harbor →
  reference to official filing
- **Avoid**: Marketing language in any financial communication; forward-looking statements
  without proper SEC safe harbor language

## Content Prohibitions

1. **No unqualified efficacy claims outside FDA-approved labeling.** Statements about a drug's
   ability to treat, cure, or prevent a condition must align with approved prescribing
   information and applicable promotional regulations. (T4_INFERRED from FDA promotional
   guidance frameworks)

2. **No off-label promotion in HCP-directed content.** Communications directed at healthcare
   professionals must remain within the bounds of approved indication labeling, consistent with
   FDA promotional standards. (T4_INFERRED from applicable regulatory framework)

3. **No named competitor disparagement.** Competitive positioning uses specific, cited, metric-
   based comparisons. Characterizations such as "better than" without quantitative metric basis
   are not consistent with the Credo's institutional dignity standard. (T4_INFERRED)

4. **No guarantee of patient outcomes.** Patient story content and disease education materials
   must include appropriate disclaimers that individual results may vary and content is for
   educational purposes. (T4_INFERRED from applicable regulations and legal risk management)

5. **No forward-looking financial projections in non-SEC-filed content.** Guidance is
   communicated through earnings releases, SEC filings, and attributed management statements
   with safe harbor language — not in marketing content. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC requirements)

6. **No Credo citation in content that is contradicted by visible corporate action.** The Credo
   hook is the brand's most powerful content mechanism and its most fragile. Using "patients
   first" language in content while simultaneously pursuing strategies perceived to limit patient
   access amplifies brand tension. Credo content requires operational proof, not assertion alone.
   (T4_INFERRED)

7. **No talc/asbestos litigation characterizations that go beyond disclosed facts.** All content
   related to the talc litigation must be limited to factual summaries of publicly disclosed
   proceedings with hedged language and proper source citations. See Layer 08 for specific
   guidance. (T4_INFERRED; legal risk assessment per project writing policy)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Anchor every commercial claim to a patient consequence.**
J&J's most effective content structure is: clinical evidence → patient benefit → commercial
implication. The patient benefit is not a marketing add-on; it is the primary claim. The
commercial outcome (prescriptions, revenue) is the consequence, not the thesis. Transferable
structure for B2B SaaS, healthcare IT, or any company with a downstream human beneficiary:
rewrite your homepage hero to name the person who benefits from what your customer buys.
"We help hospitals" is weaker than "We reduce the time patients wait for a diagnosis."

**2. Use your founding document as content, not just heritage.**
J&J publishes Our Credo in full on jnj.com, cites specific Credo language in CEO communications,
and references it in annual reports. The Credo is operational content, not a museum artifact.
The mechanism is simple: when the brand states a principle and then demonstrates it with a
specific action, the principle becomes credible evidence rather than marketing aspiration.
Transferable exercise: identify the single founding conviction of your company and write a
"proof portfolio" — three to five specific decisions your company made that cost money or
effort and were made because of that conviction. These become your most credible content assets.

**3. Let clinical trial names and regulatory decisions carry the authority load.**
JNJ's content rarely says "we think our drug is excellent." It says: "The CARTITUDE-1 trial
demonstrated [result]. Based on these data, the FDA approved [indication]." The regulatory
decision and the trial name carry the authority; J&J's role is to report and contextualize.
Transferable structure for any evidence-based industry (fintech, analytics, infrastructure):
replace "we believe our platform is best-in-class" with "in independent testing by [named
institution], our platform performed [specific metric]." Third-party validation is always a
stronger authority signal than first-party assertion.
