## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) — Portfolio Level

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Demographic** | PepsiCo's portfolio spans all age segments: Quaker targets 25–55 health-conscious adults; Pepsi-Cola targets 18–34 entertainment-oriented consumers; Gatorade targets 14–35 active/athletic consumers; Frito-Lay (Lay's, Doritos, Cheetos) targets 13–45 social snackers. No single demographic ICP covers the full portfolio. (T4_INFERRED from observed brand advertising and sponsorship targeting) |
| **Psychographic** | The unifying psychographic is **participation in culture** — whether that culture is sports, music, gaming, social gatherings, or morning routines. PepsiCo brands are positioned for social and experiential occasions, not solitary utility. (T4_INFERRED) |

(T4_INFERRED from industry channel data) |
| **Occasion-based segmentation** | PepsiCo segments its portfolio by consumption occasion: morning nutrition (Quaker, Tropicana), athletic performance and recovery (Gatorade), entertainment and social (Pepsi, Mountain Dew), snack occasions (Frito-Lay portfolio). This occasion framework drives the brand portfolio strategy more than demographic segmentation alone. (T4_INFERRED from observed brand positioning) |

## Persona 1 — The College Sports Fan (Pepsi + Doritos)

**Profile**: Tyler, 21, college student, Dallas, TX. Spends 8+ hours per week watching live sports. Buys snacks and beverages for watch parties 2–3 times per week.

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain** | Wants the social experience of watching live sports to feel curated and elevated. Boring snack spreads signal lack of effort. Budget-constrained but willing to spend on brand names that communicate cultural fluency. |
| **Gain** | The combination of Pepsi + Doritos at a watch party communicates cultural participation. The brands are recognizable as the "right" choices in the entertainment occasion context. |
| **Triggers** | Game day calendar events; Super Bowl; March Madness; NFL season opener; major concert announcements. |
| **Acquisition channel** | Social media advertising on Instagram, TikTok, YouTube; in-store end-cap displays; sports broadcast advertising. |
| **Brand relationship** | Occasion-specific loyalty — PepsiCo brands are his default choices when the occasion matches the brand's cultural positioning. No strong anti-preference toward Coca-Cola; simply Pepsi/Doritos are associated with the right occasions. |

## Persona 2 — The Competitive Athlete (Gatorade)

**Profile**: Aisha, 26, high school soccer coach and recreational runner, Chicago, IL. Trains 5 days per week; coaches 3 days per week; consumes 2–3 sports beverages per day on training days.

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain** | Hydration products on the market range from clinical/medicalized (off-putting) to purely sweet (not trustworthy for athletic use). Needs a product that signals it is engineered for performance without requiring scientific validation at the point of purchase. |
| **Gain** | Gatorade's 50-year presence on professional and collegiate sidelines provides implicit validation — if it is good enough for NFL players, it is appropriate for a dedicated recreational athlete. |
| **Triggers** | Pre-training routine; hot weather; intense workout; coaching sessions where hydration is modeled for athletes. |
| **Acquisition channel** | Sports retail (Dick's Sporting Goods, Academy Sports), convenience stores near training facilities, team bulk purchase through athletic supply accounts. |
| **Brand relationship** | High loyalty within the sports beverage category. Primary churn risk is from BODYARMOR (now Coca-Cola-owned) or Liquid I.V. marketing that offers a more premium or health-differentiated positioning. |

## Persona 3 — The Busy Parent (Quaker + Tropicana)

**Profile**: Sarah, 38, project manager, Portland, OR. Two children ages 8 and 11. Grocery shops weekly; prioritizes breakfast convenience and perceived nutritional adequacy.

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Pain** | Morning time pressure is extreme. Breakfast must be fast, relatively nutritious, and accepted by children without resistance. Quaker Oats and Tropicana orange juice are the path of least resistance — trusted, familiar, accepted. |
| **Gain** | A breakfast routine that runs without friction. Quaker's "goodness" positioning and Tropicana's "pure squeezed" claim (prior to packaging changes — see legal review) provide enough nutritional reassurance for daily purchase justification. |
| **Triggers** | Grocery store visit; back-to-school season; winter (oatmeal consumption spikes in cold months, estimated); Quaker promotional offers in weekly circular. |
| **Acquisition channel** | Grocery retail (Kroger, Safeway, Target); subscription delivery services (Amazon Subscribe & Save for shelf-stable items). |
| **Brand relationship** | Habitual repeat purchase. Susceptible to disruption from private-label competition (store-brand oats at 40% lower price) when family budget pressure is high. |

## AARRR Purchase Journey (Pepsi-Cola Sub-Brand Example)

| Stage | PepsiCo Mechanism |
|-------|------------------|
| **Acquisition** | Super Bowl and entertainment advertising drives brand salience; TikTok/Instagram content with music and sports talent drives youth exposure; in-store placement at eye-level and end-caps. |
| **Activation** | First purchase at a high-relevance occasion (game day, concert, party); sampling programs at events; price promotion that triggers trial. |
| **Retention** | Pepsi Points rewards (where active); My Pepsi account for registered consumers; recurring purchase via grocery habit formation. |
| **Referral** | Social sharing of campaign content (Pepsi's Super Bowl creative routinely generates organic social impressions); cultural conversations that the brand's sponsorship content anchors. |
| **Revenue** | Retail: $2–$5 per 2L or multi-pack; fountain/food service: approximately $0.05–$0.10 per ounce, significantly higher margin than retail. (estimated, based on publicly known FMCG pricing structures) |

## Anti-Persona

**Who PepsiCo brands are NOT optimally positioned for**:

- **The premium craft beverage buyer**: A consumer whose primary criterion is artisanal origin, small-batch production, or ultra-premium ingredient sourcing. PepsiCo's scale is antithetical to this positioning — and acquired premium brands (e.g., bubly, KeVita) have faced perception challenges in credibly serving this segment under PepsiCo ownership.
- **The strict health optimizer**: A consumer tracking macros, avoiding all added sugar, and seeking functional nutrition claims. Pepsi-Cola, Doritos, and Mountain Dew are not products designed for this audience. Gatorade Zero and Quaker serve segments of this consumer, but the core portfolio does not. Misaligned health claims in this direction create regulatory and trust risk.
- **The anti-corporate consumer**: A segment that actively avoids mega-corporation-owned brands on ethical grounds. PepsiCo's scale, while a business asset, is a brand liability for this consumer segment, particularly in the 18–30 demographic most attentive to corporate ownership of brands they perceived as independent.

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Occasion-based segmentation is more actionable than demographic segmentation for FMCG.**
PepsiCo manages its portfolio not by asking "who is our customer?" but "what moments are we serving?" The answer — morning nutrition, athletic performance, social celebration, afternoon snacking — defines which brands to deploy at which retail touchpoints, which sponsorships to pursue, and which channels to prioritize. Transferable: map your product portfolio to the specific moments of use your customers experience, rather than the demographic boxes they occupy.

**2. Youth orientation is an investment, not a demographic.**
Pepsi's consistent investment in music and sports cultural currency is a multi-decade bet that brands perceived as youth-relevant maintain cultural vitality across generations. Today's 22-year-old Pepsi drinker influenced by the Super Bowl Halftime sponsorship is tomorrow's 35-year-old who associates the brand with good times. Transferable: audience investments in the 18–25 cohort have a longer payoff horizon than immediate revenue data suggests.

**3. Distribution density is a brand moat.**
PepsiCo's ability to place its products in every gas station, school cafeteria, stadium, airline, and hotel minibar is not a logistics achievement alone — it is a brand presence achievement. Availability is itself a form of brand trust: if it is everywhere, it must be good enough to be everywhere. Transferable: for any product that benefits from habitual purchase, distribution density is a more durable competitive advantage than advertising share of voice.
