### 2.2 Copyright Portfolio

Disney's copyright portfolio is among the largest in global entertainment, encompassing:
- Over 100 years of animated and live-action film content
- Character designs, story treatments, musical compositions, and screenplay scripts
- Software and technology related to parks systems and streaming infrastructure

**Critically relevant context**: The copyright status of early Mickey Mouse works (specifically "Steamboat Willie," 1928) entered the public domain on January 1, 2024, after the copyright term expired. (T1_OFFICIAL, US Copyright Office records; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, widely reported) This applies to the specific 1928 cartoon, not to later Mickey Mouse character designs or the name and trademark, which remain protected. Disney has not publicly commented extensively on downstream commercial implications; this project notes this for accuracy.

### 2.3 Patent Portfolio

Disney holds patents primarily in:
- Theme park ride systems and safety technology
- Animatronic character design and control systems
- Streaming technology and content protection (DRM)
- Special effects technology
- Consumer electronics (Limited; Disney is not primarily a hardware company)

(T1_OFFICIAL, USPTO patent grant records, publicly searchable)

## 3. Major Litigation

### 3.1 Succession and IP Rights — Alan Dean Foster v. Disney/Lucasfilm

**Nature**: Author Alan Dean Foster publicly claimed Disney/Lucasfilm stopped paying him royalties on licensed works after acquiring Lucasfilm. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, reported by SFWA and trade press, 2020)
**Resolution**: Disney resolved the matter with Foster; specific terms were not publicly disclosed. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
**Project note**: This case illustrates creator rights issues that can arise from large-scale IP acquisition strategies.

### 3.2 Ongoing Creative Rights Litigation (Kirby Heirs v. Marvel)

**Background**: The heirs of Jack Kirby (co-creator of many Marvel characters) filed copyright termination notices under 17 U.S.C. § 203. Marvel/Disney opposed the terminations, arguing the works were "works for hire."
**Supreme Court**: The Supreme Court denied certiorari in 2014, effectively affirming lower court rulings in Marvel's favor. (T1_OFFICIAL, public court records)
**Current status**: Resolved in Marvel's favor; noted here for context on creator rights risks in Disney's IP model.

### 3.3 Florida Government Dispute — Reedy Creek / RCID (2022–2023)

**Background**: The Florida Legislature voted to dissolve the Reedy Creek Improvement District (RCID), Disney's special self-governing district, following public statements by Disney's then-CEO regarding Florida legislation. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, widely reported)
**Resolution**: Following subsequent negotiations, Florida and Disney reached a development agreement in 2023. The governance structure was modified. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, reported by Reuters, Bloomberg)
**Project note**: This dispute illustrates the intersection of corporate public communications and governmental regulatory relationships.

### 3.4 Streaming / Labor Disputes

Disney, through its entertainment subsidiaries, was subject to the 2023 WGA (Writers Guild of America) and SAG-AFTRA strikes, which affected content production across the industry. Disney is a member of the AMPTP (Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers). (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, widely reported) Specific Disney litigation in this context is not separately disclosed as a material legal proceeding in the FY2024 10-K.

## 5. Project Risk Assessment — Brand Autopsy DB

### 5.1 Disney's Litigation Posture

**Assessment**: HIGHLY ACTIVE on IP, MODERATE on defamation/analyst criticism (T4_INFERRED based on public record)

Disney has one of the most documented IP enforcement records of any global company:
- Regular copyright enforcement against unauthorized character use (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- Trademark opposition in USPTO proceedings (T1_OFFICIAL, TTAB)
- Historical monitoring of fan sites using Disney trademarks (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, press reports)

However, Disney has not demonstrated a pattern of pursuing defamation actions against financial analysts or brand researchers publishing publicly-sourced analysis. The risk to this project is primarily:
1. Trademark misuse (do not use Disney marks commercially)
2. False factual assertions without proper sourcing
3. Unauthorized reproduction of Disney's copyrighted creative content

**Risk assessment for this project**: LOW-MEDIUM for properly hedged, publicly-sourced analysis. (T4_INFERRED)

### 5.3 Writing Risk Guide — Disney

#### SAFE TO WRITE

- Revenue, operating income figures from Disney's SEC filings, cited as "(SEC 10-K FY20XX)"
- Descriptions of Disney's publicly announced content releases and park expansions
- Summaries of litigation citing specific courts, dates, and publicly reported outcomes
- Brand analysis and strategic interpretation clearly labeled as T4_INFERRED
- Descriptions of Disney's trademark portfolio citing USPTO records
- Franchise revenue analysis based on disclosed segment data

#### REQUIRES CAREFUL HEDGING

| Topic | Required Approach |
|-------|-----------------|
| Franchise quality assessment | "Public discourse has raised questions about..." not "MCU quality has declined" as bare assertion |
| Parks pricing | State observed prices with "(observed on disney.com, [date])" — do not characterize as "expensive" or "overpriced" |
| Political controversy (Florida/RCID) | Describe factual events citing news sources; do not characterize Disney's motivations |
| Creator rights and royalties | Cite specific reported cases; do not generalize to characterize Disney's IP practices |
| Labor relations | Cite NLRB filings or SAG-AFTRA/WGA public statements; do not characterize Disney's negotiating posture |

#### DO NOT WRITE

- Assertions that Disney engages in trademark "bullying" without specific regulatory or court findings using that language
- Characterizing Disney's IP enforcement as "aggressive" without citation to a specific proceeding
- Claims about Disney executive intent in political or business decisions
- Reproducing Disney character artwork, film stills, or marketing copy beyond fair use
- Asserting any pending legal proceeding has a predetermined outcome
- Claiming Disney's creative decisions are motivated by commercial greed without sourcing from Disney's own disclosures
