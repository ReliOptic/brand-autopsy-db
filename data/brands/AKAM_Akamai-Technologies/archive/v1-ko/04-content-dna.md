## Hook Patterns

### Hook 1 ‚Äî Threat Data Lead
Opens with a specific, quantified threat statistic that creates urgency.
- **Example**: "Akamai observed a 48% increase in web application attacks in Q3 2024." (T4_INFERRED, representative pattern based on SOTI report structure)
- **Mechanism**: Quantified threats capture CISO attention and establish Akamai's observational authority.

### Hook 2 ‚Äî Scale Assertion
Opens with the sheer scale of Akamai's global infrastructure.
- **Example**: "Every day, Akamai delivers over 30% of global web traffic across 4,200+ locations." (estimated, based on public Akamai statements)
- **Mechanism**: Scale is the proof of capability; infrastructure scope replaces feature comparison.

### Hook 3 ‚Äî Customer Impact Metric
Opens with a specific customer outcome ‚Äî latency reduction, attack blocked, cost saved.
- **Example**: "When [major retailer] moved to Akamai, page load times dropped 40% and conversion increased 15%." (T4_INFERRED, representative case study pattern)

### Hook 4 ‚Äî Research Question
Opens with a provocative question about internet infrastructure or security trends.
- **Example**: "What happens when your API is the attack surface?" (T4_INFERRED)

## Tone & Mood Guide

### Situation 1 ‚Äî Security Threat Advisory
**Mood**: Urgent but controlled. Authority without alarm.
- Vocabulary: "observed," "detected," "mitigated," "recommends"
- Tone: Security operations center professional ‚Äî calm competence under pressure

### Situation 2 ‚Äî Product Launch / Feature Announcement
**Mood**: Technical excitement. Innovation framed as customer capability expansion.
- Vocabulary: "introduces," "enables," "extends," "delivers"
- Tone: Engineering pride expressed through customer outcomes

### Situation 3 ‚Äî SOTI Report / Research Publication
**Mood**: Academic rigor with business relevance.
- Vocabulary: "data indicates," "our analysis suggests," "based on observed traffic patterns"
- Tone: Researcher sharing findings ‚Äî evidence-first, conclusion-hedged

### Situation 4 ‚Äî Customer Case Study
**Mood**: Problem-solution narrative with quantified outcomes.
- Vocabulary: Specific metrics ‚Äî latency in ms, attacks blocked per day, cost reduction %
- Tone: Let the numbers speak; minimal marketing embellishment

## Content Prohibitions

1. **No consumer-oriented language** ‚Äî Akamai's content targets enterprise technical decision-makers, not end users. (T4_INFERRED)
2. **No absolute security guarantees** ‚Äî "Completely secure" or "unhackable" are never appropriate. (T4_INFERRED)
3. **No direct competitor disparagement** ‚Äî Comparisons are metric-based, referencing Gartner/Forrester positioning or quantifiable benchmarks. (T4_INFERRED)
4. **No fear-mongering in threat content** ‚Äî Present data factually; do not sensationalize breaches or attacks. (T4_INFERRED)
5. **No unverified threat statistics** ‚Äî All threat data cited in content must be sourced from Akamai's own observation or credited third-party research. (T4_INFERRED)

## Steal Sheet ‚Äî 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Turn operational data into editorial content.**
Akamai's SOTI reports repurpose operational telemetry (traffic patterns, attack data) into published research. The marginal cost of this content is near zero ‚Äî the data already exists. Transferable: audit what data your product generates operationally and determine what subset is publishable and valuable to your audience.

**2. Gate research, not product information.**
Akamai gates SOTI reports (email required) but provides product documentation freely. This inverts the common pattern of gating product info and freely distributing thought leadership. The logic: research attracts senior decision-makers willing to share contact information; product documentation serves existing evaluation processes.

**3. Match content depth to buyer seniority.**
CISO-level content (SOTI reports, threat briefings) is strategic and high-level. Engineering-level content (API docs, implementation guides) is deep and technical. The same product is presented at different depths for different decision-makers.
