## Battle Cards

### Battle Card 1: Amgen Repatha vs. Regeneron Praluent (PCSK9 Inhibitor Class)

**Competitive context**: Both Repatha (evolocumab) and Praluent (alirocumab) are PCSK9 inhibitors approved for LDL-C reduction in patients with familial hypercholesterolemia or established cardiovascular disease who require additional lowering beyond statins.

| Dimension | Repatha (Amgen) | Praluent (Regeneron/Sanofi) |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------------|
| Approved indication | Adults with ASCVD or HeFH/HoFH; LDL-C reduction; CV outcomes reduction | Adults with ASCVD or HeFH; LDL-C reduction; CV outcomes reduction |
| CV outcomes data | FOURIER trial: 15% reduction in primary MACE endpoint (SEC 10-K FY2023; NEJM 2017) | ODYSSEY OUTCOMES trial: 15% reduction in major adverse CV events (NEJM 2018) |
| Dosing | 140mg every 2 weeks or 420mg monthly | 75mg or 150mg every 2 weeks |
| Prefilled pen option | Yes (Pushtronex monthly injector) | Yes |
| Amgen advantage | Monthly dosing option with Pushtronex single-use on-body injector; broader payer coverage share in US (estimated) | — |
| Regeneron advantage | Long-term safety data published; slight differentiation in dosing flexibility at lower dose tier | — |

**Brand implication**: Both products have comparable efficacy in clinical trials. Payer coverage and patient assistance program quality are the primary commercial differentiators at the point of prescribing. (T4_INFERRED)

### Battle Card 2: Otezla (Amgen) vs. Stelara/Tremfya (J&J) in Psoriasis

**Competitive context**: Otezla (apremilast), acquired from Celgene in 2019 for $13.4B, is an oral PDE4 inhibitor for plaque psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis. It competes against injectable biologics (IL-17, IL-23, TNF inhibitors) and other small molecules.

| Dimension | Otezla (Amgen) | Stelara/Tremfya (J&J) |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Mechanism | Oral PDE4 inhibitor | Injectable IL-12/23 (Stelara) or IL-23 (Tremfya) inhibitor |
| Administration | Oral pill — no injection, no REMS requirement | Subcutaneous injection, every 4-12 weeks |
| Safety monitoring | No laboratory monitoring required | Infection screening; TB test required |
| Key patient population | Mild-to-moderate psoriasis patients who prefer oral therapy or are injection-averse | Moderate-to-severe psoriasis patients; prior biologic experience |
| Amgen advantage | Only FDA-approved oral non-biologic for psoriatic arthritis with no blood monitoring; favorable tolerability profile | — |
| J&J advantage | Higher PASI response rates in head-to-head vs. conventional therapies; IL-23 class offers longer dosing intervals | — |

**Brand implication**: Otezla occupies a distinct niche as the oral option — a positioning that survives even as injectable biologics demonstrate higher PASI scores. Patient preference for oral administration is a durable market segment. (T4_INFERRED)

### Battle Card 3: Amgen Biosimilars vs. Pfizer Biosimilars (Oncology)

**Competitive context**: Amgen and Pfizer both operate large biosimilar portfolios competing with Roche's reference oncology biologics (Avastin/bevacizumab, Herceptin/trastuzumab). This is a manufacturer-versus-manufacturer competition on the same reference products.

| Dimension | Amgen (Mvasi, Kanjinti) | Pfizer (Zirabev, Trazimera) |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| Reference product for bevacizumab biosimilar | Mvasi (bevacizumab-awwb) — FDA approved 2017 | Zirabev (bevacizumab-bvzr) — FDA approved 2019 |
| Reference product for trastuzumab biosimilar | Kanjinti (trastuzumab-anns) — FDA approved 2019 | Trazimera (trastuzumab-qyyp) — FDA approved 2019 |
| Amgen advantage | First-to-market for bevacizumab biosimilar; Amgen manufacturing reputation from innovator heritage | — |
| Pfizer advantage | Broader global distribution network; combined portfolio with Hospira heritage | — |

**Brand implication**: In the biosimilar segment, Amgen's innovator brand heritage provides a trust signal that pure biosimilar manufacturers lack — a differentiation that may support formulary preference among academic medical centers. (T4_INFERRED)

## Threats

1. **Biosimilar erosion of Enbrel**: Enbrel (etanercept) faces biosimilar competition in Europe but has been protected in the US by a patent licensing arrangement with Pfizer/Wyeth that Amgen has extended. Any shift in this arrangement or additional US biosimilar approvals would directly reduce Enbrel revenue, which represented approximately $3.6B in net product sales in FY2023. (SEC 10-K FY2023)

2. **PCSK9 inhibitor class pricing compression**: Both Repatha and Praluent faced severe payer pushback on initial pricing. Amgen reduced Repatha's list price in 2018 to improve formulary access. Continued pressure from payers and potential entry of additional lipid-lowering agents (inclisiran, small interfering RNA) could further compress PCSK9 revenues. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, multiple financial press sources)

3. **Pipeline concentration risk**: Amgen's FY2023 10-K discloses that a significant portion of near-term revenue is concentrated in five to eight products. Loss of exclusivity events scheduled for Prolia/Xgeva (denosumab) patents in the late 2020s represent a structural revenue cliff. (SEC 10-K FY2023 risk factors)

## Opportunities

1. **MariTide (AMG 133) obesity/cardiometabolic pipeline**: Amgen's investigational GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist-antagonist is in Phase III development. The obesity therapeutics market is estimated by multiple analyst firms at over $100B annually by 2030 (estimated, multiple published analyst forecasts). First-mover advantages in this class are substantial. (SEC 10-K FY2023 pipeline section; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)

2. **Bispecific antibody and oncology pipeline expansion**: Amgen's blinatumomab (Blincyto), the first approved bispecific T-cell engager (BiTE), represents a platform that may support multiple pipeline candidates. BiTE technology is a proprietary modality where Amgen has published scientific leadership. (official, amgen.com/pipeline; peer-reviewed publications)

3. **Geographic expansion in emerging markets**: Amgen's international revenue represented approximately 24% of total net product sales in FY2023, indicating substantial room for expansion in markets where biologic access is growing. (SEC 10-K FY2023 geographic segment data)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Own a mechanism-of-action category before competitors do.**
Amgen's PCSK9 inhibitor leadership and BiTE platform ownership demonstrate the value of being first to scientifically validate a mechanism. Transferable principle: in technical B2B categories, the company that publishes, presents, and educates the market on a new mechanism defines the category standard — even if competitors follow with equivalent products.

**2. Use portfolio breadth to negotiate payer access across products.**
Amgen's ability to bundle innovator and biosimilar products in payer negotiations — offering savings on biosimilars in exchange for formulary access for innovator products — is a leverage strategy unavailable to single-product companies. Transferable principle for SaaS/platforms: bundle less sticky products with high-value core products to improve overall account retention.

**3. The oral vs. injectable axis is a durable segmentation in pharmaceutical markets.**
Otezla's survival against higher-efficacy injectables demonstrates that administration route is a genuine and persistent patient preference dimension. Transferable principle: in any category where the delivery mechanism creates friction (injection, complex installation, steep learning curve), a "good enough and simpler" product will hold a persistent market segment.
