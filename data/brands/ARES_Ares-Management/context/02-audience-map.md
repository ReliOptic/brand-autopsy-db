## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

Ares's LP (limited partner) base spans institutional capital allocators, insurance companies, wealth management platforms, and sovereign wealth funds. The customer is not the company borrowing from an Ares credit fund — it is the investor allocating capital to Ares-managed funds.

| Dimension | Institutional LP | Wealth/Retail LP |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Who they are** | Pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies, family offices | High-net-worth individuals (via registered vehicles like ARCC, ASIF), registered investment advisors, wealth management platforms |
| **AUM scale** | $1B+ institutional allocators; minimum fund commitments typically $10M–$50M+ (estimated) | Individual investors via BDC (ARCC, minimum investment per brokerage) or interval fund structures |
| **Geography** | Global; US, Europe, Middle East, Asia Pacific LP relationships (SEC 10-K FY2023) | Primarily US for wealth channel products |
| **Decision-making timeline** | 6–18 months from initial due diligence to fund commitment (estimated for institutional PE/credit funds) | Ongoing via publicly traded or interval fund structures |
| **Key evaluation criteria** | Track record (IRR, MOIC, DPI by vintage), team stability, investment process, fee structure, alignment of interest | Yield, dividend consistency, NAV stability, liquidity terms |

## Three Primary Personas

### Persona 1: Katherine Wu — The Pension Fund Allocator

**Role**: Director of Alternatives at a $40B public pension fund, age 51. Manages a 15% alternatives allocation (~$6B) across private equity, credit, real assets, and hedge funds. Reports to the CIO. (T4_INFERRED representative persona)

**Pain points**:
- Denominator effect: Public equity market drawdowns reduce portfolio total value, forcing the alternatives allocation percentage higher than target even without new commitments.
- Fee pressure from pension board: Must justify 1.5%/20% fee structures to trustees who compare against passive equity index returns in strong bull markets.
- J-curve management: Early-vintage fund cash drag creates short-term underperformance that is difficult to explain to non-finance-expert board members.

**Gain triggers**:
- Ares presents vintage-year IRR and DPI data showing consistent through-cycle performance with low loss rates in credit.
- Ares credit funds offer shorter J-curves than PE buyout funds — capital deployed quickly in direct lending generates current income.
- Ares's scale and relationships provide access to co-investment opportunities alongside fund commitments, reducing blended fee burden.

**Brand touchpoints**: Ares LP portal, annual investor meetings, one-on-one meetings with Ares investor relations team, Ares Insights publications, industry conferences (SuperReturn, iGlobal Forum).

### Persona 2: James Okafor — The Insurance Company CIO

**Role**: CIO of a mid-size life insurance company with $8B in investment portfolio assets, age 57. Manages asset-liability matching requirements; governed by state insurance regulation. (T4_INFERRED representative persona)

**Pain points**:
- NAIC capital charge requirements for alternative investments require careful asset classification to manage regulatory capital ratios.
- Duration matching: Insurance liabilities require predictable cash flows; private credit's floating-rate income is attractive in rising rate environments but creates complexity in falling rate scenarios.
- Concentration limits: State regulation caps the percentage of portfolio in any single alternative strategy or manager.

**Gain triggers**:
- Ares's insurance-dedicated strategies are structured to optimize NAIC capital treatment.
- Direct lending's floating-rate nature provided attractive spread expansion in the 2022–2023 rate cycle.
- Ares's insurance client relationships and dedicated insurance solutions team indicate understanding of insurance-specific constraints.

**Brand touchpoints**: Ares insurance solutions team, regulatory capital white papers from Ares Insights, industry conferences (ACLI, IFOA).

### Persona 3: Michael Torres — The High-Net-Worth Investor via RIA

**Role**: Retired entrepreneur, age 66, with $12M in investable assets. Works with a registered investment advisor who allocates a portion of his portfolio to alternative income strategies. Accessing Ares through ARCC (publicly traded BDC) and ASIF (interval fund). (T4_INFERRED representative persona)

**Pain points**:
- Traditional fixed income (treasuries, IG corporate bonds) provided low yield for a decade and then declined in value as rates rose in 2022.
- Market volatility in public equities creates anxiety about retirement income stability.
- Complexity of alternative investments: understanding NAV-based valuations, liquidity constraints, and credit risk requires advisor explanation.

**Gain triggers**:
- ARCC (Ares Capital Corporation) has maintained a consistent dividend yield and traded as a publicly listed BDC, providing liquidity alongside private credit exposure. (official, arcc.com; SEC filings for ARCC)
- Ares brand recognition as a large, established manager reduces perceived risk compared to smaller alternative credit managers.
- RIA recommends Ares based on institutional track record and product availability on major wealth management platforms.

**Brand touchpoints**: ARCC investor relations, ASIF product materials, RIA platform (Schwab, Fidelity, Pershing), Ares wealth management channel.

## Anti-Persona

**The Retail Day Trader**: An individual investor seeking liquid, short-term speculative opportunities. Ares's products — closed-end funds, BDCs, interval funds — are designed for long-term income-oriented investors who can tolerate illiquidity. Investors who prioritize capital gains over income and who need immediate liquidity are not the target audience for any Ares product, including ARCC. The ARCC share price is subject to NAV discount/premium dynamics that are inappropriate for short-term speculation. (T4_INFERRED from product structure analysis)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Insurance is a distinct LP segment requiring purpose-built communications.**
Insurance company allocators have regulatory capital constraints, duration requirements, and NAIC classification concerns that are entirely different from pension fund allocators. Ares's dedicated insurance solutions team and insurance-specific product structures reflect the recognition that insurance capital is not generically "institutional" — it is a distinct segment requiring distinct solutions. Transferable principle: within any B2B category with distinct regulatory subpopulations, the manager that builds segment-specific expertise and product structures will capture disproportionate share of that segment.

**2. The wealth channel requires product structure innovation, not just brand extension.**
Ares's expansion into the wealth channel (ARCC, ASIF, other registered vehicles) required creating product structures that comply with '40 Act regulations, offer more frequent liquidity than institutional funds, and fit within the operational constraints of wealth management platform distribution. The product architecture is the enabler of the audience expansion. Transferable principle: entering a new customer segment with an existing product structure is often the wrong approach; the product must be re-engineered for the new segment's specific requirements.

**3. The J-curve is an audience education problem, not just a financial one.**
Institutional LP allocators must explain negative early-period returns to investment committees and boards who lack private markets expertise. Ares's credit funds, with their current income from direct lending, offer a less severe J-curve than PE buyout funds — a structural advantage that is also an audience communication advantage. Transferable principle: product features that reduce the complexity of customer internal justification are brand differentiators in institutional sales contexts.
