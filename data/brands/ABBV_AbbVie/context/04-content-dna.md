## Hook Patterns

**Hook 1 — The Transformation Arc**
Structure: [Before state of suffering] → [Scientific breakthrough] → [Patient outcomes restored]
Example observed pattern: "Before [therapy], [patient name] couldn't [activity]. Today, [patient name] is [living their life]."
Usage: DTC television, disease awareness digital campaigns, corporate responsibility content (observed in AbbVie advertising)

**Hook 2 — The Pipeline Milestone**
Structure: [Regulatory milestone] + [patient population size] + [unmet need framing]
Example observed pattern: "FDA approved [product] for [indication], offering a new option for the estimated [X million] patients living with [condition]."
Usage: Press releases, investor materials, HCP communications (observed on abbvie.com/newsroom)

**Hook 3 — The Data Lead**
Structure: [Clinical endpoint] + [trial name] + [statistical significance]
Example pattern: "In the [TRIAL NAME] Phase 3 study, [X]% of patients achieved [endpoint] at [week] compared to [Y]% with placebo."
Usage: Medical congress presentations, HCP-directed promotional materials, journal supplements (T4_INFERRED from standard pharmaceutical promotional practice)

**Hook 4 — The Access Narrative**
Structure: [Financial barrier] + [AbbVie program] + [patient relief]
Example pattern: "Through myAbbVie Assist, eligible patients may receive [product] at no cost."
Usage: Patient-facing digital, physician office materials, social media (observed, myabbvieassist.com)

## Tone Guide by Situation

**Situation 1 — Announcing a new FDA approval**
Tone: Measured optimism. Acknowledge the patient population's unmet need. Lead with clinical data. Close with patient access message.
Prohibited in this context: Superlative claims ("best," "first-ever" unless factually true), celebration language that minimizes disease seriousness.

**Situation 2 — Responding to pricing criticism**
Tone: Factual and empathetic simultaneously. Lead with patient access programs. Provide R&D investment context. Do not characterize critics.
Prohibited in this context: Defensive dismissal, raw profit margin discussion, comparisons to other companies' pricing (T4_INFERRED from observed corporate communications)

**Situation 3 — Reporting an adverse event or safety update**
Tone: Clinical precision. FDA-compliant language. Lead with patient safety. Follow with updated prescribing guidance.
Prohibited in this context: Minimization language, liability hedging language that reads as evasive, product promotional content in proximity to safety disclosures (T4_INFERRED from FDA promotional guidance)

**Situation 4 — ESG / Corporate responsibility reporting**
Tone: Substantive and data-backed. Avoid aspirational claims without measurable targets. Acknowledge areas for improvement.
Prohibited in this context: Greenwashing language, vague commitments without metrics or timelines (T4_INFERRED from ESG communication best practices)

## Content Prohibitions

1. **Off-label promotion**: Promoting products for indications not approved by FDA or applicable regulatory authorities is prohibited. (T1_OFFICIAL, FDA regulatory framework 21 CFR Part 202)
2. **Anecdotal efficacy claims**: Patient testimonials presented as representative outcomes without appropriate disclosures violate fair balance requirements. (T4_INFERRED from FDA guidance)
3. **Comparative superiority without data**: Stating one AbbVie product is superior to a competitor's — without head-to-head clinical trial support — is prohibited in regulated promotional materials. (T4_INFERRED from PhRMA Code and FDA guidance)
4. **Disease stigmatization**: Content that portrays chronic disease patients in ways that could increase stigma is contrary to AbbVie's patient-centric positioning. (T4_INFERRED from brand values)
5. **Speculative pipeline promises**: Forward-looking statements about unapproved products must carry appropriate cautionary language per SEC regulations. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC forward-looking statement rules)
6. **Personal health advice**: Content cannot direct specific patients to initiate, continue, or discontinue therapy without physician involvement. (T4_INFERRED from FDA guidance)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Separate branded and unbranded content tracks by design.**
AbbVie maintains distinct content streams: branded product content (subject to FDA promotional regulations) and unbranded disease awareness content (exempt from product promotion rules). This allows broader distribution of disease education material that builds brand affinity without triggering regulatory restrictions. Transferable for regulated industries: maintaining compliant unbranded content that educates the audience expands reach while a smaller, tightly regulated branded stream handles direct product claims.

**2. Use the milestone press release as a multi-audience document.**
A single FDA approval press release simultaneously delivers: the news hook for media, the clinical data summary for HCPs, the patient access message for patient advocates, and the revenue impact framing for investors. Each paragraph is written for a different reader who may stop reading after their relevant section. Transferable for any B2B company: structure announcement content so that every stakeholder finds their needed information in the first three paragraphs, without requiring the full document.

**3. Build fair balance into the brand voice, not against it.**
Pharmaceutical fair balance requirements (equal prominence of risks alongside benefits) could be experienced as brand-limiting. AbbVie's approach frames risk disclosure as responsible stewardship: "Here's what you need to know to make an informed decision." This transforms a compliance requirement into a trust signal. Transferable for any regulated industry: transparency about product limitations, presented in the brand's voice, generates more trust than the absence of any limitation discussion.
