### 2.3 Trade Dress

Mastercard's primary trade dress asset is the interlocking circles logo — the precise geometry,
color relationship, and overlap construction of the red and yellow circles. This trade dress
has been continuously used since 1966 and has achieved extremely high levels of global consumer
recognition. The trade dress is protected through trademark registration in multiple jurisdictions.

The physical card design elements (specific placement of the circles mark, World/World Elite
designation placement, card surface texture specifications for premium tiers) may also qualify
for trade dress protection in conjunction with network card design standards. (T4_INFERRED from

## 3. Major Litigation and Regulatory History

> All descriptions below are factual summaries of publicly disclosed legal proceedings.
> This document does not characterize any party's conduct beyond what is stated in official
> filings, court orders, or regulatory agency press releases. Proceedings described as
> "ongoing" have not reached final judgment.

### 3.1 In re Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation (MDL 1720)

**Forum**: U.S. District Court, Eastern District of New York (MDL 1720)
**Plaintiffs**: Merchant class (various retailers and merchant associations)
**Nature of Action**: Long-running antitrust class action alleging that Mastercard and Visa,
together with major issuing banks, conspired to fix interchange fees and merchant discount
rates at supracompetitive levels in violation of federal antitrust law.

**Settlement History**: The litigation has had a complex settlement history:
- An initial settlement reached in 2012 was rejected by the Second Circuit in 2016.
- A revised cash settlement of approximately $6.24 billion (combined Visa and Mastercard share)
  was approved by the district court in December 2019 (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Reuters and
  court records; T1_OFFICIAL, Mastercard 10-K disclosures).
- A separate equitable relief settlement addressing rules-based claims has been the subject
  of continued litigation and appeals.
- In 2024, a proposed settlement with additional merchant class members of approximately
  $197 million (Mastercard's share, estimated) was under court review as part of continued
  proceedings. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, legal press; exact figures subject to court approval)

**Mastercard's Disclosure**: Mastercard has disclosed this litigation and related settlement
reserves in its SEC 10-K filings. The 10-K should be reviewed for current reserve amounts
and proceeding status. (T1_OFFICIAL, Mastercard 10-K FY2024, Part I Item 3)

**This project's characterization**: This litigation reflects a contested legal dispute about
network pricing practices. The existence of settlement does not constitute an admission of
liability by Mastercard. The settlements are publicly disclosed; the legal questions involved
ongoing network pricing continue to be debated in regulatory and legal contexts globally.

### 3.2 European Commission Interchange Decisions

**Regulator**: European Commission (Directorate-General for Competition)
**Legal Framework**: Treaty on the Functioning of the EU (Articles 101–102); Interchange
Fee Regulation (EU) 2015/751

**EC Decision History**:
- The European Commission issued a formal decision in 2007 finding that Mastercard's
  cross-border interchange fees set for EEA card transactions violated EU competition law.
  Mastercard appealed; the General Court upheld the decision in 2012; the European Court of
  Justice upheld it in 2014. (T1_OFFICIAL, EC press releases; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, legal press)
- Mastercard subsequently modified its cross-border interchange practices in the EEA.
- The EU Interchange Fee Regulation (IFR), effective December 2015, imposed caps of 0.2%
  (debit) and 0.3% (credit) on interchange fees across the EU for all card networks, codifying
  the pricing constraints in legislation. (T1_OFFICIAL, EU IFR text)

**Current Compliance Status**: Mastercard operates under the IFR framework in the EU.
Ongoing EU regulatory activity may address scheme fees (fees charged to issuers and acquirers
for network membership and services) separately from interchange. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE,
Payment Systems Regulator and EC monitoring)

### 3.3 UK Interchange Litigation

**Forum**: UK Competition Appeal Tribunal; UK Supreme Court; various commercial courts
**Nature**: UK merchants pursued claims for damages related to multilateral interchange fees
following the "Sainsbury's" ruling, in which the UK Supreme Court held that Mastercard's
historic interchange fees restricted competition. Merchant damages claims have proceeded
in UK courts. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, UK legal press and court records)

**Status**: Complex ongoing UK litigation involving multiple merchant claimants and multiple
proceedings. Mastercard has contested the quantum of damages and various legal theories.
The 10-K should be reviewed for current disclosed settlement reserves. (T1_OFFICIAL, 10-K FY2024)

### 3.4 Reserve Bank of India (RBI) Data Localization Enforcement

**Regulator**: Reserve Bank of India (RBI)
**Nature**: In July 2021, the RBI directed Mastercard to stop onboarding new domestic customers
in India due to non-compliance with the RBI's payment data localization requirements, which
mandate that all data related to payment systems operated in India be stored only in India.
(T1_OFFICIAL, RBI press release, July 14, 2021)

**Resolution**: Mastercard submitted a compliance report to the RBI. The RBI lifted the
restriction on new customer onboarding in June 2022 after confirming compliance. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE,
RBI press release and financial press, June 2022)

**Ongoing obligation**: Mastercard is required to maintain data localization compliance in
India on an ongoing basis. The precedent established is significant: India demonstrated
willingness to exercise market access control through data governance requirements, a pattern
that may be replicated in other jurisdictions. (T4_INFERRED from regulatory trend analysis)

### 3.5 Russia Operations Suspension (2022)

**Context**: Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Mastercard announced
the suspension of its network services in Russia effective March 10, 2022. (T1_OFFICIAL,
Mastercard press statement, March 5, 2022)

**Operational Impact**: Mastercard cards issued by Russian banks ceased to work outside
Russia. Cards issued by non-Russian banks ceased to work inside Russia. UnionPay and Russia's
domestic Mir network assumed primary domestic payment network roles in Russia.

**Disclosed Financial Impact**: Mastercard disclosed in its 2022 10-K that revenue from
operations in Russia was not material to consolidated results. The exit was managed without
significant ongoing financial exposure. (T1_OFFICIAL, Mastercard 10-K FY2022)

**Brand Implication**: The Russia exit is cited as a case study in how payment network
suspension can be executed as a geopolitical response mechanism. It also demonstrated that
a country with a developed domestic payment rail (Mir) and access to an alternative international
network (UnionPay) can absorb the exit of a Western card network without complete payment system
collapse. This precedent has implications for how governments and geopolitical actors view
payment network leverage.

### 3.6 Other Notable Proceedings

- **US DOJ debit routing investigation**: The DOJ and Federal Reserve have addressed debit
  card network exclusivity and routing rules under the Durbin Amendment to the Dodd-Frank Act,
  which requires that debit cards be enabled on at least two unaffiliated networks and that
  merchants have routing choice. Mastercard has operated under these requirements.
  (T1_OFFICIAL, Federal Reserve Board Regulation II; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- **State attorneys general and consumer protection actions**: Various state-level proceedings
  related to payment fees and consumer disclosures (disclosed in 10-K Item 3, T1_OFFICIAL).
- **Patent proceedings**: Standard patent litigation in the technology sector; disclosed in
  10-K Item 3 to the extent material. (T1_OFFICIAL)

### 4.4 Privacy and Data Regulation

Mastercard processes transaction data at extraordinary scale. Applicable privacy and data
protection frameworks include:

- **GDPR** (EU/EEA): Applies to EU resident transaction data. Mastercard operates as both a
  data controller (for its own customer data) and a data processor (for issuer and acquirer
  data). Lead supervisory authority: likely the Irish Data Protection Commission (DPC) given
  Mastercard's European corporate presence in Dublin (T4_INFERRED).
- **CCPA/CPRA** (California): Applies to California resident data. Mastercard's privacy
  practices must address California's consumer rights framework.
- **India DPDPA**: India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act creates ongoing compliance
  obligations supplementing the data localization requirements.
- **China PIPL**: Personal Information Protection Law requirements for data processed in China.
- **Sector-specific**: PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) is a contractual
  framework imposed by card networks including Mastercard on all participants in the payment
  ecosystem. Non-compliance with PCI DSS is a contractual breach, not merely a regulatory one.
  (T1_OFFICIAL, Mastercard network rules; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE for PCI DSS framework)

## 5. Project Risk Assessment — Brand Autopsy DB

> This section assesses the legal risk specifically for the Brand Autopsy DB project when

### 5.1 Mastercard's Litigation Posture

**Assessment**: MODERATE-HIGH (T4_INFERRED based on public record)

Basis for this assessment:
- Mastercard is an active participant in sophisticated, multi-jurisdictional antitrust
  litigation as a defendant, indicating an experienced and well-resourced legal team.
- The company has contested litigation vigorously in both US and EU courts over multiple decades.
- Mastercard's trademark portfolio is actively maintained; the interlocking circles logo
  and "Priceless" mark are valuable assets that would be defended against unauthorized use.
- However, Mastercard's litigation posture has been primarily antitrust/regulatory defense
  and IP protection — not defamation actions against financial analysts, journalists, or
  researchers writing based on public sources.
- No public record found of Mastercard pursuing defamation litigation against financial
  analysts, brand research publications, or journalistic outlets for properly sourced
  factual analysis. (T4_INFERRED from review of public legal records)

**Implication for this project**: Our primary legal risks are (a) trademark misuse —
reproducing Mastercard's logos, the circles mark, or the "Priceless" trademark in ways
that could suggest sponsorship or endorsement, (b) false factual assertions about
Mastercard's litigation conduct presented as established fact when proceedings are ongoing,
and (c) pejorative characterizations of Mastercard's business practices without regulatory
citation. The risk of Mastercard pursuing litigation against this project for properly hedged,
publicly-sourced brand analysis is assessed as LOW (T4_INFERRED), provided content discipline
is maintained.

### 5.3 Fair Use 4-Factor Analysis (17 U.S.C. § 107)

**Factor 1 — Purpose and Character of Use**
- Our use is analytical and educational; not commercial publishing of Mastercard's creative works.
- Content is transformative: we analyze, synthesize, and comment on publicly available information
  rather than reproducing Mastercard's original creative expression.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE

**Factor 2 — Nature of the Copyrighted Work**
- We work primarily with factual data (financial figures, regulatory records, network statistics)
  rather than Mastercard's creative works (advertising scripts, original photography).
- Factual works receive thinner copyright protection than creative works.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE

**Factor 3 — Amount and Substantiality**
- We cite and summarize; we do not reproduce Mastercard's marketing copy, advertising scripts,
  or "Priceless" campaign creative in full.
- We reproduce only brief illustrative excerpts as examples of copy structure.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE (provided citation-and-summary discipline is maintained)

**Factor 4 — Effect on the Market**
- Our analysis does not substitute for Mastercard's products, investor relations materials,
  or any commercial offering Mastercard provides.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE

**Overall Assessment**: The project's data collection and analysis activities, as currently
scoped, appear defensible under fair use doctrine (T4_INFERRED; this is not legal advice —
consult qualified IP counsel for formal opinion before any commercial distribution).

- Any assertion that Mastercard engaged in price-fixing, collusion, or anticompetitive

- Characterizing Mastercard's interchange practices as "exploitative," "predatory," or
  "harmful" as editorial assertions without specific regulatory citation to those exact terms
  from a final regulatory or judicial determination
- Claiming knowledge of Mastercard executives' internal deliberations, private strategy
  sessions, or personal motivations not disclosed in public filings or official statements
- Asserting that any ongoing legal proceeding has a predetermined outcome
- Reproducing substantial portions of the "Priceless" campaign creative (scripts, specific

- Claiming the interlocking circles logo "belongs to" or "is associated with" any entity

- Publishing or referencing non-public Mastercard information, internal network data, or
  leaked proprietary material
- Repeating unverified social media claims or anonymous forum posts as factual statements
  about Mastercard's practices
- Making claims about Mastercard employee conduct not based on official filings or final
  court judgments
- Asserting that Mastercard is currently violating any law in any jurisdiction based solely
  on a regulatory investigation that has not reached a final determination
