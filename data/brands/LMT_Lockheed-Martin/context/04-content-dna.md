## Hook Patterns

**Hook 1 — The Extreme Environment Open**
Lead with the operating conditions that make ordinary technology impossible, then introduce the system that performs anyway.
> "At 50,000 feet and Mach 1.6, the F-35's sensor fusion system processes data from six onboard sensors simultaneously — giving the pilot a complete picture of the battlespace no single sensor could provide." (T4_INFERRED structure; performance descriptions based on publicly available F-35 program materials)

**Hook 2 — The Historical Continuity Open**
Connect current program to a lineage of mission-defining innovations (SR-71, U-2, F-117) to invoke institutional authority.
> "The same Skunk Works team that built the SR-71 Blackbird — still the world's fastest air-breathing aircraft — is now developing the hypersonic systems that will define air power for the next generation." (observed content structure, LMT Skunk Works materials)

**Hook 3 — The Human Stakes Open**
Begin with the person whose life depends on the system working correctly.
> "When a pilot enters contested airspace, the difference between a mission accomplished and a pilot not returning home is measured in milliseconds. The systems Lockheed Martin engineers don't get second chances." (T4_INFERRED structure based on observed LMT narrative patterns)

**Hook 4 — The Scale Open**
Use quantified program scale to establish authority and gravity.
> "More than 900 F-35s are now operational across 17 nations. Each one is connected to the same continuously updated software baseline — making the fleet collectively smarter with every mission flown." (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, F-35 program public status reporting)

**Hook 5 — The Discovery Open**
Frame space and science programs as human exploration milestones.
> "When NASA's Orion spacecraft splashed down in the Pacific in December 2022, it completed the Artemis I mission — the first flight test of the system that will return humans to the Moon." (T1_OFFICIAL, NASA Artemis I mission records; LMT Orion production role is publicly documented)

## Content Prohibitions

1. **No casualty or combat outcome descriptions in marketing content** — Platforms are described by mission capability, not by kinetic effect on human targets. (T4_INFERRED, consistent with observed LMT communications)
2. **No classified program details** — Content about classified programs (e.g., Skunk Works advanced development) is limited to what has been officially declassified or publicly disclosed by LMT or DoD. (operational security requirement)
3. **No political endorsements or party affiliation** — Defense contractors are highly sensitive to perceived political alignment; content maintains strict non-partisan posture. (T4_INFERRED, standard defense sector practice)
4. **No speculation about adversary military capabilities** — Descriptions of threat environments use publicly available intelligence community assessments, not LMT-originated adversary capability claims. (T4_INFERRED)
5. **No comparative claims about competitor systems** — Competitive positioning in government procurement is handled through formal source selection processes; public comparative marketing creates legal and relationship risk. (T4_INFERRED)
6. **No consumer-register language** — Lockheed Martin does not produce consumer-facing content in a retail tone; colloquial or casual language undermines institutional credibility with the primary audience. (observed on lockheedmartin.com)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Content Principles

**1. Heritage content compounds authority.**
Lockheed Martin's Skunk Works historical content — U-2, SR-71, F-117 development stories — serves a present-day business function: it establishes that the organization has delivered on impossible technical challenges before, making current claims credible. Transferable: document and publish the hardest problems your organization has solved, in technical detail. Historical proof of capability is more credible than any forward-looking claim.

**2. Scale numbers do the emotional work.**
"900 F-35s operational across 17 nations" carries more emotional weight than any adjectival description because it is simultaneously specific, verifiable, and inherently impressive to anyone who understands the complexity involved. Transferable: find the scale number that represents your organization's cumulative output and put it in every hero section. Transactions processed, patients served, structures built — the number earns the claim.

**3. The discovery narrative is your civilian audience bridge.**
Space exploration content — Orion, Mars missions, satellite programs — allows Lockheed Martin to reach audiences who have no engagement with defense topics but are genuinely interested in human exploration. This content pillar is not peripheral; it is the brand's primary mechanism for reaching talent, political stakeholders, and media audiences outside the defense community. Transferable: identify the version of your work that the broadest possible audience finds inherently interesting, and build a content pillar around it, even if it is not your primary revenue product.
