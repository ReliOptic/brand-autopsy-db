## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Competitive Principles

**1. Sole-source status is the ultimate competitive moat.**
LMT's position as the only producer of the F-35 means that once the platform is fielded at scale, customer switching costs become effectively prohibitive for the program lifecycle. In B2B contexts, the analog is becoming the embedded standard: the platform that all adjacent systems integrate with first. Design for lock-in through interoperability standards, not through contractual exclusivity.

**2. Your supplier can also be your competitor — manage it explicitly.**
RTX's Pratt & Whitney division supplies the F135 engine that powers the F-35 while Raytheon competes with LMT in missile defense. This structural complexity is navigated through contract governance, not brand positioning. In enterprise contexts, when your key supplier is also your competitor in adjacent markets, the relationship requires explicit governance frameworks, not just commercial terms.

**3. Program reputation compounds over decades.**
A company with 50 years of successful program delivery in a sector has an asymmetric advantage over a new entrant because past performance is a formal evaluation criterion in government procurement. Brand reputation in defense is not built through campaigns; it is built through CPARS ratings, milestone delivery records, and GAO audit outcomes. The transferable principle: in regulated or compliance-intensive sectors, your operational track record is your brand.
