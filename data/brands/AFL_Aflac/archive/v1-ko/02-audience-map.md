## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

| Dimension | Profile |
|-----------|---------|
| **Core Demographics** | Ages 25–55, employed full-time, enrolled in employer-sponsored health insurance, household income $40K–$120K (estimated) |
| **Occupation segments** | Hourly and salaried workers at mid-to-large employers, public sector employees (education, government), small business employees |
| **Geographic focus** | United States (primary) and Japan (Aflac Japan is the largest insurance company in Japan by policies in force, official, SEC 10-K FY2024) |
| **Psychographic profile** | Risk-aware but not insurance-savvy; understands that unexpected illness creates financial stress; values simplicity and directness in financial products |
| **Purchase triggers** | Open enrollment season at workplace; life event (new child, new job, diagnosis of illness in family/social circle); workplace presentation by Aflac benefits coordinator |
| **Price sensitivity** | Moderate — premiums are deducted from payroll, making cost feel incremental; typical supplemental policy premiums range from $20–$60/month (estimated, based on publicly available rate examples) |

## Core Personas

### Persona 1 — Maria, Working Parent

- **Age / Occupation**: 34, administrative coordinator at a regional hospital, two children under 10
- **Insurance stack**: Employer-sponsored PPO health plan + Aflac accident and hospital indemnity policies
- **Monthly Aflac premium**: ~$45 via payroll deduction (estimated)
- **Core pain**: If her child is injured and she needs to take unpaid time off work, her health insurance covers the ER visit but not lost wages, transportation, or childcare for the other child. The financial gap between medical coverage and real-world costs is her primary vulnerability.
- **Aflac selection rationale**: Cash benefit paid directly to her — not the hospital — means she can cover rent and groceries during a week off work. The payroll deduction makes it feel affordable. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Brand relationship**: Knows the Aflac Duck; enrolled during workplace open enrollment after a benefits coordinator presentation. Does not think about Aflac until she needs to file a claim.
- **Churn risk**: Low — payroll deduction creates inertia; switching cost is minimal but so is the trigger to switch. Churn occurs primarily at job change.
- **Media touchpoints**: TV (Aflac Duck ads), workplace benefits materials, employer HR portal

---

### Persona 2 — David, Small Business Owner

- **Age / Occupation**: 48, owns a plumbing company with 15 employees
- **Insurance stack**: Group health plan for employees + Aflac voluntary supplemental benefits
- **Aflac relationship**: Offers Aflac as a voluntary benefit to employees; Aflac is zero-cost to him as employer
- **Core pain**: Attracting and retaining skilled tradespeople in a competitive labor market. Offering supplemental insurance demonstrates that he cares about employee wellbeing without increasing his benefits cost.
- **Aflac selection rationale**: Aflac's voluntary model means David can offer additional benefits with no direct employer cost. Aflac handles enrollment and payroll deduction administration. (official, aflac.com/business)
- **Brand relationship**: Views Aflac as a retention tool, not an insurance product. Values the simplicity of the Aflac enrollment process.
- **Churn risk**: Low — zero cost to employer; would only switch if a competitor offered a materially better enrollment experience or product set.
- **Media touchpoints**: Aflac business-to-business marketing, insurance broker recommendations, industry trade publications

---

### Persona 3 — Kevin, Young Professional

- **Age / Occupation**: 27, software developer at a mid-size tech company, single, no dependents
- **Insurance stack**: High-deductible health plan (HDHP) + HSA; no supplemental insurance
- **Monthly Aflac premium**: $0 — has not enrolled
- **Core pain**: Healthy and does not perceive immediate need for supplemental coverage. Views insurance as a cost center, not a safety net.
- **Why Aflac is relevant**: Kevin's HDHP has a $3,000 deductible. A ski accident or appendectomy would require $3,000 out-of-pocket before insurance pays — a cash gap Aflac accident insurance would cover. Kevin does not know this. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Brand relationship**: Recognizes the Aflac Duck from childhood TV but has never considered the product. Skipped the Aflac section during open enrollment.
- **Conversion trigger**: A peer's hospitalization experience or a targeted digital campaign explaining HDHP gaps.
- **Media touchpoints**: Digital ads, social media, employer benefits portal

## Purchase Journey — AARRR Framework

| Stage | Aflac's Mechanism | Key Indicators |
|-------|------------------|----------------|
| **Acquisition** | Workplace benefits enrollment → Aflac benefits coordinator presentations → TV advertising (Aflac Duck) → Digital marketing → Broker/agent recommendations | New policy count per enrollment season; brand awareness (estimated >90% in US, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE) |
| **Activation** | Enrollment completion → Payroll deduction setup → Policy document delivery → MyAflac account creation | Enrollment completion rate; payroll deduction activation rate (not publicly disclosed) |
| **Retention** | Automatic payroll deduction → Annual re-enrollment → Claims experience satisfaction → Persistent coverage across job changes (portability) | Policy persistency rate (disclosed at segment level in 10-K); Aflac U.S. persistency approximately 79% (SEC 10-K FY2024) |
| **Referral** | Claims experience stories → Workplace word-of-mouth → Employer recommendation to peers | NPS (not publicly disclosed); workplace referral rate (estimated) |
| **Revenue** | Premium income from supplemental policies × persistency × cross-sell (accident + cancer + hospital + critical illness) | Net earned premiums: U.S. segment $5.9B, Japan segment $10.3B, FY2024 (SEC 10-K FY2024) |

## Anti-Persona — Who Aflac Is Not For

| Type | Characteristics | Why Aflac Is Not the Match |
|------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Fully self-insured individual** | High net worth; $50K+ emergency fund; views insurance as unnecessary overhead | The financial gap Aflac covers is irrelevant when the individual can self-insure against short-term income loss. |
| **Primary insurance shopper** | Seeking comprehensive health, auto, or life insurance as primary coverage | Aflac is supplemental; it does not replace primary health insurance. Positioning confusion would damage brand clarity. |
| **Gig economy worker without employer** | Independent contractor; no employer-sponsored benefits platform | Aflac's primary distribution is workplace-based; individual direct purchase exists but is not the core channel. (T4_INFERRED) |
| **Retiree on Medicare** | Age 65+; Medicare + Medigap covers most health expenses | Aflac's supplemental products are designed for working-age populations with employer-sponsored primary insurance. Medicare supplemental is a different market. (T4_INFERRED) |

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Distribute through the employer, sell to the employee.**
Aflac's voluntary benefit model makes the employer a zero-cost distribution channel. The employer offers Aflac as a perk; Aflac handles enrollment and payroll deduction; the employee pays the premium. Transferable structure for B2B2C: design a product that costs the employer nothing but benefits the employee, then use the employer relationship as your distribution channel.

**2. Name the gap before you sell the solution.**
Aflac's marketing creates awareness of the financial gap between health insurance coverage and real-world costs. "Your health insurance pays the hospital. Who pays you?" (T4_INFERRED, representative of observed messaging pattern). Transferable exercise: identify the specific gap your product fills, then lead your marketing with the gap statement before introducing the product.

**3. Reduce purchase friction to payroll deduction.**
The payroll deduction model reduces monthly premium payments to a line item that the policyholder rarely evaluates after initial enrollment. This creates structural retention — cancellation requires active effort against automatic continuation. Transferable structure: convert any recurring payment into an automatic deduction embedded in an existing payment flow.
