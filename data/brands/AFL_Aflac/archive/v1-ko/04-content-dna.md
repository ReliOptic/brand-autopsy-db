## Hook Patterns

### Hook 1 — The Duck Interruption
The Aflac Duck physically enters a scene where someone is discussing financial stress, illness, or insurance confusion. The duck's persistent "Aflaaaac!" interrupts the conversation.

- **Example**: TV commercial where coworkers discuss unexpected medical bills; the duck appears on a desk, quacking the brand name until acknowledged. (official, Aflac TV campaign)
- **Mechanism**: Comic interruption breaks through insurance category attention deficit. The humor lowers defensive barriers.

### Hook 2 — The Gap Reveal
Opens with a relatable scenario (hospital visit, accident, diagnosis) and reveals the financial gap between what insurance covers and what the person actually owes.

- **Example**: "You thought your insurance covered everything. Then the bills started arriving." (T4_INFERRED, representative of observed messaging pattern)
- **Mechanism**: Problem awareness precedes solution presentation. The viewer recognizes their own vulnerability.

### Hook 3 — Cash in Hand
Shows a person receiving a direct cash payment from Aflac during a difficult moment. The visual emphasis is on the cash benefit, not the insurance policy.

- **Example**: Policyholder receives an Aflac check while recovering at home, uses it for groceries and rent. (observed, Aflac testimonial content)
- **Mechanism**: Concretizes the abstract insurance promise. Cash is tangible; "coverage" is not.

### Hook 4 — The Question Open
Poses a direct question that most viewers cannot answer correctly, revealing their insurance knowledge gap.

- **Example**: "If you were hospitalized for three days, could you cover $3,000 in out-of-pocket costs?" (T4_INFERRED, representative of observed messaging pattern)
- **Mechanism**: Self-assessment triggers engagement. Most viewers realize they cannot answer confidently.

## Tone & Mood Guide

### Situation 1 — TV/Digital Advertising (Duck Campaign)
**Mood**: Humorous, light, memorable — the duck carries the emotional tone.
- Vocabulary: Simple, conversational, brand name embedded in duck's call
- Pacing: Quick setup → comedic complication → duck interruption → brand close
- Music: Upbeat, light, comedic timing support

### Situation 2 — Workplace Enrollment Materials
**Mood**: Informative, reassuring, professional but not corporate-stiff.
- Vocabulary: "Supplemental," "cash benefits," "payroll deduction," "voluntary"
- Structure: Problem (the gap) → Solution (Aflac) → Action (talk to your benefits coordinator)
- Tone: Helpful advisor, not insurance salesperson

### Situation 3 — Claims and Policyholder Communication
**Mood**: Empathetic, supportive, action-oriented.
- Vocabulary: "We're here for you," "your benefit," "your claim"
- Structure: Acknowledge the situation → Explain the process → Provide the benefit
- Tone: Caregiver — warm, competent, not condescending

### Situation 4 — CSR and Childhood Cancer Content
**Mood**: Sincere, emotional, purpose-driven. The duck appears as "My Special Aflac Duck" — a robotic companion for pediatric cancer patients.
- Vocabulary: "Courage," "support," "comfort," "fight"
- Tone: Earnest without resorting to emotional exploitation; the CSR content does not pivot to product promotion

## Content Prohibitions

1. **No fear-based urgency**: "Before it's too late" or "You could lose everything" — Aflac motivates through awareness, not terror. (T4_INFERRED)
2. **No disparagement of primary health insurance**: Aflac is supplemental TO primary insurance, not a replacement or criticism of it. (T4_INFERRED)
3. **No medical claims or health advice**: Aflac is a financial product, not a medical one. Content does not advise on treatment. (T4_INFERRED)
4. **No guarantee language for claim outcomes**: "Guaranteed to pay" — all claims are subject to policy terms. (T4_INFERRED)
5. **No competitor naming in consumer content**: The duck does not quack at MetLife or Unum. (T4_INFERRED, observed pattern)
6. **No casualty or graphic medical imagery**: Scenarios depict financial stress, not physical injury. (T4_INFERRED)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Let the mascot do the heavy lifting in a low-engagement category.**
The Aflac Duck makes insurance advertising watchable. In a category where consumers actively avoid content, the duck creates content people seek out. Transferable structure: if your product category has inherently low consumer interest, invest in a character or narrative device that makes the content entertaining independent of the product message.

**2. Educate about the problem before selling the solution.**
Aflac's most effective content creates awareness of the financial gap before introducing Aflac as the answer. Transferable exercise: devote at least 50% of your top-of-funnel content to problem education rather than product promotion.

**3. Embed CTA in a human relationship, not a button.**
"Ask your employer about Aflac" routes the CTA through a trusted human relationship (employer/HR) rather than a direct purchase button. Transferable structure: identify a trusted intermediary in your customer's life and route your CTA through that relationship.
