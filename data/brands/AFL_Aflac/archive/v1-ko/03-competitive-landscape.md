## Battle Cards

### Battle Card 1 — Aflac vs. Unum Group

**Unum's positioning**: "Unum helps employers and employees navigate the complexities of leave, disability, and supplemental benefits." (observed on unum.com)

**Aflac's advantages**:
- Consumer brand awareness: Aflac's estimated brand awareness exceeds 90% in the U.S.; Unum's consumer awareness is materially lower (estimated, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- Direct-to-policyholder cash benefit messaging is simpler and more memorable than Unum's employer-oriented value proposition
- Aflac's Japan business provides geographic revenue diversification that Unum does not have (SEC 10-K FY2024)

**Aflac's vulnerabilities**:
- Unum has a stronger position in employer-paid (non-voluntary) disability and group life insurance
- Unum's broker relationships in mid-to-large employer segment may be deeper (T4_INFERRED)

---

### Battle Card 2 — Aflac vs. MetLife

**MetLife's positioning**: "MetLife provides insurance, annuities, employee benefits, and asset management to help customers navigate life's twists and turns." (observed on metlife.com)

**Aflac's advantages**:
- Supplemental insurance specialization vs. MetLife's broad portfolio means Aflac's claims processing and product design are optimized for the supplemental category
- The Aflac Duck provides consumer recall that MetLife's Snoopy mascot (discontinued in most contexts) no longer matches (T4_INFERRED)
- Aflac's workplace voluntary enrollment model is more deeply established than MetLife's voluntary benefits offering (T4_INFERRED)

**Aflac's vulnerabilities**:
- MetLife's bundled group benefits offering (medical + dental + life + disability) creates a one-stop-shop appeal for employers that Aflac's supplemental-only portfolio cannot match
- MetLife's global scale and balance sheet dwarf Aflac's (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)

---

### Battle Card 3 — Aflac Japan vs. Japan Post Insurance

**Japan Post Insurance positioning**: Leverages Japan Post Office network as physical distribution (24,000+ locations) with government-backed trust. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)

**Aflac Japan's advantages**:
- Aflac is the largest insurance company in Japan by individual policies in force, with over 24 million policies (official, Aflac annual report)
- Partnership with Japan Post Holdings for distribution provides access to Japan Post's network while maintaining independent brand identity (official, SEC 10-K FY2024)
- Cancer insurance leadership: Aflac pioneered cancer insurance in Japan and maintains dominant market share in this category (official, Aflac investor materials)

**Aflac Japan's vulnerabilities**:
- Japan's aging population and shrinking workforce reduce the addressable market over time (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, demographic data)
- Prolonged low interest rate environment in Japan has pressured investment returns (SEC 10-K FY2024, risk factors)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Own a category by creating it.**
Aflac created the cancer insurance category in Japan in 1974 and has maintained market leadership for five decades. When you define the category, you become the default option. Transferable structure: if no established category exists for your product, name it, define it, and market the category before marketing yourself within it.

**2. Make your mascot do your awareness work permanently.**
The Aflac Duck generates brand recall without ongoing product explanation. Once the duck is recognized, the brand name is recalled. Transferable insight: a mascot that encodes the brand name in its behavior (the duck literally says "Aflac") reduces the need for tagline memorization.

**3. Compete on distribution model, not just product.**
Aflac's competitive moat is not product uniqueness — competitors offer similar supplemental policies. The moat is distribution: 400,000+ payroll accounts in the U.S. (estimated) create structural retention. Transferable structure: build your competitive advantage in how you reach and retain customers, not only in what you sell.
