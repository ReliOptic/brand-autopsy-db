## Core Personas

### Persona 1 — Diane, 58, Chronic Disease Patient

- **Age / Occupation**: 58, retired school administrator, suburban Midwest
- **Insurance**: Aetna Medicare Advantage plan; Caremark manages her formulary
- **Prescription burden**: 6 monthly maintenance medications (metformin, lisinopril, atorvastatin, levothyroxine, aspirin, vitamin D supplement), filled at her local CVS Pharmacy
- **Annual CVS spend**: Approximately $840–$1,200 in co-pays after insurance (estimated, based on typical Medicare Part D out-of-pocket structures); plus OTC purchases approximately $400–$600 annually (estimated)
- **Core pain**: Managing six prescriptions across two physicians who do not share an electronic health record system requires Diane to manually track refill dates, insurance coverage changes, and drug interaction awareness. She has called her insurer and pharmacist separately on the same question. The duplication is both time-consuming and anxiety-producing.
- **CVS selection rationale**: ExtraCare auto-refill program removes manual tracking. Caremark-managed formulary means her insurance coverage is visible in real time at the pharmacy counter. A MinuteClinic three blocks from her home handled a UTI in 45 minutes without a physician appointment when her primary care had no availability for two weeks. The integration of these three functions under one brand has reduced her active management burden materially.
- **Brand relationship**: Low-enthusiasm but high-loyalty. Does not follow CVS on social media or engage with campaigns. Loyalty is structural — switching pharmacies would require re-establishing auto-fill routines, formulary preferences, and ExtraCare history. The switching cost is not emotional; it is operational.
- **Churn risk**: Low. Churn would be triggered by a major formulary change pricing her medications out of tier, or an Aetna plan departure from her Medicare Advantage market.
- **Media touchpoints**: AARP publications, local television news, direct mail (ExtraCare coupons), CVS.com prescription management portal

### Persona 2 — Marcus, 34, Urban Working Parent

- **Age / Occupation**: 34, software engineer, urban East Coast, two children ages 4 and 7
- **Insurance**: Employer-sponsored Aetna commercial plan; Caremark PBM
- **Core use pattern**: Pediatric prescriptions (antibiotics, ear infection treatments), annual flu shots for the family, and MinuteClinic for his children's urgent-but-non-emergency illnesses when their pediatrician cannot see them same-day
- **Annual CVS touchpoints**: Approximately 12–18 pharmacy visits, 3–5 MinuteClinic visits per year (estimated)
- **Core pain**: His children get sick on weekday evenings and weekends. The alternatives to MinuteClinic are the pediatrician (closed), the ER (expensive, 3-hour wait), or waiting it out. MinuteClinic's extended hours and weekend availability fill a specific gap in the pediatric care calendar that neither his primary care system nor urgent care alternatives address as conveniently.
- **CVS selection rationale**: The MinuteClinic visit produces a prescription that is filled at the adjacent CVS Pharmacy within the same building in under 20 minutes. The single-location integration eliminates a second trip. For Marcus, the value is measured in time: the entire care episode from symptom presentation to prescription-in-hand takes under an hour for conditions that would otherwise require two separate trips and two wait periods.
- **Brand relationship**: Transactional but appreciative. He has recommended MinuteClinic to colleagues experiencing the same pediatric access problem. He engages with the CVS app for prescription tracking but does not read corporate communications.
- **Churn risk**: Medium. He would switch to a competing urgent care chain if one opened closer to his home or if MinuteClinic wait times increased materially.
- **Media touchpoints**: App notifications (CVS app), Reddit (parenting communities), word of mouth

### Persona 3 — Carol, 47, Employer HR Benefits Manager

- **Age / Occupation**: 47, HR benefits director at a mid-size manufacturing firm (800 employees), manages the Caremark PBM contract and Aetna employer group plan
- **Core relationship**: B2B customer — CVS Health is her vendor, not her personal pharmacist
- **Spend under management**: Approximately $4.2 million annually in pharmaceutical benefits administered through Caremark; approximately $9.8 million in Aetna group premiums (estimated, based on typical mid-size employer healthcare cost benchmarks)
- **Core pain**: Specialty drug costs (biologics, oncology) are driving her per-employee pharmaceutical spend up approximately 8–12% annually. Her employees on specialty medications frequently have difficulty accessing these drugs through in-network channels. She is accountable to the CFO for drug spend and to employees for access quality — a tension that is difficult to resolve with a disintegrated vendor structure.
- **CVS selection rationale**: Caremark's specialty pharmacy, CVS Specialty, provides a single point of management for both the formulary (cost) and the dispensing (access) of high-cost specialty medications. The integration allows her to report to the CFO using a single data stream rather than reconciling three vendor invoices. Aetna's employer reporting tools allow her to track population health trends that inform future benefit design.
- **Brand relationship**: Contractual and analytical. She evaluates Caremark on rebate pass-through rates, specialty drug management outcomes, and member satisfaction scores. She does not have brand loyalty in the consumer sense; she has a vendor relationship governed by a contract.
- **Churn risk**: High at renewal cycle. Enterprise PBM contracts are re-bid every 3–5 years. OptumRx (UnitedHealth) and Express Scripts (Cigna/Evernorth) compete aggressively for accounts of this size.
- **Media touchpoints**: Benefits industry publications (SHRM, Employee Benefit News), Caremark account management team, Aetna employer portal

## Anti-Persona — Who CVS Health Is Not For

| Type | Characteristics | Why CVS Health Is Not the Match |
|------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| **Concierge medicine patient** | Pays $3,000–$10,000 annually for same-day physician access and personalized care navigation | CVS Health is mass-market; MinuteClinic operates at volume, not personalization. The concierge patient has already solved the access problem CVS is designed to address. |
| **Specialty hospital patient** | Requires quaternary care (oncology, transplant, cardiac surgery) from academic medical centers | CVS Health operates at the primary and preventive care layer. Specialty and inpatient care are outside its clinical scope. |
| **Compounding pharmacy customer** | Requires custom-formulated medications not available through standard retail dispensing | CVS Pharmacy's standardized dispensing model does not support high-volume custom compounding. |
| **PBM-agnostic small employer** | Companies with fewer than 50 employees for whom PBM administrative complexity exceeds its value | Caremark's enterprise PBM model creates overhead that is disproportionate for very small employer groups. |
| **Health privacy maximalist** | Individuals who object to integrated data sharing between insurer, pharmacist, and clinic records | The CVS Health integration thesis depends on data sharing across its three business segments. Patients with strong objections to integrated health record access are structurally misaligned with the brand's value proposition. |

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. The logistics of care is a deeper loyalty driver than the sentiment of care.**
CVS Health's highest-retention customers (the chronic disease patient managing multiple prescriptions) do not choose CVS because of emotional brand affinity. They stay because the operational machinery — auto-refill, formulary visibility, single-location prescription and care — reduces the daily administrative burden of managing their health. Transferable structure: in any service business with recurring operational complexity, build retention mechanics that reduce customer administrative labor. The stickiest product is the one that remembers things so the customer doesn't have to.

**2. The brand-defining sacrifice must align with the brand's stated identity.**
CVS's tobacco removal worked as a brand signal because it was internally consistent — a company claiming to be in the health business that sold cigarettes had a credibility gap, and removing tobacco closed it. The sacrifice had to be permanent and costly to be believable. Transferable structure: before making a brand sacrifice as a marketing event, audit whether the sacrifice actually resolves an internal contradiction. A sacrifice that solves an external perception problem without resolving an underlying business model tension will be read as performative.

**3. Own the integration layer in a fragmented market.**
The most defensible position in any fragmented industry is not to be the best at one function but to own the coordination between functions that no individual player currently manages well. CVS Health's Aetna acquisition was predicated on this logic: the intersection of insurance and pharmacy is where the most friction, waste, and cost accumulate. Transferable structure: map your industry's highest-friction handoffs and assess whether building or acquiring the coordination layer between them would create more value than improving any single vendor's individual performance.
