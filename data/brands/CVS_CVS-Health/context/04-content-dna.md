## Hook Patterns

### Hook 1 — The Access Problem Statement
**Structure**: Lead with the healthcare access gap, then position CVS as the structural solution.
**Example**: "1 in 4 Americans live more than a mile from the nearest primary care physician. More than 9,000 CVS Pharmacy locations are already in your neighborhood." (observed on CVS Health awareness campaigns, paraphrased)
**Why it works**: Converts the scale of CVS's physical footprint from a retail fact into a social utility argument.

### Hook 2 — The Cost Savings Frame
**Structure**: Lead with a quantified out-of-pocket saving available through ExtraCare, generic substitution, or CVS Health Savings Finder.
**Example**: "Switch to the generic. Save $47 this month." (observed on ExtraCare promotional communications)
**Why it works**: Health cost anxiety is the primary barrier to prescription adherence. Quantified savings remove the hesitation at the pharmacy counter.

### Hook 3 — The Time Recovery Argument
**Structure**: Lead with the time cost of the incumbent healthcare navigation process; present MinuteClinic or CVS Pharmacy as the faster alternative.
**Example**: "Your doctor's next available appointment is in 11 days. MinuteClinic can see you today." (observed on MinuteClinic marketing materials, paraphrased)
**Why it works**: Time is the scarcest resource for the working parent or employed adult. A care access promise measured in minutes rather than days converts convenience into a clinical argument.

### Hook 4 — The Chronic Condition Continuity Promise
**Structure**: Lead with the disruption risk of prescription gaps for patients managing chronic conditions; present ExtraCare auto-refill as the risk reduction mechanism.
**Example**: "Your blood pressure medication shouldn't run out. Auto-refill so it never does." (observed on CVS Pharmacy enrollment communications, paraphrased)
**Why it works**: For the 133 million Americans with at least one chronic condition (estimated, CDC data), a prescription lapse is not an inconvenience — it is a medical risk. The continuity promise is emotionally loaded for this audience.

### Hook 5 — The Prevention Value Calculation
**Structure**: Lead with the cost of treating a preventable condition versus the cost of the preventive action available at CVS.
**Example**: "A flu shot at CVS: $0 with most insurance. An ER visit for flu complications: averaging over $1,000. Make the easier call." (observed on CVS seasonal flu campaign messaging, paraphrased)
**Why it works**: Healthcare cost-aversion is strongest for emergency or unplanned expenses. The prevention value calculation converts a vaccine visit from an optional wellness action into a financial decision.

## Tone & Mood Guide

| Situation | Tone | Example Approach |
|-----------|------|-----------------|
| **Acute illness (MinuteClinic)** | Calm, reassuring, fast | Remove obstacles from the messaging: no appointment needed, seen today, prescription ready in 15 minutes. Every word reduces friction. |
| **Chronic disease management** | Supportive, practical, consistent | Long-term relationship framing: "We're with you every refill." Progress narrative over dramatic transformation claims. |
| **Insurance enrollment (AEP)** | Informative, clear, low-pressure | Medicare Advantage decision is high-anxiety for the 65+ audience. Avoid urgency manipulation; prioritize clarity on coverage terms and cost. |
| **Corporate / ESG** | Purposeful, substantive, evidence-backed | Health equity claims must be grounded in operational evidence (clinic locations in underserved ZIP codes, workforce health programs). Avoid vague purpose language without operational specificity. |

## Content Prohibitions

1. **Clinical claims without regulatory compliance**: CVS Health's pharmacists and MinuteClinic practitioners are licensed healthcare providers. Content attributing clinical outcomes to CVS (rather than to the provider) without appropriate regulatory review is not permissible. (T4_INFERRED from FDA/FTC regulated environment)
2. **Insurance benefit guarantees**: Plan benefits, formulary coverage, and co-pay amounts vary by plan year and employer contract. No content can guarantee specific coverage without plan-level qualification. (T4_INFERRED from insurance regulatory environment)
3. **Comparative efficacy claims between named drugs**: Asserting that Drug A is more effective than Drug B is a clinical claim requiring FDA-regulated substantiation. (T4_INFERRED from FDA promotional drug regulations)
4. **Personal health data references without consent**: Content cannot reference or imply knowledge of an individual's health conditions, prescriptions, or insurance status without explicit consent architecture. (T4_INFERRED from HIPAA compliance requirements)
5. **Emergency substitution framing**: MinuteClinic explicitly does not position itself as an emergency room substitute. Content suggesting it is appropriate for chest pain, stroke symptoms, or other emergencies would be clinically dangerous and legally problematic. (observed on MinuteClinic scope-of-care disclosures)
6. **Competitor prescription pricing comparisons without current verification**: Drug pricing changes frequently; asserting CVS is cheaper than a named competitor without current data creates legal exposure and is not CVS Health's typical content approach. (T4_INFERRED)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. In healthcare content, specificity removes anxiety faster than reassurance.**
"We're here for you" is a healthcare marketing cliché. "MinuteClinic can see you today between 8 AM and 8 PM, with a typical visit of 15–30 minutes, at 1,100 locations near you" is a healthcare content mechanism. Transferable structure: replace emotional reassurance language with operational specificity. Tell customers exactly what will happen, how long it will take, and what it will cost. Specificity is the anxiety-reducer that vague empathy cannot achieve.

**2. Build the prevention case as a financial argument, not a health argument.**
Health content that leads with health benefits ("vaccines prevent disease") competes against inertia and competing health priorities. Health content that leads with financial consequences ("an unvaccinated flu season costs you X in lost workdays and potential ER costs") converts a health decision into a financial risk-management decision. The financial frame reaches audiences that the health-benefit frame does not. Transferable structure: translate every prevention or maintenance product/service into its financial equivalent for the target audience.

**3. The loyalty program is also a content distribution channel.**
CVS's ExtraCare program reaches 74 million active members with direct communications: emails, app notifications, and direct mail. (official, CVS Health IR) This owned audience is larger than most consumer media brands' readership. Transferable structure: a loyalty or subscription program is not just a revenue mechanism — it is an owned media channel with consent-based access to your highest-value customers. The content strategy for the loyalty audience should be distinct from the acquisition content strategy.
