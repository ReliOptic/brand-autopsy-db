## Competitive Context Note

Duke Energy operates primarily as a regulated electric and gas utility with monopoly franchise rights in its service territories. Direct competition for retail customers does not exist in most of its markets. The competitive landscape relevant to Duke Energy involves:

1. **Investor competition**: Competing for utility sector capital allocation against peer utilities
2. **Regulatory positioning**: Competing for favorable rate case outcomes versus consumer/environmental advocacy
3. **Talent and strategic positioning**: Competing against energy sector peers for talent, technology partnerships, and clean energy project development

## Battle Card 1 — Duke Energy vs. NextEra Energy

**NextEra's observable position**:
- NextEra Energy Resources is the world's largest generator of renewable energy from wind and solar (official, NextEra IR)
- Florida Power & Light (FPL), NextEra's regulated utility, serves approximately 5.7M customers in Florida — overlapping with Duke Energy Florida's service territory (adjacent, not directly competing)
- Market cap of ~$140B reflects premium valuation for clean energy growth narrative (estimated)

**Duke Energy's observable position**:
- Duke Energy Florida serves approximately 1.9M customers in central Florida (SEC 10-K FY2024)
- Duke Energy's $73B capital plan through 2028 includes significant renewable and grid modernization investment (official, Duke Energy investor day)
- Duke Energy's regulated rate base across six states provides earnings stability that pure-play renewable developers cannot offer (T4_INFERRED)

**Key metric differences**:
- NextEra's renewable generation capacity: 35,000+ MW (official, NextEra IR)
- Duke Energy's renewable capacity: approximately 11,000 MW (estimated, based on Duke Energy generation fleet disclosures)

## Battle Card 2 — Duke Energy vs. Southern Company

**Southern Company's observable position**:
- Southern Company serves approximately 9M customers across GA, AL, MS, FL — comparable scale to Duke Energy (official, Southern Company IR)
- Southern Company has invested in nuclear power, including the Vogtle Units 3 and 4 in Georgia (completed 2024), representing a different clean energy pathway (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, widely reported)
- Southern Company's clean energy portfolio includes nuclear as a low-carbon baseload strategy (official, Southern Company)

**Duke Energy's observable position**:
- Duke Energy has a significant existing nuclear fleet (approximately 10,000 MW capacity) as part of its low-carbon baseload strategy (SEC 10-K FY2024)
- Duke Energy's geographic diversification across six states vs. Southern's concentration in the Deep South (T4_INFERRED from service territory comparison)

## Battle Card 3 — Duke Energy vs. Dominion Energy

**Dominion's observable position**:
- Dominion has divested its gas transmission and storage assets (2020 sale to Berkshire Hathaway Energy, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE) to focus on regulated utilities in Virginia and the Carolinas
- Dominion Virginia Power is a competitor in North Carolina's adjacent territory (T4_INFERRED)
- Dominion's market cap of ~$40B reflects smaller regulated rate base than Duke (estimated)

**Duke Energy's observable advantages**:
- Larger geographic footprint and customer base providing scale economics (T4_INFERRED)
- Diversified fuel mix including nuclear, natural gas, solar, and wind (SEC 10-K FY2024 generation fleet disclosures)
- Multi-state regulatory relationships reduce single-jurisdiction concentration risk (T4_INFERRED)

## Threats & Opportunities

### Threats

1. **Renewable cost competition**: Declining solar and battery storage costs may pressure regulators to favor distributed generation that bypasses Duke's central grid model. (T4_INFERRED based on energy market trends discussed in Duke's 10-K risk factors)
2. **Rate affordability pressure**: Large capital investment programs require rate increases; regulatory pushback from affordability advocates is a disclosed risk. (T1_OFFICIAL, 10-K risk factors)
3. **Coal plant retirement costs**: Stranded asset risk from accelerated coal retirement timelines; regulatory recovery of these costs is not guaranteed. (T1_OFFICIAL, 10-K risk factors)
4. **Severe weather exposure**: Duke's Carolinas and Florida territories are exposed to hurricane and ice storm risk that strains grid infrastructure and restoration costs. (T1_OFFICIAL, risk factor discussion)

### Opportunities

1. **Data center load growth**: The southeastern US is experiencing significant data center construction demand, driving electricity load growth that benefits regulated utilities with growing rate bases. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, trade press; Duke management commentary on earnings calls, T2_PRIMARY_RELIABLE)
2. **EV charging infrastructure**: Electric vehicle adoption creates new electricity demand and potential revenue from EV charging rate structures. (T4_INFERRED; Duke has programs underway)
3. **Grid modernization federal funding**: IRA (Inflation Reduction Act) and IIJA (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act) funding creates opportunities for capital investment with federal support. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
4. **Nuclear extension**: Duke's nuclear fleet extension (license renewals, potential uprates) provides low-carbon baseload capacity at lower cost than new construction. (T4_INFERRED from Duke Energy's disclosed capital plans)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. In monopoly or near-monopoly markets, brand competition is regulatory, not consumer.**
Duke Energy's brand success is measured in rate case approvals and favorable regulatory relationships, not consumer preference share. Transferable: for B2G businesses, platform ecosystems, or any market with structural advantages, define brand success metrics appropriate to your actual competitive context, not the consumer brand metrics borrowed from adjacent sectors.

**2. Benchmark the narrative, not just the financials.**
Duke Energy competes with peers like NextEra and Southern Company not just for capital but for the narrative of clean energy leadership. In investor communications, being perceived as a transition leader affects valuation multiples. Transferable: in capital-markets-facing businesses, the quality and credibility of the strategic narrative is a financial asset.

**3. Scale creates regulatory optionality.**
Duke's multi-state regulatory footprint means a difficult proceeding in one state can be offset by favorable outcomes in others. Transferable: multi-market or multi-segment businesses have natural hedges against single-jurisdiction risk. Make that diversification legible in investor and stakeholder communications.
