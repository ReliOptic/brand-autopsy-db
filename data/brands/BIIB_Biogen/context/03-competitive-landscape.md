## Battle Cards

### Battle Card 1: Biogen vs. Roche/Genentech (Multiple Sclerosis)

**Where Biogen wins**:
- Portfolio breadth — Biogen offers oral (Tecfidera, Vumerity), injectable (Avonex, Plegridy), and infusion (Tysabri) options, allowing personalization across the MS treatment spectrum; Roche's MS portfolio is primarily Ocrevus (infusion). (official, Biogen.com and Genentech.com product portfolios)
- Tysabri's natalizumab mechanism provides high efficacy in highly active relapsing MS and has a decades-long efficacy and safety data set. (T1_OFFICIAL, Tysabri prescribing information and clinical data)
- Established neurologist relationships built over 30+ years of MS commercialization. (T4_ESTIMATED)

**Where Roche wins**:
- Ocrevus is the only approved treatment for primary progressive MS (PPMS) — a segment Biogen does not address with equivalent approval. (T1_OFFICIAL, FDA approval for PPMS indication)
- Ocrevus has achieved market leadership in high-efficacy MS therapy based on clinical adoption data. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, industry analyst reports)
- Roche's anti-CD20 mechanism in Ocrevus has broad prescriber acceptance across the neurology community. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)

**Biogen talking point**: "For neurologists managing a diverse MS patient population — from newly diagnosed relapsing patients to highly active disease requiring maximum efficacy — Biogen's portfolio provides a complete treatment spectrum across oral, subcutaneous, and infusion routes with decades of outcome data." (T4_ESTIMATED)

### Battle Card 2: Biogen vs. Eli Lilly (Alzheimer's Disease)

**Where Biogen wins**:
- Leqembi received traditional FDA approval (July 2023) based on the confirmatory Clarity AD trial, which demonstrated statistically significant slowing of clinical decline on the CDR-SB endpoint. (T1_OFFICIAL, FDA traditional approval announcement July 6, 2023)
- Biogen's Alzheimer's commercial infrastructure, patient support programs, and healthcare system education investment were in place at Leqembi launch; Lilly's donanemab infrastructure was built subsequently. (T4_ESTIMATED)
- Biogen/Eisai partnership brings Eisai's Japanese market strength and combined commercial scale. (official, Biogen-Eisai collaboration announcements)

**Where Lilly wins**:
- Donanemab received FDA traditional approval in July 2024 for early symptomatic Alzheimer's, providing a competitor anti-amyloid option with distinct dosing characteristics (quarterly dosing and potential treatment completion). (T1_OFFICIAL, FDA approval July 2, 2024)
- Eli Lilly's overall commercial scale and financial resources exceed Biogen's; Lilly can sustain aggressive investment in Alzheimer's commercial execution and pipeline. (SEC 10-K FY2023, Lilly)
- Donanemab's dosing-to-completion model may appeal to patients and prescribers seeking a defined treatment course. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, clinical and industry commentary)

**Biogen talking point**: "Leqembi is the first anti-amyloid therapy to receive traditional FDA approval with a demonstrated statistically significant slowing of clinical decline, supported by the full Clarity AD dataset and a growing real-world evidence base across academic memory centers." (T4_ESTIMATED, grounded in T1_OFFICIAL FDA status)

### Battle Card 3: Biogen vs. Novartis (SMA)

**Where Biogen wins**:
- Spinraza has the longest clinical follow-up data in SMA of any approved treatment, with outcome data from ENDEAR, CHERISH, and SHINE studies extending years post-initiation. (official, Biogen clinical data publications)
- Spinraza is approved across all SMA types and ages, including pre-symptomatic patients — the broadest label. (T1_OFFICIAL, Spinraza prescribing information)
- Long-term safety data from thousands of patients provides clinical confidence for families making irreversible treatment decisions. (T4_ESTIMATED, based on published Spinraza real-world data)

**Where Novartis wins**:
- Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec) is a one-time gene therapy for SMA patients under 2 years of age — the convenience of a single treatment versus ongoing Spinraza intrathecal injections is a compelling benefit for families. (T1_OFFICIAL, Zolgensma prescribing information)
- Zolgensma's one-time administration model eliminates the scheduling and adherence burden of regular intrathecal injections. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, clinical commentary)

## Threats and Opportunities

### Threats

1. **Leqembi commercial uptake below expectation**: The initial commercial launch of Leqembi faced challenges including Medicare Coverage with Evidence Development restrictions, infrastructure requirements for diagnosis and monitoring, and prescriber hesitancy in the context of the Aduhelm controversy. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, healthcare media reporting; SEC 10-K FY2023, Risk Factors)
2. **MS portfolio erosion from biosimilars**: Biogen's older MS products (Avonex, Tecfidera) face biosimilar competition that is eroding revenue. The MS franchise, historically the primary revenue driver, is declining as high-efficacy branded competitors and biosimilars gain share. (SEC 10-K FY2023)
3. **Competitive pressure on Spinraza from gene therapy and oral options**: Zolgensma's one-time dosing and Risdiplam's oral route of administration create significant competitive pressure on Spinraza's intrathecal injection model, particularly in newly diagnosed patients. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)

### Opportunities

1. **Leqembi label expansion and uptake growth**: As CMS coverage restrictions evolve, amyloid PET access expands, and prescriber experience with ARIA management grows, Leqembi's accessible patient population may expand significantly. (T4_ESTIMATED, based on publicly discussed CMS coverage evolution)
2. **Pipeline in neuropsychiatry and rare neurological diseases**: Biogen's pipeline includes assets in Parkinson's disease, ALS, schizophrenia, and rare neurological diseases through licensing agreements — potential for revenue diversification within neuroscience. (official, Biogen pipeline page; SEC 10-K FY2023)
3. **Skyclarys (omaveloxolone) in Friedreich's ataxia**: Biogen's approval of Skyclarys in 2023 — the first approved therapy for Friedreich's ataxia — extends the company's rare neurology presence. (T1_OFFICIAL, FDA approval February 2023)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. First-mover approval in a neglected disease area creates durable market position.**
Biogen's approvals in MS (1990s), SMA (2016), and Alzheimer's (2023) each represented the first approved option in previously untreatable conditions. First-mover approval creates patient registries, prescriber familiarity, and payer pathway infrastructure that subsequent entrants must overcome, not just improve upon clinically. Transferable: in any market with unmet need and regulatory pathway, first-mover status creates structural advantages beyond the product itself.

**2. Sustained R&D commitment through failure cycles creates category ownership.**
The Alzheimer's field experienced multiple Phase 3 failures between 2000 and 2020. Biogen's continued investment, including the controversial Aduhelm approval, maintained the company's position at the frontier of the field when Leqembi succeeded. Transferable: in high-uncertainty research categories, the companies that remain committed through the failure cycle are positioned to own the category at breakthrough.

**3. Price a breakthrough therapy at a level the system can absorb, not just what the market might bear.**
The Aduhelm pricing controversy ($56,000/year initial list price) generated substantial reputational and policy damage that complicated Leqembi's launch. Biogen's subsequent pricing of Leqembi at approximately $26,500/year reflected a recalibration toward payer and patient access realities. Transferable: in healthcare markets where government payer and political scrutiny is intense, pricing that pushes the boundary of what the system will accept creates reputational and policy risk that can exceed the revenue upside.
