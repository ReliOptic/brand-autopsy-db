## 3. Major Litigation — Publicly Disclosed Cases

> All descriptions below are factual summaries of publicly disclosed legal proceedings. This project does not characterize any party's conduct as proven, unlawful, or intentional. All proceedings described are based on public filings and reputable journalism.

### 3.1 Cryptocurrency Revenue Disclosure — SEC Settlement (2022)

**Nature**: Regulatory enforcement action by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission.

**Summary**: In May 2022, Nvidia agreed to pay $5.5 million to settle SEC charges. The SEC alleged that Nvidia failed to adequately disclose that a significant portion of its FY2022 Gaming segment revenue was driven by demand from cryptocurrency mining customers, not traditional gamers. The SEC alleged this omission was material to investors evaluating the sustainability of Gaming segment revenue. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC Litigation Release No. 25326, May 6, 2022; available at sec.gov)

**Nvidia's position**: Nvidia neither admitted nor denied the SEC's findings as part of the settlement. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC administrative order)

**Resolution**: Settlement finalized. Nvidia paid the $5.5 million civil penalty. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC press release May 6, 2022)

**Writing risk for this project**: This settlement is a public enforcement record. It should be described factually as a regulatory settlement with no admission of wrongdoing, per the SEC's own order language. The word "fraud" should not be used in connection with this matter without citing a specific legal determination that uses that term. (T4_INFERRED from project legal writing protocol)

### 3.2 Arm Acquisition Termination — Regulatory Blocked Deal (2022)

**Nature**: Regulatory proceedings by FTC (U.S.), EU Commission, UK Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), and China SAMR.

**Summary**: In September 2020, Nvidia announced an agreement to acquire Arm Holdings from SoftBank for approximately $40 billion in equity and cash. Multiple global regulators initiated investigations on grounds that the transaction raised competition concerns in the semiconductor and intellectual property licensing markets. In February 2022, Nvidia announced the termination of the proposed acquisition, citing regulatory challenges. (T1_OFFICIAL, Nvidia 8-K February 8, 2022; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Bloomberg, Reuters)

**Financial impact**: Nvidia disclosed a $1.36 billion charge related to the terminated acquisition (breakup fee component) in its FY2022 financial statements. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K FY2022)

**Current status**: Arm Holdings subsequently completed a separate IPO on NASDAQ in September 2023. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Arm IPO coverage)

**Writing risk for this project**: The regulatory proceedings that blocked this deal are public record. This project may describe regulators' stated competition concerns factually (with citation to official agency statements) but should not characterize regulatory conclusions as proving anticompetitive conduct. (T4_INFERRED)

### 3.3 Securities Class Action Litigation — H100 Demand Disclosure (Ongoing as of FY2025 10-K)

**Nature**: Shareholder class action lawsuit.

**Summary**: Per Nvidia's FY2025 10-K, Item 3 (Legal Proceedings), Nvidia discloses that it is subject to various legal proceedings and claims that arise in the ordinary course of business. Investors should consult the most recent 10-K directly for current litigation disclosures, as the status of legal proceedings changes and this analysis may not reflect the most current state. (T1_OFFICIAL, 10-K FY2025 Item 3)

**Writing risk for this project**: Class action filings contain allegations, not proven facts. This project should describe shareholder litigation as "alleged" and cite the SEC filing rather than any secondary reporting. (T4_INFERRED)

### 3.4 Antitrust Regulatory Inquiries — AI Market Position (Reported 2024)

**Nature**: Regulatory inquiry / investigation.

**Summary**: Multiple press reports indicate that Nvidia's dominant position in the AI accelerator market attracted regulatory attention from U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ), EU Commission, and other authorities in 2024. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE: Bloomberg, Reuters, WSJ coverage 2024) At the time this analysis was prepared, no formal charges or enforcement actions had been publicly confirmed by the relevant agencies. Nvidia's FY2025 10-K includes risk factor disclosure regarding antitrust regulatory risk. (T1_OFFICIAL)

**Writing risk for this project**: Antitrust investigations at the inquiry stage are not enforcement actions. This project must clearly distinguish between (a) reported regulatory inquiry and (b) formal charges. Using the word "monopoly" as a factual assertion without citing a court determination would be inappropriate. (T4_INFERRED from project legal writing protocol)

### 3.5 Export Control Compliance — China Restrictions (Ongoing)

**Nature**: Regulatory compliance matter; not litigation.

**Summary**: The U.S. Department of Commerce Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) issued expanded export control regulations affecting advanced semiconductor chips, including Nvidia's H100, A100, H800, and A800 GPUs, in October 2023 and subsequent updates. These regulations require export licenses for shipment to certain geographies, including China. Nvidia has disclosed these restrictions as material to its operations in multiple 10-K and 8-K filings. (T1_OFFICIAL, Nvidia 8-K October 2023; FY2024 and FY2025 10-K Risk Factors)

**Nvidia's disclosed response**: Development and sale of the H20 chip (engineered to comply with export regulations) for the China market. Disclosure of expected revenue impact in IR communications. (T1_OFFICIAL)

**Writing risk for this project**: Export control compliance is a government-imposed regulatory requirement. This project should not characterize Nvidia's response to export controls as evidence of evasion, circumvention, or bad faith. The disclosed H20 product development is a disclosed, legal compliance response. (T4_INFERRED)

### 5.4 Writing Risk Guide — Nvidia Specific

#### SAFE TO WRITE (with proper citation)

- Revenue, earnings, and segment data from SEC 10-K/10-Q with specific fiscal year and filing citation
- Product specifications from Nvidia's official datasheets with citation to nvidia.com
- Litigation descriptions that match SEC disclosure language with citation to specific 10-K Item 3 or 8-K
- Trademark and patent data from USPTO public records with search citation
- Regulatory settlement facts from SEC press releases (sec.gov) with date and release number
- Competitive comparisons using specific, sourced metrics (e.g., MLPerf results, published datasheets)
- Market capitalization observations with date and "market-determined figure, not investment advice" caveat

#### REQUIRES CAREFUL HEDGING

| Topic | Required Language Approach |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Antitrust inquiry | "Press reports indicate regulatory inquiry has been initiated" — never "Nvidia is engaged in anticompetitive conduct" |
| Class action allegations | "Plaintiffs allege..." or "The complaint alleges..." — never "Nvidia [did the alleged thing]" |
| Export control characterization | "U.S. regulations require export licenses for..." — never "Nvidia was prohibited from selling to China" (too absolute; licensing is the mechanism) |
| AI market share estimates | "Estimated at approximately X% per analyst reports" with source — never as established fact |
| Jensen Huang's statements | "Jensen Huang stated in [event/call]..." with specific citation — never paraphrase as settled company policy |
| Competitor characterizations | Metric-based only ("AMD MI300X TFLOPS figure vs. H100 TFLOPS figure") — never qualitative superiority assertions |
| CUDA market position | "Estimated X% of AI training workloads use CUDA per analyst reports" — never as an undisputed fact |

#### DO NOT WRITE — Absolute Prohibitions

- "Nvidia defrauded investors" — the SEC settlement involves no admission of fraud; "SEC alleged disclosure failures" is accurate
- "Nvidia has a monopoly" — this is a legal conclusion that requires court determination; write "dominant market position" with analyst source
- "Nvidia engaged in regulatory misconduct" — intent attribution without court finding
- Any claim that the Arm deal was blocked because Nvidia intended anticompetitive harm — regulators cited concerns; intent is not established
- "Nvidia's CUDA is designed to lock customers in" — "designed to" implies intent; write "CUDA's architecture creates switching costs that have been observed to..." with T4_INFERRED label
- Prediction of outcomes in ongoing litigation or regulatory proceedings
- Any characterization of export control compliance as evasion without a specific enforcement action citation

## 11. Brand-Legal Interface — Practical Writing Guide for This Project

### 11.1 How to Write About the 2022 SEC Settlement

**Correct approach**:
> "In May 2022, Nvidia agreed to pay $5.5 million to settle SEC charges related to alleged failures to adequately disclose that Gaming segment revenue was materially impacted by cryptocurrency mining demand. Nvidia neither admitted nor denied the SEC's findings. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC Litigation Release No. 25326, May 6, 2022)"

**Incorrect approach (prohibited)**:
> "Nvidia defrauded investors by hiding its crypto revenue." — This asserts fraud without legal determination and mischaracterizes a settlement that included no admission of wrongdoing.

### 11.2 How to Write About CUDA Market Position

**Correct approach**:
> "Nvidia's CUDA platform is estimated to power approximately 80–90% of AI training workloads, based on analyst reports from Mercury Research and multiple sell-side technology analysts. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE) This concentration creates significant switching costs for organizations whose ML engineering teams have invested in CUDA-optimized code. (T4_INFERRED)"

**Incorrect approach (prohibited)**:
> "Nvidia has a monopoly on AI computing." — "Monopoly" is a legal conclusion requiring court determination. "Dominant market position" is the correct descriptor with an analyst source.

### 11.3 How to Write About Competitors

**Correct approach**:
> "AMD's MI300X offers 192GB of HBM3 memory per card (official, AMD MI300X product brief), compared to the H100's 80GB and H200's 141GB HBM3e (official, Nvidia H100 and H200 product briefs). For LLM inference workloads requiring large model context windows, AMD's higher memory capacity may reduce the need for tensor parallelism across multiple cards. (T4_INFERRED from memory capacity comparison)"

**Incorrect approach (prohibited)**:
> "AMD's MI300X is inferior to the H100." — Qualitative inferiority assertion without metric basis. "Inferior" is a value judgment; specific metric comparison is the correct approach.

### 11.4 How to Write About Antitrust Inquiries

**Correct approach**:
> "Press reports indicate that the European Commission and U.S. Department of Justice have conducted preliminary inquiries into Nvidia's position in the AI accelerator market. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, Bloomberg, Reuters, 2024) At the time this analysis was prepared, no formal charges or enforcement decisions had been publicly announced by the relevant agencies. Nvidia's FY2025 10-K discloses antitrust regulatory risk as a risk factor. (T1_OFFICIAL)"

**Incorrect approach (prohibited)**:
> "Nvidia is under antitrust investigation for monopolizing AI chips." — "Monopolizing" is a legal conclusion; "investigation" overstates inquiry status; specific agency attribution required.
