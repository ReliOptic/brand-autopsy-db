## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

**Primary ICP — Commercial Airline Operator**
- Organization type: Commercial airline (flag carrier, low-cost carrier, cargo operator)
- Fleet size: 20–1,000+ aircraft
- Decision-makers: CEO, CFO, VP Fleet Planning, VP Maintenance
- Purchase cycle: 5–15 year fleet planning horizon; orders placed years in advance
- Key drivers: Fuel efficiency, seat-mile economics, pilot type rating compatibility, maintenance cost, regulatory certification
- Geography: Global; U.S., Europe, Middle East, Asia-Pacific primary markets

**Secondary ICP — Defense / Government**
- Organization type: U.S. Department of Defense, allied nation defense ministries, NASA
- Decision-makers: Procurement officers, program managers, legislative appropriators
- Purchase cycle: Multi-year defense contracts; often sole-source or duopoly competition
- Key drivers: Mission capability, schedule reliability, cost-plus contract management, domestic manufacturing content

## Three Personas

### Persona 1: "The Fleet Planner" — Catherine, 52
- **Role**: VP Fleet and Network Planning at a major U.S. airline
- **Challenge**: Retiring aging 757 fleet; needs a replacement with similar range and runway performance for thin routes
- **Pain**: Supply chain delays from Boeing post-COVID have pushed delivery slots; parts availability causing AOG (aircraft on ground) events
- **Gain**: The 737 MAX 10's fuel efficiency vs. predecessor (14–20% improvement per Boeing claims, official, boeing.com/737max) and common type rating with existing 737 fleet eliminates retraining cost
- **Decision trigger**: FAA certification status and delivery slot confirmation
- **Loyalty driver**: Fleet standardization — a 200-aircraft fleet trained on one type cannot easily switch

### Persona 2: "The Defense Program Manager" — Col. Robert, 47
- **Role**: USAF program manager for next-generation mobility aircraft acquisition
- **Pain**: Congressional budget uncertainty; schedule risk from prime contractor delays
- **Gain**: Boeing's established relationship with USAF, existing platform expertise (C-17, KC-46), and domestic manufacturing base
- **Decision dynamic**: Government procurement; award criteria defined by RFP specifications, not brand preference — but Boeing's incumbent relationships and past performance ratings are structural advantages (T4_INFERRED from defense procurement dynamics)

### Persona 3: "The MRO Director" — James, 44
- **Role**: Director of Maintenance, Repair & Overhaul at a regional airline
- **Pain**: Parts availability; Boeing's parts supply chain experienced significant disruption post-COVID
- **Gain**: Boeing Global Services' ePlanning and analytics tools; OEM parts with airworthiness certification documentation
- **Loyalty driver**: OEM parts carry regulatory certainty that aftermarket alternatives cannot always provide

## Anti-Persona

**Who Boeing does NOT serve:**
- Private aviation buyers (general aviation) — Boeing does not manufacture business jets or piston aircraft; that segment belongs to Cessna, Gulfstream, Bombardier
- Budget airlines requiring sub-100-seat turboprops — Boeing's narrowbody minimum is the 737 family (~120+ seats)
- Customers requiring rapid delivery — Boeing's order backlog for new aircraft is measured in years; buyers needing immediate delivery must acquire used aircraft through other channels

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. In B2B, switching costs are your most durable brand asset.**
A fleet operator with 200 Boeing-trained pilots cannot easily migrate to Airbus without multi-year type rating programs. Design your product so that adoption creates organizational dependencies — training, workflow, data — that make switching costly independent of satisfaction.

**2. Air shows are the product launch format for capital goods.**
Boeing's major product and order announcements happen at Paris and Farnborough. The audience is global, the coverage is industry-wide, and the social proof (order count announcements) is immediate. Transferable: identify your industry's equivalent of an air show — the conference or event where buyer decisions are legitimized — and invest disproportionately in presence there.

**3. Serve the operator's downstream customer.**
Boeing's most powerful sales argument is not the aircraft specification — it is the end passenger's preference for newer, quieter, more comfortable aircraft. By keeping airlines focused on the passenger experience benefit, Boeing elevates the conversation above price. Transferable: in B2B sales, identify your customer's customer and build your value proposition around serving that downstream end user.
