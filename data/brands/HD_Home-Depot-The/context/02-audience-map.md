## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

| Dimension | Profile |
|-----------|---------|
| **Core Demographics** | Ages 25–64, homeowner (70%+ of transactions, estimated); ages 25–44 skew DIY; 45–64 skew Pro and project-delegation. Household income $60K–$150K for DIY core; professional contractors across all income tiers. (estimated, based on JCHS 2024 home improvement market research, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE) |
| **Occupation segments** | (DIY) Homeowners, apartment renters undertaking permitted renovations, weekend warriors; (Pro) General contractors, remodeling contractors, roofers, plumbers, electricians, HVAC technicians, property managers, facility maintenance professionals |
| **Platform behavior** | homedepot.com attracts approximately 180–200 million monthly unique visitors (estimated, SimilarWeb, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE). Mobile app downloads ranked consistently in top 10 shopping apps in the U.S. App Store (estimated). Pro Xtra loyalty members account for approximately 50% of company sales despite representing a smaller share of transaction count. (T4_INFERRED from company guidance commentary) |
| **Psychographic — DIY** | Derives identity satisfaction from completing projects personally. "I built that" is a social signal. Values competence, savings, and visible results. YouTube how-to content is the primary pre-purchase research medium. Not trying to become a professional — trying to feel like one. |
| **Psychographic — Pro** | Time is the primary constraint. Product availability, account credit terms, and delivery reliability matter more than price. Loyalty is earned through operational reliability, not promotions. Switching supplier means risking project timeline. |
| **Purchase triggers — DIY** | Seasonal events (spring lawn/garden, fall prep); homeownership lifecycle events (new home purchase, pre-sale renovation); emergency repair (appliance failure, water damage); weekend inspiration via social media or HGTV content. |
| **Purchase triggers — Pro** | Job award → materials procurement; standing reorder relationships; project bid phase requiring material pricing; equipment failure requiring same-day rental. |
| **Geographic concentration** | ~90% U.S.-based operations; approximately 180 Canadian stores (The Home Depot Canada); approximately 13 stores in Mexico (The Home Depot México) as of FY2024. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K FY2024) |

## Core Personas

### Persona 1 — Marcus, Weekend DIY Homeowner

- **Age / Occupation**: 34, high school science teacher, suburban Atlanta, GA (first home purchased 18 months ago)
- **Household income**: $78,000/year
- **Spend profile**: $2,400–$3,600/year at The Home Depot (estimated); primarily in-store, increasingly BOPIS (Buy Online, Pick Up In Store) for planned projects
- **Active projects**: Replaced bathroom vanity, installing LVP flooring in living room, planning a deck build for next summer
- **Core pain**: He can watch a 12-minute YouTube video and understand the steps, but the gap between understanding and execution surfaces at the store. Which brand of underlayment? What grade of deck board for his climate? How many 2x6s does a 14×20 deck need? Walking the lumber aisle alone with a phone calculator is anxiety-producing.
- **Home Depot selection rationale**: Associates at his local store (Decatur, GA) have caught two costly mistakes. First: an associate in flooring identified a subfloor moisture issue before he installed anything. Second: a Pro Desk associate walked him through a deck permit checklist he didn't know existed. These interventions, not price or selection, are why he drives past a Lowe's to reach The Home Depot.
- **Brand relationship**: Pro Xtra member (enrolled through cashier prompt); watches The Home Depot's YouTube channel; attended one free Saturday morning workshop on tile installation. Considers himself "intermediate" and uses The Home Depot as a benchmark for advancing his skills.
- **Churn risk**: Medium. If a Lowe's or independent hardware store offers materially better in-store advisory in his category, or if Amazon same-day delivery expands meaningfully on commodity items (fasteners, paint brushes), he will bifurcate spend. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Media touchpoints**: YouTube (home improvement content), Instagram (renovation accounts), The Home Depot app, in-store end-cap displays

### Persona 2 — Diana, Independent Remodeling Contractor (Core Pro)

- **Age / Occupation**: 48, sole proprietor of a kitchen and bath remodeling business, Phoenix, AZ metro; 2–3 active projects simultaneously, typically $35,000–$85,000 project value
- **Annual spend at The Home Depot**: $180,000–$240,000/year (estimated); almost exclusively through Pro Xtra account with invoiced credit terms
- **Project load**: 22–26 completed projects per year; materials account for 35–40% of total job cost
- **Core pain**: A delayed cabinet delivery or wrong SKU in a tile order means three people standing around at $50/hour while a client calls for a progress update. Logistics must be invisible. Availability, accuracy, and on-time delivery are non-negotiable.
- **Home Depot selection rationale**: The Pro Xtra account delivers 2% back on eligible purchases, dedicated Pro Desk phone access, and priority order staging in the Pro area outside the main floor. The SRS Distribution acquisition (2024, ~$18.25B) adds roofing material access through an integrated supply network — relevant when projects include exterior work. (T1_OFFICIAL, 8-K SRS acquisition; T4_INFERRED for Pro implication)
- **Brand relationship**: Transactional loyalty — not emotional. She does not wear a Home Depot t-shirt. But she has referred three subcontractors to Pro Xtra because the referral earns her credit. The relationship is economic, and the Pro ecosystem was designed precisely for this dynamic.
- **Churn risk**: Low for primary supply, moderate for specialty. She sources custom cabinetry from specialty suppliers regardless of Home Depot's catalog. If Lowe's Pro or a regional supply house offers better credit terms or delivery windows, she would test them on one project. Switching cost is high but not infinite. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Media touchpoints**: Pro Xtra app, email invoices, Pro Desk relationship, NARI (National Association of the Remodeling Industry) publications

### Persona 3 — Robert, Property Manager (Institutional Pro)

- **Age / Occupation**: 55, facilities director for a 400-unit apartment complex portfolio in Dallas-Fort Worth metro
- **Annual spend at The Home Depot**: $90,000–$130,000/year across maintenance and renovation categories; managed through a corporate Pro Xtra account
- **Scope**: Maintenance of HVAC, plumbing, electrical, appliances, and common areas across 4 properties; coordinates 6 maintenance technicians
- **Core pain**: Turn time on vacant units drives revenue. A vacant unit costs $1,200–$1,500 in lost rent per week. Every day waiting for an appliance, fixture, or paint color is revenue lost. He needs same-day availability across a wide product range, not best price.
- **Home Depot selection rationale**: The Home Depot's Buy Online, Ship to Store same-day program and the Rental Center for one-off equipment needs minimize capital tied up in inventory. Store network density means at least one location is within 10 miles of every property he manages.
- **Brand relationship**: Institutional, not personal. The account is managed by procurement. However, he advocates for maintaining The Home Depot as primary supplier because the operational risk of switching — retraining 6 technicians on new account systems, losing established credit terms — outweighs any price differential on individual items. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Churn risk**: Low near-term; moderate if a competitor builds a dedicated multi-property facilities management program with better account tooling. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Media touchpoints**: National Apartment Association publications, The Home Depot Pro account portal, Pro Xtra email communications

### Persona 4 — Linda, "Do-It-Herself" Homeowner (Emerging Segment)

- **Age / Occupation**: 41, marketing manager, divorced homeowner in Chicago suburbs, children ages 9 and 12
- **Household income**: $95,000/year
- **Annual spend at The Home Depot**: $1,800–$2,400/year
- **Active projects**: Repainting interior rooms, building raised garden beds, replacing bathroom hardware, planning kitchen cabinet refresh with paint-and-new-hardware approach
- **Core pain**: The home improvement retail environment has historically coded as masculine — heavy equipment, ambiguous aisle labeling, associates who default to explaining basics she already knows. She wants advice calibrated to her actual knowledge level, not assumptions about it.
- **Home Depot selection rationale**: The Home Depot's "Do-It-Herself" workshop program and instructional content specifically targeting women homeowners (observed on homedepot.com DIY projects) directly address her experience gap. Online project calculators and step-by-step project guides reduce dependence on in-store interaction when she prefers self-directed research.
- **Brand relationship**: Growing. Was formerly a "send a handyman" default customer. Has completed 4 independent projects over 18 months and derives material identity satisfaction from each. The Home Depot is the enabler of that identity shift. Strong candidate for Pro Xtra enrollment via increasing project volume.
- **Churn risk**: Medium-high. This segment is contested by Pinterest-adjacent brands (Target home goods, Wayfair) and HGTV-adjacent retailers. If The Home Depot's DIY content and store experience continue to feel male-default, this persona bifurcates spend toward platforms with more visible representation. (T4_INFERRED)
- **Media touchpoints**: Instagram, Pinterest, YouTube, HGTV, The Home Depot app, DIY blog content

## Purchase Journey — AARRR Framework

| Stage | The Home Depot's Mechanism | Key Indicators |
|-------|---------------------------|----------------|
| **Acquisition** | Spring and fall seasonal campaigns ("Let's Do This"); Homer mascot advertising; homedepot.com how-to content dominates Google searches for project-related queries (estimated, SEMrush, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE); Black Friday/Memorial Day promotional events; Pro Xtra referral program | Net new Pro Xtra enrollments; store traffic counts; website unique visitors (~180–200M/month, estimated) |
| **Activation** | First project completion with associate guidance; first BOPIS order; Pro Desk first contact; how-to workshop attendance | App download + first order; Pro Xtra card first transaction |
| **Retention** | Pro Xtra loyalty tiers (2% back on eligible purchases for qualifying volume, T1_OFFICIAL program terms); seasonal email cadence; project reminder notifications; delivery subscription for Pro accounts; repeat commodity delivery | Pro Xtra repeat purchase rate; average transaction frequency (~30–35/year for active Pro customers, estimated) |
| **Referral** | Pro-to-Pro referral incentives within Pro Xtra; how-to content sharing via social (DIY project results as social identity content); "Built with The Home Depot" user-generated project posts | Social UGC volume; Pro referral credit redemption |
| **Revenue** | Average ticket approximately $90 (estimated from annual transactions vs. reported revenue); Pro customers drive disproportionate average ticket and frequency. SRS Distribution adds commercial building materials revenue stream. | Total comparable sales; Pro segment ~50% of sales (estimated); online sales as % of net sales (management has cited ~15%, T4_INFERRED from company digital commentary) |

## Anti-Persona — Who The Home Depot Is Not For

| Type | Characteristics | Why The Home Depot Is Not the Match |
|------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Luxury renovation buyer** | Seeks Restoration Hardware-grade curated aesthetics, white-glove design service, bespoke materials. Willing to pay 3–5× market rate for premium finishes. | The Home Depot's value proposition centers on accessibility and breadth, not curated luxury. The brand's aesthetic register is aspirational-accessible, not luxury-exclusive. |
| **Hyper-specialized commercial trade** | Master electrician or mechanical contractor requiring commercial-grade specialty product lines not stocked at retail depth | Industrial distributors (Grainger, Fastenal, Graybar) serve this customer better. Home Depot's commercial depth is improving post-SRS acquisition but remains secondary to specialty distributors for highest-specification commercial work. |
| **Pure price optimizer** | Will drive to three stores and check two apps to save $4 on a box of screws | The Home Depot's pricing is competitive but not always lowest on commodity items. Amazon and warehouse clubs capture this customer on fungible commodity SKUs. The Home Depot's value proposition is convenience, selection breadth, and service — not guaranteed price leadership on every item. |
| **Rural online-only customer** | Lives in a rural area without reasonable proximity to a store; relies entirely on mail-order for all home improvement needs | The Home Depot's competitive advantage is built on store density and same-day availability. Rural customers without convenient store access receive a structurally diminished version of the brand promise. |
