## 3. Major Litigation and Legal History

> All descriptions below are factual summaries of publicly disclosed legal proceedings.
> This document does not characterize any party's conduct beyond what is stated in official
> filings, court orders, or regulatory agency communications.

### 3.1 The Home Depot Data Breach (2014) — Settled

**Nature**: In September 2014, The Home Depot disclosed that its payment card systems had
been compromised, affecting approximately 56 million payment card records and 53 million
email addresses, per public reporting at the time. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, widely reported
by Bloomberg, Reuters, and major media)

**Resolution**: The Home Depot reached settlements with multiple parties:
- Settlement with payment card networks and issuing banks: approximately $25 million
  disclosed (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, media reporting)
- Settlement with state attorneys general: approximately $17.5 million across 46 states
  plus D.C. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, media reporting; The Home Depot 8-K disclosures)
- Class action settlement with consumers: terms disclosed in court approval filings
  (T1_OFFICIAL, public court records)

**Status as of FY2024 10-K**: The Home Depot's FY2024 10-K discloses that the material
litigation arising from the 2014 breach has been substantially resolved. The company
notes in its risk factors that cybersecurity incidents remain a material ongoing risk.
(T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K FY2024)

**Significance for this project**: The 2014 breach is the most significant legal event
in The Home Depot's recent history by settlement magnitude. It established that the
company faces material financial consequences from data security failures and resulted
in the payment of disclosed settlements to multiple parties. Writing about this event
must use the settlement figures and disclosed facts only — no characterization of the
cause as negligence or misconduct unless drawn from a specific court finding using those
terms.

### 3.2 SRS Distribution Acquisition — Regulatory Review (2024)

**Nature**: The $18.25 billion acquisition of SRS Distribution was subject to standard
antitrust review under the Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) Act. (T1_OFFICIAL, HD 8-K filing,
June 2024)

**Status**: The acquisition closed in June 2024, indicating HSR clearance was obtained.
The Home Depot disclosed the acquisition close via 8-K. (T1_OFFICIAL, HD 8-K, June 2024)

**Significance**: The successful clearance indicates the relevant antitrust authorities
did not identify the transaction as raising material competitive concerns in the relevant
markets at the time of review. As SRS Distribution is a specialty trade distributor
(not a retail competitor to The Home Depot's store format), the competitive overlap was
assessed as limited. (T4_INFERRED from standard antitrust review outcomes and market
structure; this is project analysis, not a regulatory determination)

### 3.3 Employment and Labor Proceedings

The Home Depot's FY2024 10-K, Part I, Item 3 discloses that the company is from time
to time a party to various employment-related legal proceedings including wage and hour
class actions, discrimination claims, and NLRB proceedings, which it describes as
"ordinary course" for a company of its size and workforce scale. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K
FY2024) The company employs approximately 465,000 associates as of FY2024 (estimated,
T4_INFERRED from 10-K workforce disclosure context).

**For this project**: Employment litigation at this scale is routine for large U.S.
retailers with large hourly workforces. Do not characterize employment proceedings as
evidence of systemic misconduct without specific court findings or regulatory orders
using such characterizations.

### 3.4 Consumer Class Actions

The Home Depot is periodically subject to consumer class action lawsuits, including
product liability, pricing accuracy, and consumer protection claims. These are disclosed
in the 10-K as ongoing and are characterized by the company as "ordinary course"
proceedings. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K FY2024, Part I, Item 3)

The Home Depot's international operations in Canada and Mexico, as well as its global
supply chain, create potential exposure under the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)
and equivalent international anti-corruption frameworks. The 10-K identifies compliance
with anti-corruption laws as a risk factor. No specific FCPA proceedings are disclosed
in the FY2024 10-K. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K FY2024 risk factors)

The Home Depot's data environment — processing millions of payment card transactions
and managing the Pro Xtra member database — creates significant data privacy regulatory
exposure:

| Regulation | Jurisdiction | Relevance |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| **PCI DSS** (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) | Industry standard (card network mandate) | Direct applicability to all payment card processing; 2014 breach established consequences of non-compliance |

| **State privacy laws** (Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, others) | Multiple U.S. states | Growing patchwork of state-level privacy obligations |

part of the settlement terms. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, state AG announcements) The Home
Depot's 10-K discloses ongoing investment in cybersecurity as a response to disclosed
risks. (T1_OFFICIAL, SEC 10-K FY2024)

### 4.3 Environmental Regulation

The Home Depot sells regulated products including pesticides, paint, paint solvents,
treated lumber, and propane. Its distribution centers and fleet operations are subject
to emissions regulations. The Home Depot Foundation and ESG disclosures indicate ongoing
environmental commitments. (T1_OFFICIAL, HD ESG Report; homedepot.com/c/sustainability)

### 5.1 The Home Depot's Litigation Posture

**Assessment**: MODERATE — IP-protective with active resolution of major class actions
(T4_INFERRED based on public litigation record)

**Basis for this assessment**:
- The Home Depot's litigation history centers on: (a) data breach aftermath (now substantially
  resolved), (b) employment class actions (routine for large retailers), (c) consumer class
  actions (routine for large retailers), and (d) M&A regulatory compliance.
- The Home Depot does not appear, based on publicly available records, to have pursued
  defamation actions against financial analysts, brand researchers, or journalists for
  factual reporting based on public sources. (T4_INFERRED from absence of such cases in
  public records)
- The company actively monitors and enforces its trademark portfolio, particularly against
  uses of "Home Depot" or the orange apron identity in ways that could create consumer
  confusion. (T4_INFERRED from trademark filing patterns)

**Implication for this project**: Our primary risks are (a) trademark misuse (using "Home
Depot" in ways that could imply sponsorship or affiliation), (b) false factual assertions
about the company's operations, financials, or legal history, and (c) characterizing
settled or ongoing proceedings beyond what the public record supports. The risk of The
Home Depot pursuing litigation against this project for properly hedged, publicly-sourced
brand analysis is assessed as LOW, provided content discipline is maintained. (T4_INFERRED)

### 5.3 Fair Use 4-Factor Analysis (17 U.S.C. § 107)

**Factor 1 — Purpose and Character of Use**
- Our use is analytical and educational, not commercial reproduction of HD's creative works.
- Content is transformative: we analyze, synthesize, and comment on publicly available
  information rather than reproducing The Home Depot's original creative expression.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE

**Factor 2 — Nature of the Copyrighted Work**
- We primarily work with factual data (financial figures, regulatory records, product specs,
  observed design elements) rather than HD's creative works.
- Factual works receive thinner copyright protection than creative expression.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE

**Factor 3 — Amount and Substantiality**
- We do not reproduce HD's marketing copy, video scripts, or creative content verbatim
  in substantial portions.
- We cite and summarize; we do not duplicate.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE (provided citation-and-summary discipline is maintained)

**Factor 4 — Effect on the Market**
- Our analysis does not substitute for HD's products, investor relations materials,
  or any commercial offering HD provides.
- Assessment: FAVORS FAIR USE

**Overall Assessment**: The project's data collection and analysis activities, as currently
scoped, appear defensible under fair use doctrine (T4_INFERRED; this is not legal advice —
consult qualified IP counsel for formal opinion).

### 5.4 Writing Risk Guide — The Home Depot, Inc.

> This is the most operationally critical section for day-to-day content creation.
> Follow these guidelines for every sentence written about The Home Depot in this project.

#### SAFE TO WRITE (with proper sourcing)

- Revenue, net income, margin figures from HD's SEC filings, cited as "(SEC 10-K FY20XX)"
- Store count, geographic footprint from most recent 10-K
- Descriptions of HD's products and services based on homedepot.com or press releases
- Summaries of litigation and regulatory proceedings citing specific regulators, courts,
  and official press releases or settlement announcements
- Descriptions of HD's trademarks citing USPTO records
- Historical financial performance trends citing public 10-K data
- Quotations from HD's own SEC filings, earnings calls, or press releases with attribution
- Descriptions of Pro Xtra program benefits as stated on homedepot.com
- Comparisons of HD's financial metrics to competitors using cited public data
- Description of the 2014 data breach using disclosed settlement facts

#### REQUIRES CAREFUL HEDGING

| Topic | Required Hedging Approach |
|-------|--------------------------|
| Pro segment revenue share | "The Home Depot has indicated approximately 50% of sales are attributed to Pro customers" — tag as estimated; the company has not disclosed an exact figure in its 10-K segment reporting |
| SRS integration success | "The integration is ongoing" — do not predict outcomes or characterize integration quality |
| Online sales percentage | "Management has referenced approximately 15%" — tag as estimated; not in 10-K line items |
| Competitor comparisons | Use specific financial metrics; never "better" or "worse" as bare assertions |
| 2014 breach characterization | Use disclosed settlement amounts and facts only; do not characterize the breach as resulting from specific internal failings unless directly cited from court findings using those terms |
| Associate labor practices | Use 10-K disclosures and official ESG report; tag third-party investigative reports as T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE |
| Housing market impact | "Management has attributed..." — use company's own characterizations from earnings calls |
| SRS acquisition strategic rationale | "The acquisition was described by management as..." — cite earnings call commentary; do not assert strategic intent beyond what management has disclosed |

#### DO NOT WRITE (High Legal / Project Integrity Risk)

- Any assertion that The Home Depot engaged in fraud, deception, or criminal conduct without
  direct citation from a court judgment or regulatory final order using those specific terms
- Characterizing the 2014 data breach as resulting from "negligence" or "recklessness" unless
  a court or regulatory body has used those specific terms in a final order
- Claiming knowledge of HD internal deliberations, strategy sessions, or executive intent not
  disclosed in public filings
- Reproducing substantial portions of HD's copyrighted marketing copy, how-to guides,
  or video content
- Publishing or referencing non-public information or leaked internal documents
- Asserting that any ongoing proceeding has a predetermined outcome
- Using HD's logo, wordmark, orange apron imagery, or Homer mascot in our database without
  transformative editorial purpose and clear analytical context
- Asserting HD is guilty of any violation in a proceeding that has not reached final judgment
- Repeating unverified forum posts or anonymous claims about HD practices as factual statements
- Attempting to access homedepot.com data through technical circumvention of access controls
  (HTTP 403 responses must be respected)
