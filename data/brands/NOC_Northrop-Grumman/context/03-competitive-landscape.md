## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Competitive Principles

**1. Depth beats breadth in concentrated markets.**
Northrop Grumman's deliberate concentration in stealth aviation, nuclear systems, and space intelligence — rather than competing across all defense domains at Lockheed Martin's breadth — has resulted in sole-source positions on the two most strategically significant defense programs in the current U.S. procurement cycle. In B2B markets with long switching cycles, owning the deepest capability in a narrow segment often generates more durable revenue than spread-thin competition across many segments.

**2. A program loss can become a positioning win.**
Northrop Grumman lost the F-22 program to Lockheed Martin in 1991. Rather than remaining in the tactical fighter market, it concentrated its advanced aircraft investment on low-observable bombers — a market where it had deeper heritage — and ultimately won the B-21. The competitive loss clarified strategic focus. Transferable: after a significant competitive loss, analyze whether the defeat represents a capability gap worth closing or a signal to concentrate resources where your institutional advantage is greatest.

**3. Fixed-price contracts are a brand signal, not just a financial instrument.**
Northrop Grumman bid the B-21 on a fixed-price contract — absorbing development risk rather than passing it to the government. This signals institutional confidence in the technical approach and program management maturity. In competitive B2B contexts, willingness to accept outcome-based or fixed-price contracts rather than time-and-materials arrangements signals confidence in your own capability in a way that no marketing claim can replicate.
