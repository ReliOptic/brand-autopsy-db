## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

Airbnb operates a two-sided marketplace with distinct ICPs for each side:

**Supply Side — Hosts**
- Individual homeowners with a spare room, second property, or primary residence available during travel periods
- Property managers operating multiple short-term rental units
- Boutique hotel operators seeking additional distribution channel
- Experience hosts offering activities, tours, or lessons
- Geographic concentration: major tourism destinations, urban centers, and rural/unique-stay locations globally
- (T4_INFERRED from observed host community profiles and Airbnb host resources)

**Demand Side — Guests**
- Leisure travelers seeking non-hotel accommodations for stays of 2–7 nights
- Long-term/remote workers seeking month-plus accommodations ("live and work anywhere" segment)
- Group travelers (families, friend groups) seeking whole-home inventory unavailable in traditional hotels
- Experience-seekers supplementing accommodation bookings with Airbnb Experiences
- (T4_INFERRED from Airbnb product design and public market commentary)

## Persona 1 — The Experience-First Traveler

**Name**: Priya Nair, 31, UX Designer  
**Location**: London, UK | Annual travel budget: ~£4,000 (estimated)

**Pain points**:
- Hotel rooms feel interchangeable — the same lobby, same breakfast, same floor plan
- Wants to stay in neighborhoods where locals actually live, not tourist districts
- Concerned about cancellation policies and reliability when booking non-hotel accommodations
- Group travel coordination is complex when hotel rooms require multiple separate bookings

**Gain sought**:
- A unique home with a kitchen, outdoor space, and local character
- Host recommendations for neighborhood restaurants and activities
- One booking for the entire group in a shared space
- AirCover guarantee as a safety net against host cancellations

**Engagement channels**: Airbnb app, Instagram travel accounts, word-of-mouth from friends, Google travel search (T4_INFERRED from platform analytics commentary in public earnings calls)

## Persona 2 — The Supplemental Income Host

**Name**: David Torres, 58, retired teacher  
**Location**: Lisbon, Portugal | Hosting a 2-bedroom apartment near the city center

**Pain points**:
- Fixed pension income insufficient to cover rising property costs
- Uncertainty about guest reliability and property damage risk
- Complexity of managing bookings, pricing, and guest communications
- Regulatory uncertainty about short-term rental rules in his city

**Gain sought**:
- Consistent supplemental income to supplement pension (estimated €1,500–2,500/month during peak season)
- AirCover Host protection against guest damage
- Pricing tools that optimize nightly rates automatically
- Superhost status as a trust signal attracting better guests

**Engagement channels**: Airbnb host app, Airbnb Community Center forums, host meetups, email communications from Airbnb (observed, airbnb.com/host-community)

## Persona 3 — The Remote Work Nomad

**Name**: Kenji Watanabe, 35, software engineer  
**Company**: Fully remote, U.S.-based startup | Monthly accommodation budget: ~$3,500 (estimated)

**Pain points**:
- Monthly hotel stays are cost-prohibitive and lack kitchen or work-from-home setup
- Apartment lease requirements (12-month minimum, credit check, security deposit) incompatible with nomadic lifestyle
- Inconsistent Wi-Fi quality and workspace availability across short-term rentals

**Gain sought**:
- Furnished apartments with reliable high-speed internet and dedicated workspace
- Monthly pricing discounts (Airbnb's long-stay pricing incentives)
- Ability to explore multiple cities over 12 months without long-term commitments
- "Work from anywhere" verification features (Wi-Fi speed ratings)

**Engagement channels**: Airbnb app monthly-stays filter, digital nomad community forums, LinkedIn remote work content (T4_INFERRED from Airbnb product features targeting this segment)

## AARRR Audience Journey

| Stage | Guest Action | Airbnb Mechanism |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Acquisition** | Discovers Airbnb via search, social, or word-of-mouth | SEO for destination + accommodation keywords, brand advertising, referral program |
| **Activation** | First booking completed | Streamlined checkout, AirCover trust messaging, host review system reducing hesitation |
| **Retention** | Books second and third stays | Email campaigns with personalized destination suggestions, saved wishlists, loyalty through Superhost connections |
| **Revenue** | Books premium listings, Experiences, add-ons | Upsell through Experiences marketplace, premium stays category, cleaning fee transparency |
| **Referral** | Recommends to friends or becomes a host | Referral credit program, "Become a Host" conversion from guest to host journey |

## Anti-Persona

**Who Airbnb does not primarily serve**:
- Business travelers requiring corporate billing, consistent amenity standards, and loyalty point accrual (business travel remains dominated by branded hotel chains with corporate programs)
- Travelers requiring guaranteed amenity standards (disability accessibility, specific room features) where hotel certification provides certainty
- Price-minimum seekers whose primary decision criterion is lowest total cost without experience consideration
- Travelers in markets with restrictive short-term rental regulations who cannot legally use Airbnb accommodations (T4_INFERRED from market access constraints)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Design onboarding for the side of the marketplace with higher acquisition cost.**
Airbnb's host acquisition is harder and more expensive than guest acquisition — a host listing generates repeated guest revenue. The host onboarding flow, Superhost program, AirCover protection, and host community forums are all investments in the supply side because supply quality drives demand. Transferable for two-sided marketplaces: invest disproportionately in onboarding the side that generates inventory or supply, because demand follows quality supply.

**2. Convert user behavior segments into product lines.**
Airbnb observed that a meaningful percentage of its guests were staying 28+ days, so it built long-stay pricing, monthly discount features, and workspace verification specifically for this segment — without launching a separate brand. Transferable: when usage data reveals an underserved behavior pattern within your existing user base, build a product feature for that pattern before a competitor builds a separate product targeting it.

**3. The review system is the product, not a feature.**
Airbnb's two-way review system (guests review hosts, hosts review guests) is not a nice-to-have trust signal — it is the infrastructure that makes stranger-to-stranger transactions possible at scale. Removing it would collapse the marketplace. Transferable: identify the one mechanism in your product that makes the core transaction possible under conditions of uncertainty, and treat that mechanism as the product's foundation, not a feature to be deprioritized.
