## Raison d'Etre — Why This Brand Exists

> What the world loses if this brand disappears:
>
> "The integrated oilfield services and industrial technology infrastructure that connects subsurface reservoir knowledge with surface production and emissions-reduction capability — and the 160-year institutional memory of drilling, completion, and measurement science embedded in it."

Baker Hughes is not simply an equipment supplier to the oil and gas industry. The company was formed through the 2017 merger of Baker Hughes and GE Oil & Gas, creating an entity that combines oilfield services heritage with industrial technology depth. (official, company history) As stated in the BKR FY2023 10-K: "Baker Hughes is an energy technology company that provides solutions for energy and industrial customers worldwide." (SEC 10-K FY2023) The brand's raison d'etre is to enable the global energy system — from hydrocarbon extraction to LNG liquefaction to emissions-sensing technology — during an era when that system must simultaneously meet current demand and decarbonize. (T4_ESTIMATED, based on company strategic communications)

## Brand Promise

> What the customer is guaranteed to receive after payment:

"Technology, expertise, and operational support that reduces the cost, risk, and emissions footprint of energy production and industrial processes — delivered by a company with integrated subsurface-to-surface capability and a stated commitment to the energy transition."

Three tangible outcomes are delivered:

1. **Drilling and completion reliability**: Drill bits, downhole tools, completion fluids, and wellbore integrity services that reduce non-productive time and improve well economics. (official, bakerhughes.com product pages)
2. **Industrial technology and LNG**: Turbomachinery, compression systems, and LNG technology from the GE Oil & Gas heritage, serving both upstream and downstream customers. (SEC 10-K FY2023)
3. **Energy transition capability**: Industrial and Energy Technology (IET) segment products that address methane detection, carbon capture, and hydrogen compression — differentiated from pure-play oilfield services peers. (official, BKR sustainability reports)

## Positioning Statement

> "For national oil companies, international operators, and industrial energy customers who require technology-intensive solutions across the full energy value chain, Baker Hughes is the oilfield services and industrial technology company that uniquely combines subsurface expertise with turbomachinery, LNG, and emissions-reduction capability — enabling customers to produce more efficiently today while preparing for the energy system of tomorrow."

The strategic differentiator is the IET/OFSE (Oilfield Services and Equipment) dual-segment structure. Unlike Schlumberger/SLB (pure oilfield services) and Halliburton (drilling-dominant), Baker Hughes positions itself as an energy technology company that serves both hydrocarbon production and industrial decarbonization. (T4_ESTIMATED, based on public segment disclosures and competitor 10-K filings)

## Banned Words

1. **"Guaranteed" / "Certain" (as performance absolutes)** — Downhole operations carry inherent geological uncertainty; absolute performance guarantees are operationally indefensible. (T4_ESTIMATED)
2. **"Cheap" / "Low-cost alternative"** — BKR competes on technology value, not commodity pricing. Price-leading language contradicts the IET premium positioning. (T4_ESTIMATED)
3. **"Revolutionary"** — Engineering credibility requires demonstrable proof, not rhetorical claims. BKR publishes technical papers; it does not self-proclaim revolutions. (T4_ESTIMATED, observed on bakerhughes.com)
4. **"Dominate" / "Crush"** — Inappropriate for a company selling to government-owned national oil companies and regulated utilities. (T4_ESTIMATED)
5. **"Oil company"** — BKR actively positions as an "energy technology company" in all official communications; "oil company" contradicts the IET diversification narrative. (official, SEC 10-K FY2023 self-description)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Reframe the legacy business as a transition enabler, not a liability.**
Baker Hughes operates in oil and gas — a sector under ESG pressure — yet positions its core OFSE capabilities as enablers of the energy transition (lower-emission wells, gas monetization, methane detection). The transferable structure: identify which capabilities in a "legacy" business have direct application in the successor market, and lead all communications with the transition narrative rather than defensively explaining the legacy one.

**2. Use dual-segment architecture to serve two buyer profiles simultaneously.**
The OFSE segment serves petroleum engineers and drilling operations managers. The IET segment serves industrial plant managers, LNG project developers, and sustainability officers. Baker Hughes maintains distinct technical languages, case studies, and value propositions for each — but unified under one brand umbrella. Transferable structure: when a company serves two distinct buyer personas with incompatible purchasing drivers, build segment-specific content tracks rather than averaging the message for a median audience.

**3. Publish engineering knowledge as brand infrastructure.**
Baker Hughes's technical library — white papers, reservoir simulation data, flow assurance case studies — functions as a trust-building asset with a customer base that evaluates vendors on demonstrated engineering credibility. The content is not marketing collateral; it is peer-grade technical documentation. Transferable structure: in any B2B sector where buyers have technical expertise, publishing verified data outperforms advertising spend as a trust-building mechanism.
