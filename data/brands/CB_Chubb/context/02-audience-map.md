# 02 — Audience Map
## Brand: Chubb Limited (NYSE: CB)
## Sector: Property & Casualty Insurance (Financials)

---

## 1. Audience Architecture Overview

Chubb operates across two primary market segments — Personal Lines (high-net-worth and ultra-high-net-worth individuals) and Commercial Lines (mid-market to large enterprise and multinational corporations) — plus a broker/agent distribution layer that functions as a parallel audience requiring distinct messaging.

**Audience Tiers:**
- Tier 1: End Policyholders (Personal & Commercial)
- Tier 2: Distribution Partners (Independent Agents, Brokers, MGAs)
- Tier 3: Institutional Influencers (Risk Managers, CFOs, Boards, Regulators)
- Tier 4: Capital Markets / Investors (Analysts, Institutional Shareholders)

---

## 2. Tier 1A — Personal Lines Policyholders (HNW / UHNW)

**Household Income:** $500K+ annually
**Net Worth:** $5M–$100M+
**Asset Profile:** Primary residence ($2M+), secondary/vacation properties, fine art, jewelry, wine, collector vehicles, watercraft, aircraft
**Geographic Concentration:** Coastal metros (Miami, NYC, LA, San Francisco), Sun Belt luxury markets (Scottsdale, Naples, Palm Beach), mountain resort corridors (Aspen, Jackson Hole)

**Psychographic Profile:**
- Self-identify as stewards of accumulated assets, not consumers
- Expect bespoke service, not call-center triage
- Distrust standardized policy language; demand coverage clarity
- Time-poor; delegate insurance decisions to advisors and estate attorneys
- Loss-averse in the behavioral economics sense — protecting what exists outweighs acquiring new coverage

**Decision Triggers:**
- Major asset purchase (estate, art acquisition, vessel)
- Catastrophic loss at a peer's household (word-of-mouth signal)
- Wildfire, hurricane, or flood proximity event
- Advisor/broker recommendation at wealth review

**Key Fears:**
- Carrier disputes claim after a total loss
- Policy language excludes a covered asset class they assumed was protected
- Premium increase without explanation at renewal
- Claims adjuster who undervalues bespoke property

**Content Appetite:** Long-form educational guides (wildfire mitigation, art storage), advisor-generated briefings, print materials at estate attorney and wealth management offices, minimal digital self-serve

## 3. Tier 1B — Commercial Lines Policyholders

### Sub-Segment: Mid-Market Commercial ($50M–$500M revenue)
**Industries:** Manufacturing, real estate, professional services, hospitality, food & beverage
**Key Buyer:** CFO, VP of Risk, Controller
**Coverage Needs:** Commercial property, general liability, umbrella, workers' compensation, cyber, management liability (D&O, EPLI)

**Decision Criteria:**
1. Carrier financial strength (AM Best rating)
2. Claims handling speed and certainty
3. Industry-specific underwriting expertise
4. Breadth of coverage under a single carrier relationship
5. Broker recommendation

**Content Appetite:** Risk management white papers, industry-specific case studies, CFO/risk manager peer forums, trade press placements (Risk & Insurance, Business Insurance)

### Sub-Segment: Large Enterprise / Fortune 500
**Industries:** Diversified; Chubb appetite includes energy, financial institutions, healthcare, technology
**Key Buyer:** Chief Risk Officer, Treasury, General Counsel
**Coverage Needs:** Complex layered programs, global property, specialty lines (marine, surety, political risk), captive integration, multinational coordination

**Decision Criteria:**
1. Global admitted network capability
2. Specialty underwriting depth
3. Loss control and risk engineering resources
4. Claims consistency across jurisdictions
5. Long-term relationship underwriting (not repricing every renewal)

**Content Appetite:** Direct CRO/GC engagement, Chubb-hosted executive roundtables, bespoke program modeling, peer benchmarking reports

---

## 4. Tier 2 — Distribution Partners

### Independent Agents & Brokers (Personal Lines)
**Profile:** UHNW-specialist brokers, estate planning-adjacent advisors, regional boutique agencies
**Volume:** ~60% of Chubb personal lines written premium flows through independent brokers
**Motivations:** Carrier reputation protects broker reputation; fast endorsements; agreed-value policies that eliminate client disputes; access to Chubb risk engineers for client value-add
**Key Content:** Chubb Broker Portal, Masterpiece product guides, field rep relationship, co-marketing materials for client presentations

### Wholesale Brokers & MGAs (Commercial Lines)
**Profile:** Wholesale specialists accessing Chubb's E&S and specialty appetite
**Motivations:** Chubb binding authority relationships, specialty product appetite, fast turnaround on submissions
**Key Content:** Appetite guides, underwriting appetite updates, wholesale-specific submission portals

### Retail Brokers (Mid-Market Commercial)
**Profile:** Regional and national brokers (Gallagher, Hub, Lockton, USI, regional independents)
**Motivations:** Competitive pricing on standard accounts; specialty capability for complex accounts; consistent claims advocacy
**Key Content:** Broker relationship manager touchpoints, market briefings, claims case studies, loss control resources

## 6. Tier 4 — Capital Markets / Investors

**Audience:** Institutional equity holders, sell-side analysts (insurance sector), credit rating agencies, fixed-income investors (Chubb debt issuances)

**Primary Concerns:**
- Combined ratio discipline (Chubb target: sub-90%)
- Catastrophe exposure management
- Reserve adequacy
- Geographic and line-of-business diversification
- ROE vs. peer group (AIG, Travelers, Hartford, Zurich)

**Content Channels:** Quarterly earnings calls, investor day presentations, 10-K/10-Q filings, AM Best / S&P / Moody's rating reports, sell-side initiation reports

**Voice Required:** Actuarial precision, no spin, explicit acknowledgment of catastrophe loss variability, consistent messaging on underwriting philosophy

## 10. Battle Cards

### Battle Card 1 — Winning the UHNW Broker Conversation vs. PURE Insurance
**Threat:** PURE (Privilege Underwriters Reciprocal Exchange) markets directly to UHNW policyholders, positioning its reciprocal structure as a policyholder-owned advantage.

| Dimension | Chubb | PURE |
|---|---|---|
| Financial strength | AM Best AA | AM Best A+ (excellent but below Chubb) |
| Policy form | Masterpiece (agreed-value, blanket coverage) | PURE Platinum (agreed-value, member-owned framing) |
| Broker relationship | Broker-distributed; supports broker | Primarily direct to consumer; may bypass broker |
| Claims | White-glove; dedicated adjuster | Also white-glove; smaller scale |
| Global capability | Full multinational admitted | Limited international |

**Chubb Wins When:** The broker relationship is strong and the client has international exposure (second homes abroad, yacht, art shipped internationally).
**PURE Wins When:** Client is motivated by the reciprocal/member-ownership narrative and has primarily domestic exposures.
**Counter-Message:** "PURE's member model is compelling — but Chubb's global admitted network and 150-year claims track record are not replicable by a carrier founded in 2006."

---

### Battle Card 2 — Winning Mid-Market Commercial vs. The Hartford
**Threat:** The Hartford competes aggressively in mid-market commercial with competitive pricing, a broad product suite, and strong brand awareness.

| Dimension | Chubb | The Hartford |
|---|---|---|
| Underwriting approach | Specialty-first; complex risk appetite | Broad standard commercial; volume-driven |
| Workers' comp | Available but not lead product | Market leader; Claim-forward |
| Management liability | Deep D&O / EPLI / Cyber expertise | Competitive but less specialized |
| Brand perception | Premium carrier; expertise-defined | Solid mid-market; not luxury-positioned |
| Claims advocacy | High broker satisfaction scores | Consistent but not differentiated |

**Chubb Wins When:** Account needs management liability depth (public company D&O, fiduciary) layered with commercial property.
**Hartford Wins When:** Account is workers' comp-heavy and price is the primary decision variable.
**Counter-Message:** "The Hartford built a great middle-market machine. Chubb built a great complex-risk machine. Know which problem you're solving."

---

### Battle Card 3 — Winning the CRO/Risk Manager vs. Zurich Insurance
**Threat:** Zurich competes directly with Chubb for large multinational commercial accounts, with comparable global admitted networks and risk engineering capabilities.

| Dimension | Chubb | Zurich |
|---|---|---|
| Global admitted network | 54 countries; strong Americas and Asia-Pacific | 210+ countries via Zurich/local admitted; stronger EMEA |
| Financial strength | AM Best AA | AM Best AA- |
| Underwriting philosophy | Conservative; disciplined combined ratio | More volume-oriented in certain markets |
| Risk engineering | Property loss control; industry-specific | Broad risk engineering; strong in manufacturing |
| Claims multinational coordination | Single claims contact per account | Regional claims structures |

**Chubb Wins When:** Account is Americas-anchored with Asia-Pacific growth and needs seamless US primary with coordinated international placements.
**Zurich Wins When:** Account is EMEA-headquartered and requires deeper European admitted presence.
**Counter-Message:** "Zurich's EMEA footprint is deep. Chubb's Americas-Pacific network — and the underwriting discipline behind it — is the benchmark for global accounts domiciled in the US."

## 11. Steal Sheets

### Steal Sheet 1 — The Broker-First Distribution Covenant
**What to steal from Chubb:** An explicit, enforced commitment to never compete against the broker for the end client's relationship.
**Why it works:** Independent brokers control premium placement. A carrier that competes with its distribution channel destroys the relationship that feeds its book.
**How competitors can deploy it:** Codify in agency agreements. Train field reps to support broker growth, not bypass it. Compensate reps on broker satisfaction scores, not just bound premium.
**Risk:** Carriers with direct-to-consumer ambitions cannot authentically execute this without abandoning the direct model.

---

### Steal Sheet 2 — The Risk Manager Educational Ecosystem
**What to steal from Chubb:** RIMS-level engagement through education — white papers, roundtables, risk management certifications — that makes Chubb synonymous with risk management expertise, not just coverage.
**Why it works:** Risk managers specify preferred carriers in RFPs. If Chubb educated the risk manager, the RFP often reads like a Chubb product sheet.
**How competitors can deploy it:** Fund risk management education programs. Sponsor RIMS, PRIMA, and ARM content tracks. Ghost-author white papers that go out under the risk manager's name.
**Risk:** Long sales cycle. Requires 2–3 year investment before educational influence shows up in premium.

---

### Steal Sheet 3 — Audience-Segmented Claims Metrics as Marketing
**What to steal from Chubb:** Publishing claims satisfaction scores segmented by policyholder type (UHNW personal, mid-market commercial, large enterprise) as a primary marketing asset.
**Why it works:** Claims experience is the actual product — not the policy. Publishing segmented satisfaction data makes claims quality visible at the point of sale, when the buyer cannot yet experience it.
**How competitors can deploy it:** Conduct post-claim NPS surveys. Segment results by client tier. Publish in broker-facing and buyer-facing materials. Make the data auditable.
**Risk:** Requires genuine claims excellence. Publishing mediocre data accelerates negative selection and broker disintermediation.
