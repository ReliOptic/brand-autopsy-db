## Battle Card 1: Uber vs. Lyft

**The Dynamic**: Lyft is Uber's only direct ride-hailing competitor in the United States. The rivalry has persisted since Lyft's founding in 2012. Both companies have competed intensely on driver incentives, rider promotions, and brand positioning.

**Uber Advantages**:
- Global network: Uber operates in 70+ countries; Lyft is US and Canada only (SEC 10-K FY2023; Lyft 10-K)
- Multi-product ecosystem: Uber Eats, Uber Freight, Uber for Business create cross-platform loyalty Lyft cannot replicate
- Revenue scale: ~$37.3B (Uber FY2023) vs. ~$4.4B (Lyft FY2023) (T1_OFFICIAL for Uber; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE for Lyft)
- Autonomous vehicle partnerships and investment in future mobility (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)

**Lyft Advantages**:
- Driver-friendly reputation: Lyft has historically scored higher in driver satisfaction surveys in the US (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, industry surveys)
- Simpler product focus may enable faster response to US market dynamics
- Corporate travel partnerships through Lyft Business

**Key Metric**: Uber's US ride-hailing market share is estimated at approximately 68–72% vs. Lyft's 28–32% (estimated, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, industry reports). Uber holds a commanding share position.

## Battle Card 2: Uber Eats vs. DoorDash

**The Dynamic**: In the US food delivery market, DoorDash holds the largest market share by orders, followed by Uber Eats. Both compete aggressively on restaurant partnerships, delivery speed, and subscription offerings.

**Uber Eats Advantages**:
- Global presence: Uber Eats operates in 45+ countries; DoorDash's international footprint is smaller (SEC 10-K FY2023)
- Bundled value through Uber One: subscribers get combined ride and delivery benefits, creating cross-service loyalty
- Shared driver pool with ride-hailing: courier supply can flex between rides and deliveries

**DoorDash Advantages**:
- US food delivery market share leadership (estimated approximately 65% of US orders, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- DashPass subscription with strong US subscriber base
- Deeper US restaurant and grocery partnerships (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- WooT (DoorDash's merchant-focused tools) provides more robust merchant analytics

**Key Metric**: Uber Eats generated approximately $12.1B in Delivery Bookings revenue in FY2023 (SEC 10-K FY2023; T1_OFFICIAL). US market share is approximately 23–27% by order count (estimated, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE).

## Battle Card 3: Uber vs. DiDi / Grab (International)

**The Dynamic**: Uber exited China (sold to DiDi in 2016) and Southeast Asia (sold to Grab in 2018) in exchange for equity stakes in each platform. In Latin America and other markets, Uber competes directly with DiDi-backed platforms (99 in Brazil) and Bolt.

**Uber Advantages**:
- Brand recognition among international travelers who use Uber in their home market and expect it to work abroad
- Platform consistency: same app, same UX, same payment method across 70+ countries
- Corporate travel through Uber for Business provides institutional relationships that transcend local competitors

**DiDi / Grab Advantages**:
- Deep local market knowledge and local payment method integration
- Super-app financial services (Grab's GrabPay) create switching costs beyond ride-hailing
- Local regulatory relationships in home markets

## Threats (min 3)

1. **Autonomous vehicle displacement**: Waymo (Alphabet), Tesla robotaxi, and Zoox (Amazon) represent a potential future in which the driver supply layer — Uber's primary cost and constraint — is replaced by autonomous vehicles not controlled by Uber. This could erode Uber's marketplace model. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, industry reporting; SEC 10-K FY2023 risk factors)
2. **Driver classification regulation**: Ongoing legislative and regulatory efforts in the EU, UK, and US states to reclassify gig workers as employees could materially increase Uber's cost structure. California's AB5, the UK Supreme Court ruling, and EU Platform Work Directive are the primary precedents. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, regulatory reporting; SEC 10-K FY2023)
3. **Super-app competition in growth markets**: Grab, Gojek, and similar super-apps in high-growth markets integrate ride-hailing with financial services, grocery, and healthcare — creating switching costs that pure ride-hailing cannot match. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
4. **Public transit investment**: Major cities investing in expanded subway, bus rapid transit, and bike-share systems reduce the total addressable market for urban ride-hailing. (T4_INFERRED)

---

## Opportunities (min 3)

1. **Uber One subscription growth**: Expanding the subscriber base for the bundled ride and delivery subscription drives higher frequency, higher lifetime value, and cross-product engagement. (official, Uber investor communications; SEC 10-K FY2023)
2. **Uber for Business and enterprise travel**: Post-COVID corporate travel recovery and the need for centralized travel management creates demand for Uber's business platform. 170,000+ companies are estimated to use Uber for Business. (estimated, SEC 10-K FY2023)
3. **Advertising platform (Uber Advertising)**: Uber's data on consumer location, movement, and restaurant preferences creates a unique advertising targeting capability. The Uber Advertising business, launched formally in 2022, targets $1B+ in annual revenue (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, public earnings commentary). (official, Uber investor day)
4. **Autonomous vehicle partnerships**: Rather than building AVs, Uber is positioning as the distribution platform for autonomous vehicle fleets operated by partners (Waymo partnership announced). (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, industry reporting)

## Steal Sheet

**1. Exit losing battles to maintain winning ones.**
Uber's decisions to exit China (2016) and Southeast Asia (2018) in exchange for equity stakes in DiDi and Grab were strategically disciplined: convert a money-losing market war into an equity position and redeploy capital to markets where Uber can win. Competitive strategy is as much about choosing where not to compete as about competing.

**2. Platform breadth changes the unit economics.**
A customer who uses both Uber rides and Uber Eats has higher lifetime value than one who uses only rides, but the incremental cost to serve that customer is low — the app, the payment system, and the brand are already installed. Multi-product expansion that leverages an existing customer relationship is structurally more efficient than acquiring a new customer. The platform thesis justifies the breadth.

## Competitive Pricing Analysis

Uber does not publish its pricing algorithms. Pricing observations are based on publicly available market research and journalistic reporting. `(T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, T4_INFERRED)`

### Rideshare Pricing Dynamics

| Price Factor | Uber | Lyft | Notes |
|-------------|------|------|-------|
| **Base fare** | Market-variable; city-specific | Market-variable; city-specific | Both companies adjust base fares by market; no published national rate |
| **Surge multiplier** | Dynamic; displayed pre-booking as upfront price | Dynamic; displayed as multiplier | Uber displays upfront total; Lyft historically displayed multiplier — Uber's approach reduces sticker shock `(T4_INFERRED)` |
| **Service tier pricing** | UberX (base), Comfort, Black, Black SUV | Standard, Comfort, Lux, Lux Black | Comparable tier structure; slight naming differences |
| **Cancellation fee** | Market-specific; typically $5–10 after driver en route | Market-specific | Similar policies; both enforce to reduce driver idle time |
| **Airport fees** | City-specific surcharges disclosed in fare estimate | Similar | Both subject to airport access fees passed to riders |

### Delivery Pricing Dynamics

| Fee Component | Uber Eats | DoorDash | Notes |
|--------------|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Delivery fee** | Variable; waived for Uber One members above threshold | Variable; waived for DashPass members | Both use membership to waive delivery fees as primary retention tool |
| **Service fee** | Percentage of order subtotal | Percentage of order subtotal | Both charge platform service fee; exact rates vary by market |
| **Small order fee** | Yes (below threshold) | Yes (below threshold) | Standard industry practice |
| **Restaurant commission** | Not publicly disclosed; estimated 15–30% `(estimated)` | Not publicly disclosed; estimated 15–25% `(estimated)` | Commissions are commercially negotiated and confidential |

## Competitive Response Playbook

Based on observable market behavior, Uber's competitive responses follow identifiable patterns: `(T4_INFERRED from observable market actions)`

| Competitive Threat | Uber's Typical Response |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Lyft driver incentive surge** | Match or exceed driver incentive in affected markets; accelerate Pro rewards promotion |
| **DoorDash consumer promotion** | Uber Eats counter-promotion; Uber One discount emphasis; restaurant exclusivity deals |
| **New market entrant (e.g., Bolt entering a city)** | Lower prices in that market temporarily; increase driver incentives to defend supply |
| **Regulatory threat (worker classification)** | Lobby; fund ballot initiatives (as with Prop 22); adapt product to minimize employment signals |
| **AV competitor announcement** | Announce or deepen existing AV partnership (e.g., Waymo); frame as platform-agnostic |
| **Safety incident at competitor** | Quietly publish own safety data; avoid explicit comparison; let Safety Report speak |
