## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

Uber operates a multi-sided marketplace with three primary customer groups: (1) riders / consumers, (2) drivers / delivery couriers (supply side), and (3) enterprise / business accounts. This audience map covers all three, with depth on the primary consumer persona.

| Dimension | Rider ICP | Driver ICP | Enterprise ICP |
|-----------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Geography** | Urban and suburban; 70+ countries (SEC 10-K FY2023) | Same geographies; skews toward dense urban markets | Global enterprises; 170,000+ companies use Uber for Business (estimated, SEC 10-K FY2023) |
| **Demographics** | Ages 18–55; broad income range; tech-comfortable | Ages 22–60; car owner; flexible schedule seeker | Travel managers, finance teams, HR |
| **Psychographics** | Values convenience, time efficiency, on-demand availability; avoids ownership costs | Values flexibility, supplemental income, independence | Values cost control, duty-of-care compliance, expense reporting integration |
| **Pain Point** | Unreliable or expensive traditional transport; parking hassles; alcohol and nighttime safety concerns | Need for flexible income without full-time commitment constraints | Complex employee travel expense management; duty of care for traveling employees |
| **Success State** | Car arrives in under 5 minutes, at the predicted price, without incident | Earns target income in preferred hours with minimal friction | Centralized billing, dashboard reporting, and no reimbursement paperwork |

## Persona 1 — The Urban Commuter / Convenience Rider (Primary Consumer)

**Profile**: Urban professional, age 25–45, uses Uber 3–10x per month for commuting, airport trips, and late-night travel. Does not own a car or prefers not to drive. Household income $60K–$120K (estimated).

**Pain**: Traffic, parking costs, and the unreliability of public transit at off-peak hours. Concerns about surge pricing unpredictability.

**Gain**: Predictable upfront pricing; real-time ETA; automatic payment; the ability to request a specific vehicle type (UberX, Uber Black, Uber Comfort).

**Triggers**:
- Running late for an airport departure
- Attending a venue with no nearby parking
- Leaving a social event after drinking
- Weather events that make walking or cycling impractical

**Uber Response**: Uber's product hierarchy (UberX for value, Uber Comfort for space, Uber Black for premium) serves the full price sensitivity range within this persona. Scheduled Rides address the anxiety trigger of airport departures. (official, uber.com)

## Persona 2 — The Uber Eats Power User (Delivery Consumer)

**Profile**: Urban or suburban adult, age 20–50, orders food delivery 4–8x per month. May overlap heavily with Rider persona (same app). Values restaurant variety, delivery speed, and in-app order tracking.

**Pain**: Limited delivery options from individual restaurant apps; inconsistent delivery time estimates; food quality degradation on long deliveries.

**Gain**: 800,000+ restaurant partners globally (estimated, Uber Eats investor communications); real-time GPS delivery tracking; Uber One membership for delivery fee savings. (official, uber.com/ubereats)

**Triggers**:
- Working late with no time to cook
- Weekend entertainment and meal delivery combined
- Discovery of a new restaurant not available through other platforms

**Uber Response**: Uber Eats is the second-largest food delivery platform in many markets (estimated, T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE). The Uber One subscription bundles ride and delivery savings, creating cross-product loyalty. (official, uber.com)

---

## Persona 3 — The Part-Time Driver (Supply Side)

**Profile**: Adult with a qualifying vehicle, age 25–55, seeking flexible supplemental income. May be a full-time worker using Uber to supplement income, or semi-retired. Values the ability to set their own hours.

**Pain**: Needs income but cannot commit to fixed shift schedules; wants to maximize earnings per hour; frustrated by algorithm opacity in trip dispatch.

**Gain**: Flexible schedule — drive when and where they choose; upfront trip information (in markets where offered); earnings tracking dashboard; potential access to Uber Pro rewards at higher ratings. (official, Uber driver communications)

**Triggers**:
- Gap between fixed expenses and income
- Life event requiring additional income (new child, medical expense)
- Seeing peer referral for driver sign-up bonus

**Uber Response**: Driver recruitment uses earnings-focused messaging ("Earn on your schedule"). Uber Pro program offers benefits at higher rating and acceptance rate thresholds. (official, uber.com/drive)

## AARRR Purchase Journey (Rider)

| Stage | Trigger | Uber Touchpoint |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| **Acquisition** | Peer recommendation; first trip need in a new city; referral code | App download; referral discount for first ride; airport arrival signage |
| **Activation** | First ride requested and completed without issue | In-app ETA countdown; upfront fare display; automatic payment confirmation |
| **Retention** | Next transport need within days or weeks | Push notification ("Your usual route is busy — try scheduling your ride"); Uber One subscription offer |
| **Referral** | Positive experience + incentive | "Give $10, Get $10" referral program; natural word of mouth |
| **Revenue** | Increased frequency; upgrade to premium tier; Uber Eats cross-sell | Uber One; Uber Black; Uber Eats first order promo |

## Anti-Persona

**The Privacy-Focused Non-Smartphone User / Car Owner in a Low-Density Suburb**: Adults who do not own smartphones, are uncomfortable with app-based payment, live in areas with low driver supply density, or have strong privacy objections to GPS-tracked movement. Uber's product and value proposition does not serve this profile, and the low driver density in suburban and rural areas means the product cannot deliver on its core promise in those geographies. (T4_INFERRED)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Audience Principles

**1. The two-sided marketplace requires two audience strategies.**
Uber's rider growth strategy and driver growth strategy are distinct products with distinct messaging, distinct incentives, and distinct brand registers. When supply drops, riders suffer. Investing in driver acquisition and retention is as important as rider acquisition — arguably more so, since supply-side quality determines demand-side satisfaction.

**2. The subscription model converts occasional users into habitual ones.**
Uber One bundles ride and delivery discounts into a monthly subscription, converting Uber from a transaction into a habit. For any marketplace with multi-product capability, a subscription tier that rewards cross-product use is the highest-leverage retention tool available.

**3. Cross-sell within the session, not after it.**
Uber's most effective Eats cross-sell happens at the end of a ride — "Your ride is over. Order dinner while you're still in the app." Session-based cross-sell has higher conversion than re-engagement campaigns because intent is still active. Transferable: design cross-sell moments to appear at the natural conclusion of one completed need, not in a separate email three days later.

## Audience Segmentation by Frequency

Platform consumers differ substantially in usage frequency, and frequency determines monetization potential. Uber does not disclose frequency distribution publicly; estimates are based on platform economics literature and observable product design signals. `(estimated, T4_INFERRED)`

| Frequency Tier | Estimated % of MAPCs | Trips/Month | Revenue Contribution | Uber Strategy |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Power users** | ~10–15% | 10+ | Disproportionately high (~40% of revenue) `(estimated)` | Uber One subscription; premium tier upsell; Eats cross-sell |
| **Regular users** | ~25–35% | 3–9 | Core revenue base | Frequency reinforcement; loyalty rewards |
| **Occasional users** | ~40–50% | 1–2 | Lower per-user; important for scale | Re-engagement; promotional nudges |
| **Dormant (reactivation)** | ~10–20% | <1 | Minimal; reactivation opportunity | Discount-driven reactivation campaigns |

Power users are the highest priority retention target. Losing a power user to Lyft (rides) or DoorDash (delivery) costs approximately 5–8x the revenue impact of losing an occasional user. `(estimated, T4_INFERRED from frequency/revenue distribution logic)`

## Audience by Product Line — Overlap Analysis

| User Group | Uber Rides | Uber Eats | Both | Neither |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|------|---------|
| **Urban 18–35** | High | High | High overlap — same person, same app | Low |
| **Suburban 35–55** | Moderate | High | Moderate overlap | Moderate |
| **Business traveler** | Very high | Moderate | Moderate | Low |
| **Driver/courier** | Supply side | Supply side | Flex between both | — |
| **Enterprise admin** | Yes (Uber for Business) | Yes (Eats for Business) | Usually both | Low |

The overlap between ride and delivery users within the same app is Uber's structural advantage over single-product competitors. A user who opens Uber for a ride and sees Uber Eats promoted is already in-session with brand trust established. The incremental conversion cost is dramatically lower than acquiring a net-new DoorDash user. `(T4_INFERRED)`

## Geographic Audience Profile — Key Markets

| Market | Primary Use Case | Competitive Intensity | Key Audience Nuance |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **United States** | Rides + Food delivery | High (Lyft + DoorDash) | Suburban airport trips; urban commuter replacement; college campus density |

Careem note: Uber acquired Careem (a Middle East rideshare leader) in 2020 for approximately $3.1B. Careem operates as a separate brand within the Uber ecosystem in the MENA region. (T1_OFFICIAL, 10-K disclosures; 8-K acquisition filing)

## Uber One — Audience Within Audience

The Uber One membership program creates a high-value audience segment within the broader MAPC base:

| Dimension | Uber One Member | Non-Member MAPC |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Usage frequency** | Higher — membership benefits incentivize more trips and orders | Lower — transactional usage |
| **Cross-product usage** | Very high — members use both rides and Eats to maximize benefits | Moderate — may use only one product |
| **Churn risk** | Lower — switching requires giving up subscription benefits | Higher — will switch for promotions |
| **Revenue per user** | Significantly higher `(estimated)` | Lower |
| **Brand affinity** | High — membership creates investment in Uber ecosystem | Moderate |

Uber One pricing as publicly listed: $9.99/month or $99.99/year. (T2_PRIMARY_RELIABLE, observed on uber.com) Exact membership count disclosed as "tens of millions" in FY2024 communications. (T1_OFFICIAL)
