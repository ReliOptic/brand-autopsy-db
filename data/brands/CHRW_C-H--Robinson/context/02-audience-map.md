| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Primary Audience** | Mid-to-large shippers (manufacturers, retailers, food and beverage companies) with complex domestic freight needs across truckload and LTL modes in North America |

## Audience Tiers

### Tier 1 — Shipper (Core Revenue Customer)

Supply chain managers, logistics directors, and VP-level operations executives at companies moving freight regularly across North America. As of the 2023 10-K, CHRW served approximately 100,000 customers globally. (official, CHRW 2023 10-K) The customer base spans retail, manufacturing, food and beverage, consumer products, and industrial sectors.

**Core needs**: Consistent carrier capacity in both tight and loose market conditions; competitive freight pricing derived from network scale; real-time shipment visibility; a single point of contact for multi-modal freight; technology integration with internal ERP and TMS systems.

**Pain points**: Carrier capacity shortages during peak seasons (Q4, produce season); spot rate volatility; manual carrier sourcing and tendering processes; lack of in-transit visibility; managing dozens of direct carrier relationships without dedicated logistics staff.

**Triggers for CHRW engagement**: Capacity failure during peak season when direct carrier relationships are insufficient; cost reduction initiative requiring freight spend benchmarking; technology modernization project requiring TMS integration; entry into new geographic lanes without existing carrier relationships.

---

### Tier 2 — Carrier Partner

Small-to-midsize trucking companies — owner-operators through regional fleets of 20–500 trucks — seeking consistent load access without sales overhead. CHRW's 85,000+ contracted carriers represent the largest contracted carrier network of any North American freight broker. (official, CHRW 2023 Annual Report)

**Core needs**: Consistent load availability to minimize empty miles; competitive rates; fast payment terms; minimal administrative burden for load acceptance and documentation.

**Pain points**: Finding consistent backhaul freight; managing payment cycles with direct shipper customers; owner-operators seeking supplementary loads beyond dedicated routes; fuel cost volatility requiring rate adjustment.

**Triggers for CHRW engagement**: Empty backhaul leg requiring load fill; entry into a new lane or geography; capacity in off-peak periods; need for quick-pay option to improve cash flow.

---

### Tier 3 — Enterprise / Managed Services Customer

Fortune 500 supply chain executives evaluating full or partial logistics outsourcing. CHRW's Managed Services offering provides dedicated supply chain management including carrier procurement, TMS operation, and reporting. This segment involves multi-year contractual relationships and higher revenue per customer than transactional brokerage.

**Core needs**: Operational scale without headcount growth; TMS infrastructure without internal IT capital investment; market intelligence on freight pricing trends; performance reporting and carrier accountability.

**Pain points**: Internal logistics team constraints; TMS capital investment requirements; difficulty benchmarking freight rates without market-level data; board-level pressure to reduce logistics cost as a percentage of revenue.

## Persona 1 — The Retail Supply Chain Manager

**Profile**: Marcus T., 41 — Senior Manager, Inbound Logistics, national big-box retailer. Manages 15,000+ annual truckload and LTL shipments from vendor to DC network.

**Pain**: Carrier rejection rates spike in Q4; manual tendering consumes staff time; inability to benchmark contracted rates against spot market creates cost uncertainty at budget time.

**Gain**: Automated tendering through Navisphere integration with his existing TMS; carrier fallback waterfall that fills capacity when primary carriers reject; quarterly benchmarking report demonstrating cost savings versus spot market.

**Trigger**: Q4 carrier fallout leaves 200 loads untendered during peak week; CHRW closes the capacity gap within 24 hours using spot market carrier access.

**CHRW touchpoints**: Dedicated account manager; Navisphere portal; market rate reports; Q4 capacity planning call in September.

## Persona 2 — The Owner-Operator Carrier

**Profile**: Sandra R., 52 — Owner-operator, single dry-van truck, Nashville-based. Runs primarily Midwest-to-Southeast lanes with occasional backhaul availability.

**Pain**: Finding consistent backhaul loads from Southeast to Midwest; payment terms on direct shipper accounts run 45–60 days; administrative burden of shipper compliance documentation varies by customer.

**Gain**: Quick-pay options through CHRW's carrier payment programs; consistent load availability on lanes she already runs; transparent rate quotes accessible online without phone calls.

**Trigger**: Empty backhaul creates an unprofitable week; CHRW load board shows posted rate on Nashville-to-Chicago lane at competitive rate.

**CHRW touchpoints**: Carrier load board (chrobinson.com/carriers); carrier mobile app; CHRW carrier support line.

## Persona 3 — The VP of Supply Chain (Enterprise)

**CHRW touchpoints**: CHRW Managed Services team; executive business reviews; Navisphere enterprise integration; annual performance reporting package.

CHRW does not effectively serve: individual consumers shipping personal items (parcel and courier); very small businesses with fewer than 10 annual shipments; shippers requiring dedicated fleet assets with guaranteed equipment availability (requiring asset-based carriers); and hyperlocal last-mile delivery operations where asset density determines service quality. ## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Serve both sides of the marketplace with distinct value propositions.**
CHRW's audience map is genuinely two-sided: shippers receive network access and pricing leverage; carriers receive consistent load access and reduced sales overhead. Most platforms favor one side; CHRW invests in both because carrier network depth is what makes the shipper value proposition possible. Transferable structure: for any marketplace business, map the value proposition to each supply-side and demand-side participant separately and invest in both.

**2. Seasonal pain points are the highest-value acquisition moments.**
CHRW's strongest acquisition story is Q4 capacity: when a shipper's direct carrier relationships fail during peak season, CHRW's scale solves the crisis that no single carrier can address. Being present at the moment of maximum pain — not general interest — creates lasting customer relationships. Transferable structure: identify the one or two moments in your customers' annual cycle where failure without your help is most likely, and build acquisition strategy around those specific moments.

**3. Technology integration creates retention that pricing cannot dissolve.**
Once a shipper integrates Navisphere into their TMS workflow, the switching cost becomes operational disruption, not just price comparison. CHRW's technology investment serves a retention function as much as a service function. Transferable structure: design your product to integrate with the customer's operational workflow — the deeper the integration, the higher the switching cost and the more durable the relationship.
