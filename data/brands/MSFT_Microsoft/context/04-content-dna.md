## Hook Patterns

1. **The capability announcement hook**: "Introducing [product name] — now available in Microsoft 365." — Leads with the product name and availability date. Enterprise IT readers scan for actionable news, not narrative. (observed in Microsoft 365 blog post patterns)

2. **The customer outcome hook**: "How [Company X] reduced IT ticket volume by 40% using Microsoft Copilot." — Customer story leads with a specific, quantified business outcome before describing the product. This is the primary structure for Microsoft case study content. (observed on microsoft.com/customers)

3. **The CEO thought leadership hook**: "The next generation of AI is not about replacing people — it's about amplifying human capability." — Satya Nadella's public communications frequently open with a philosophical reframing of the AI narrative before introducing Microsoft's position. (observed in Satya Nadella LinkedIn posts and keynote openings)

4. **The developer tutorial hook**: "In this tutorial, you'll build a [specific outcome] using [specific Azure service] in under 30 minutes." — Developer content leads with the concrete outcome the reader will achieve, not with the product's features. (observed on learn.microsoft.com)

5. **The security threat hook**: "A new credential-based attack technique is targeting enterprise Active Directory environments. Here's what security teams need to know." — Microsoft Security blog leads with the threat scenario before presenting Microsoft's detection or mitigation capability. (observed on microsoft.com/security/blog)

## CTA Patterns

1. **"Start free"** — Azure free account, GitHub free tier, Microsoft 365 trial. Primary acquisition CTA for developer and SMB audiences. (observed on azure.microsoft.com, github.com)
2. **"Talk to an expert"** — Enterprise sales contact CTA. Appears on enterprise product pages where self-service purchase is not appropriate. (observed on microsoft.com/enterprise)
3. **"Read the case study"** — Customer outcome content drives decision-maker credibility. Present on product pages alongside capability descriptions. (observed on microsoft.com)
4. **"Watch the demo"** — Video demonstration CTA for product pages where visual understanding of the product is necessary. Common for Teams, Copilot, and Azure services. (observed on microsoft.com)
5. **"Join the community"** — Microsoft Tech Community and GitHub community CTAs for developer and IT professional audiences. Community membership drives retention and advocacy. (observed on techcommunity.microsoft.com)

## Tone & Mood Guide

| Situation | Tone | Mood | Example |
|-----------|------|------|---------|
| **Product launch** | Energetic, specific, outcome-focused | Excited, credible | "Today, we're announcing Microsoft 365 Copilot is generally available for enterprise customers." — Date-anchored, outcome-specific, no vague promise language. (official, Microsoft 365 Copilot GA announcement, November 2023) |
| **Developer documentation** | Technical, precise, example-driven | Helpful, neutral | "The following code sample demonstrates how to authenticate using Azure Active Directory using the MSAL library." — Code-first, no marketing language. (observed on learn.microsoft.com) |
| **Executive thought leadership** | Reflective, philosophical, forward-looking | Thoughtful, empowering | Satya Nadella's shareholder letters use "growth mindset" framing and acknowledge uncertainty honestly before presenting Microsoft's strategic direction. (official, Microsoft Annual Report FY2023, Satya Nadella letter) |
| **Security incident or vulnerability disclosure** | Formal, precise, safety-first | Serious, transparent | Microsoft Security Response Center disclosures follow a structured format: CVE identifier, severity rating, affected products, mitigation steps. No alarm language, no minimization. (official, MSRC advisory format, microsoft.com/msrc) |

## Content Prohibitions

1. **Unqualified AI capability claims**: Stating that Copilot or any Microsoft AI product "will" produce specific outcomes without qualification creates advertising claim liability. Microsoft's AI communications include capability descriptions with demonstrations, not guaranteed outcomes. (T4_INFERRED from observed communications and FTC AI advertising guidelines)

2. **Uptime or availability guarantees without SLA specificity**: Microsoft's Azure SLAs are contractually specific (e.g., 99.9% for Azure Virtual Machines with two or more instances). Content cannot state "always-on" or "guaranteed availability" without the specific SLA parameters. (official, Azure SLA documentation)

3. **Competitive feature comparisons without verification**: Stating that Microsoft 365 "does more" than Google Workspace requires specific, verifiable feature comparisons. Vague superiority claims introduce advertising standards liability. (T4_INFERRED from advertising claim standards)

4. **Privacy overclaiming**: Statements such as "Microsoft never uses your data" are not accurate to the complexity of Microsoft's data processing across products and jurisdictions. Microsoft's privacy communications reference specific data use policies per product. (T4_INFERRED from Microsoft Privacy Statement complexity)

5. **Executive attribution of forward guidance**: Quoting an executive making specific forward-looking financial or capability predictions in content channels outside of SEC-compliant disclosure contexts creates Regulation FD risk. Executive commentary on product capability is distinct from financial guidance; both require appropriate qualification. (T4_INFERRED from SEC Regulation FD requirements)

6. **Describing competitors' products as insecure without independent verification**: Implicit or explicit claims that competitor platforms are less secure than Microsoft products require independently verifiable evidence. Microsoft's security marketing references third-party analyst reports and certifications, not unsubstantiated security comparisons. (T4_INFERRED from advertising claim standards)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Customer outcome stories as the primary enterprise sales asset.**
Microsoft's customer case study library on microsoft.com/customers is among the most extensive in enterprise software. Every case study is structured identically: customer industry and size, challenge, Microsoft solution deployed, specific quantified outcome. This format allows enterprise IT buyers to self-qualify: "Is my situation similar to [company]? Did they get a result I care about?" Transferable structure: build a case study library organized by industry, company size, and outcome type — not by product. The buyer is searching for their problem, not your solution.

**2. Technical education as long-term brand investment.**
Microsoft Learn (learn.microsoft.com) provides free, vendor-neutral-feeling technical training on Azure, Microsoft 365, and security topics. Engineers who learn Azure through Microsoft Learn become certified and then advocate for Azure in their organizations. Transferable structure: identify the technical skills your target audience needs to use your product effectively and build free, high-quality educational content around those skills — not product documentation. The educational content creates a permanent inbound pipeline of technically qualified users.

**3. Conference content as a year-round content engine.**
Microsoft Build (developers) and Microsoft Ignite (IT professionals) each generate hundreds of hours of session recordings, announcement blog posts, and breakout content that is reused and referenced for the 12 months between conferences. A single conference creates a content library that sustains the editorial calendar for a year. Transferable structure: design your annual events as content production events, not just attendance events. Every session should be recorded, indexed, and made searchable — transforming a one-time event into a permanent reference library.
