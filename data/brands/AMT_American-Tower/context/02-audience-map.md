## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

American Tower is a pure B2B company. It has no consumer customers. Its tenant base consists of wireless network operators, broadcasters, and government or public safety agencies that lease space on tower structures to mount communications equipment.

| Dimension | Profile |
|-----------|---------|
| **Primary customer type** | Wireless telecommunications carriers (mobile network operators) requiring passive infrastructure for RAN (Radio Access Network) equipment deployment |
| **Secondary customer type** | Broadcast companies requiring high-elevation antenna mounts; government and public safety agencies; emerging private wireless network operators |
| **Decision-maker profile** | Network planning engineers, real estate and infrastructure procurement teams, and technology acquisition officers within large wireless carriers |
| **Geographic scope** | United States (largest revenue market), Brazil, Germany, France, Spain, Nigeria, South Africa, Kenya, and other international markets (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, segment disclosures) |
| **Contract behavior** | Long-term lease relationships governed by Master Lease Agreements (MLAs); typical initial terms of 5–10 years with multiple renewal options; annual escalators typically 3% or CPI-linked (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024) |
| **Price sensitivity** | Moderate — carriers are price-aware but value site location, structural capacity, and permitting certainty over marginal cost savings. Switching costs are high once equipment is installed. (T4_INFERRED) |
| **Investment trigger** | Network capacity expansion (4G densification, 5G rollout), spectrum license deployment obligations, coverage gap closure, competitor network parity requirements (T4_INFERRED from publicly disclosed carrier investment plans) |

## Core Personas

### Persona 1 — Network Infrastructure Director at a Major U.S. Carrier

- **Role**: Senior network infrastructure director at one of the three major U.S. wireless carriers (AT&T, T-Mobile, Verizon — public companies whose tower-lease relationships with AMT are disclosed in SEC filings as material customer relationships)
- **Primary objective**: Deploy 5G mid-band and millimeter-wave spectrum across metropolitan and suburban markets on schedule and within capital budget
- **Pain point**: Permitting delays for new tower construction; zoning restrictions on new structures in dense urban markets; capital constraints that favor leasing over building
- **AMT selection rationale**: Access to an existing portfolio of pre-permitted, structurally certified sites with available co-location capacity. Leasing from AMT avoids the 2–5 year permitting and construction timeline for new tower builds. (T4_INFERRED from publicly observable carrier and tower industry dynamics)
- **Relationship with AMT**: Multi-year MLA governs all domestic site relationships. The three major U.S. carriers collectively represent a substantial portion of AMT's U.S. domestic revenue, as disclosed in AMT's 10-K (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, customer concentration risk disclosure)
- **Tension**: Carrier consolidation reduces the number of independent tenants and increases buyer bargaining power. AMT's 10-K identifies customer concentration as a material risk. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, Risk Factors)

---

### Persona 2 — International MNO Infrastructure Manager (Latin America / Africa)

- **Role**: Infrastructure acquisition manager at a regional mobile network operator in Brazil, Mexico, Nigeria, or South Africa
- **Primary objective**: Expand 4G coverage into peri-urban and rural markets under regulatory coverage obligations; begin 5G deployment in major cities
- **Pain point**: High cost of tower construction in geographically dispersed markets; limited access to capital; regulatory pressure to extend coverage to underserved populations
- **AMT selection rationale**: AMT's established local tower portfolios in these markets provide immediate access to existing infrastructure without requiring greenfield development capital. (T4_INFERRED from observed AMT international market presence)
- **Relationship with AMT**: Lease agreements governed by local subsidiary contracts; currency risk and regulatory environment are disclosed as material risks in AMT's 10-K (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, international operations risk)
- **Tension**: Currency devaluation in markets such as Brazil and Nigeria can reduce the USD-equivalent value of lease revenue, which AMT discusses in its 10-K disclosures (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, foreign currency risk)

---

### Persona 3 — Institutional REIT Investor / REIT Analyst

- **Role**: Portfolio manager at a real estate investment fund or REIT-specialist asset manager; or sell-side equity analyst covering the tower REIT sector
- **Primary objective**: Assess AMT's dividend sustainability, FFO (Funds From Operations) growth trajectory, leverage management, and long-term contracted revenue visibility
- **Pain point**: Macro interest rate sensitivity — as a REIT, AMT's valuation is sensitive to interest rate movements, which affect both discount rates and refinancing costs. Rising rates in 2022–2023 compressed REIT valuations broadly. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, publicly reported REIT sector dynamics)
- **AMT selection rationale as investment**: Long-duration contracted cash flows with built-in escalators; asset-light incremental revenue model; global diversification as a risk offset (T4_INFERRED from observed AMT investor presentation content)
- **Key metrics tracked**: AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations) per share; tenant revenue growth; organic tenant billings growth; leverage ratio (net debt / EBITDA); dividend coverage and growth trajectory (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT investor relations materials and earnings call transcripts)
- **Tension**: India operations sale (ATC India to Brookfield Asset Management) removed a large international market from the portfolio. Investors tracking international growth narratives need to recalibrate post-India. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, public reporting on ATC India transaction)

## Customer Concentration Risk

American Tower's 10-K explicitly identifies customer concentration as a material business risk. The three major U.S. wireless carriers — AT&T, T-Mobile, and Verizon — collectively represent a significant portion of AMT's total domestic revenue (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, Risk Factors and Note disclosures on major customers). This concentration means that network investment decisions, lease renegotiations, or consolidation activity by any of these carriers can have a material effect on AMT's financial results — a dynamic that distinguishes tower REITs from more diversified real estate asset classes.

## Non-Audience Clarification

The following groups are NOT American Tower's audience and should not be addressed in AMT brand or content strategy:

- Retail consumers (individuals purchasing wireless service — they interact with carriers, not tower companies)
- Small businesses purchasing connectivity (same as above — AMT has no retail channel)
- Homeowners adjacent to tower sites (AMT's relationship with local communities is a regulatory and ESG matter, not a brand communications matter)

This B2B exclusivity is a defining characteristic of the tower REIT business model and should be reflected in all analysis and content creation for this brand. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, Business Description)

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Map your customer base by decision-making role, not just by company type.**
AMT's carrier tenants are large organizations, but the actual leasing decision is made by network planning engineers and infrastructure procurement teams — not by CMOs or CEOs. Transferable discipline: identify the specific job function that controls vendor selection in your target accounts. Build content and sales materials for that person's evaluation criteria, not for the company's general profile.

**2. Investor relations is a customer acquisition channel.**
AMT's institutional investor communications function partly as a signal to carrier tenants: a well-capitalized, investment-grade company is a more reliable long-term infrastructure partner than a financially distressed one. Transferable principle: for any B2B company, strong financial communications create indirect trust signals with customers who need vendor stability assurance.

**3. Design onboarding for switching-cost accumulation.**
A carrier that installs antennas on an AMT tower, integrates its network management systems with AMT's site data, and trains its field teams on AMT's processes has accumulated substantial switching costs. Transferable structure: identify every integration point in your customer onboarding process and ensure each one adds measurable operational value — making the switching cost visible as accumulated benefit rather than as lock-in.
