## 2. IP Portfolio

### 2.1 Trademark Portfolio (T1_OFFICIAL, USPTO)

American Tower maintains trademark registrations in the United States and internationally for its corporate identity and business-related marks. The following marks are publicly searchable via USPTO TESS:

| Mark | Class(es) | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| AMERICAN TOWER | IC 36 (real estate services), IC 37 (construction services) | Core house mark for the tower REIT business |
| AMERICAN TOWER CORPORATION | IC 36, 37 | Full legal entity name mark |
| CORESITE | IC 38 (telecommunications services), IC 42 (data center services) | Mark for the data center subsidiary |
| CORESITE REALTY | IC 36 | Data center REIT identity mark |

American Tower's trademark portfolio is modest compared to consumer technology companies, reflecting the B2B nature of its business. AMT's brand protection concerns center on corporate identity, not on consumer-facing marks. (T4_INFERRED from observable USPTO records)

### 2.2 Patent Portfolio

American Tower's patent portfolio is limited relative to technology companies. Tower infrastructure involves physical structures, ground leases, and telecommunications equipment — equipment that AMT does not manufacture. The primary IP considerations for AMT are:

- **Proprietary site management software and systems** — internal tools for managing global tower portfolio operations (not publicly disclosed in detail; T4_INFERRED)
- **Ground lease negotiation methodology** — not patentable but represents accumulated institutional knowledge
- **Tower structural engineering specifications** — technical expertise accumulated over decades of tower construction

AMT is not a technology developer in the traditional IP sense. Its competitive moat is physical asset ownership, geographic coverage, and operational expertise — not patent-protected technology. (T4_INFERRED)

### 2.3 Real Property Rights

AMT's primary competitive asset is its portfolio of real property rights: ground leases, tower ownership documents, and permitting records. These constitute the primary barrier to competitive entry that protects AMT's business model. The terms of ground leases are disclosed at a general level in AMT's 10-K; specific lease terms are confidential commercial agreements. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, ground lease disclosure)

## 3. Litigation and Regulatory Proceedings

> All descriptions below are factual summaries of publicly disclosed legal proceedings.
> This document does not characterize any party's conduct beyond what is stated in official
> filings, court orders, or regulatory agency press releases.

### 3.1 General Litigation Profile

American Tower is a party to various legal proceedings in the ordinary course of its business, as disclosed in its 10-K, Part I, Item 3. These include:

- **Ground lease disputes**: Disputes with ground lease landlords over lease terms, renewal rights, or payment obligations. AMT's 10-K notes these occur in the ordinary course of business. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, Legal Proceedings)
- **Tenant disputes**: Disputes with carrier tenants over lease terms, co-location fees, or structural modification rights. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024)
- **Construction and permitting disputes**: Local government or community challenges to tower construction permits. These are addressed through administrative and judicial processes. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024, regulatory risk)
- **International regulatory proceedings**: In certain international markets, regulatory bodies have reviewed tower market competition and lease practices. AMT's 10-K discloses material international regulatory matters. (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024)

Investors and analysts should consult the most recent AMT 10-K, Part I, Item 3 for the current list of material legal proceedings, as this section is updated with each annual filing.

### 3.2 U.S. Tower Siting and Permitting Regulatory Framework

American Tower's U.S. tower construction and modification activities operate within a federal and local regulatory framework:

- **Section 6409(a) of the Spectrum Act (2012)**: Requires state and local governments to approve certain modifications to existing wireless infrastructure without subjecting applicants to local zoning requirements. This provision has been beneficial to tower operators including AMT in accelerating 5G equipment installation on existing towers. (T1_OFFICIAL, public law; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, telecommunications regulatory reporting)
- **NEPA / Section 106 (National Historic Preservation Act)**: New tower construction requires environmental and historic preservation review, administered through the FCC's Tower Construction Notification System. (T1_OFFICIAL, FCC regulations)
- **FCC RF Emission Standards**: All AMT towers and co-located equipment must comply with FCC radio frequency emission exposure limits. (T1_OFFICIAL, FCC regulations)
- **FAA Obstruction Standards**: Towers above certain heights require FAA lighting and marking requirements. (T4_INFERRED from standard tower operator obligations)

### 3.3 International Regulatory Considerations

AMT operates in approximately 40 countries, each with its own regulatory framework for tower construction, spectrum licensing, and telecommunications infrastructure. Key international regulatory considerations disclosed in AMT's 10-K include (T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY2024):

- **Local permitting requirements**: Vary significantly by country and municipality
- **Foreign ownership restrictions**: Some markets restrict foreign ownership of telecommunications infrastructure; AMT has used joint venture and local subsidiary structures
- **Currency controls and repatriation**: Certain markets impose restrictions on the ability to repatriate profits
- **Expropriation risk**: AMT's 10-K identifies expropriation of assets by foreign governments as a disclosed risk factor in certain international markets

### 5.4 Writing Risk Guide — American Tower Corporation

> This is the most operationally critical section for day-to-day content creation.
> Follow these guidelines for every sentence written about AMT in this project.

#### SAFE TO WRITE (with proper sourcing)

- Revenue, AFFO, and balance sheet figures from AMT's SEC filings, cited as "(T1_OFFICIAL, AMT 10-K FY20XX)"
- Site count and geographic footprint descriptions based on AMT's investor materials, cited as "(observed, AMT IR materials)"
- Descriptions of AMT's business model (multitenant tower leasing, REIT structure, MLA characteristics) based on 10-K
- Summaries of disclosed legal proceedings citing AMT's 10-K Part I Item 3 and relevant regulatory sources
- Descriptions of AMT's tenant relationships (without disclosing confidential MLA terms not in public filings)
- Competitive comparisons using observable, publicly reported metrics (site count, geographic scope, revenue)
- Credit ratings from Moody's, S&P, Fitch citing agency publications
- ESG disclosures based on AMT's published ESG/Sustainability Report

#### REQUIRES CAREFUL HEDGING

| Topic | Required Hedging Approach |
|-------|--------------------------|
| AMT's strategic intent | "AMT's 10-K states..." or "Management indicated on the earnings call..." — NEVER "AMT's strategy is to..." as a bare assertion |
| AFFO estimates | Always cite AMT's own disclosed guidance or earnings releases; tag estimates as "(estimated)" with derivation method |
| Carrier MLA terms | General terms disclosed in 10-K are citable; specific confidential terms are not available and must not be speculated |
| Market share assertions | Use observable data (site count comparisons) rather than asserting percentage market share without a sourced study |
| International regulatory outcomes | "Proceedings are ongoing" — never predict outcomes of pending regulatory matters |
| India transaction impact | State what is disclosed in 8-K and 10-K filings; do not speculate on strategic rationale beyond what management has publicly stated |
| Interest rate / valuation commentary | Use published analyst estimates with attribution; tag as (estimated) or T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE |

#### DO NOT WRITE (High Legal / Project Integrity Risk)

- Any assertion that AMT engaged in anticompetitive conduct without direct citation from a regulatory final order or court judgment using those specific terms
- Assertions about confidential MLA pricing or lease terms not disclosed in public filings
- Claiming knowledge of AMT's internal deliberations, board discussions, or executive strategy not disclosed in public filings
- Asserting that any ongoing legal or regulatory proceeding has a predetermined outcome
- Making claims about AMT's conduct in international markets based solely on unverified media reports
- Reproducing substantial portions of AMT's investor presentation content verbatim
- Publishing or referencing non-public information about carrier relationships or specific site characteristics
- Asserting AMT has a "monopoly" in any market as a bare assertion without citing a regulatory finding that uses that characterization

American Tower publishes an annual ESG/Sustainability Report (observed, AMT corporate website). Publicly stated commitments as of recent reports include:

- Reduction of Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions per tower site
- Renewable energy procurement programs in domestic and international markets
- Energy efficiency improvements in tower power systems (solar-hybrid solutions in Africa and emerging markets)

These are AMT's stated commitments and reported initiatives. Independent third-party verification of specific ESG claims varies by disclosure type. All ESG content about AMT should cite AMT's official ESG report and label interpretive conclusions as T4_INFERRED. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, global ESG disclosure regulatory trends)

- Independent board majority, as disclosed in DEF 14A
