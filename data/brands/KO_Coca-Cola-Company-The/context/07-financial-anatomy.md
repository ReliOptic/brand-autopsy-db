### 2.3 Geographic Revenue Context

The company operates in more than 200 countries and territories as disclosed in its 10-K. Significant international revenue creates material foreign currency exposure, addressed under risk factors. (T1_OFFICIAL)

## 7. Financial Risk Matrix

### Based on SEC 10-K Risk Factors (FY2025, Item 1A)

| Risk Category | Key Content | Brand Strategy Impact |
|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Macroeconomic & Geopolitical | Unfavorable economic conditions, inflation, interest rate changes, and geopolitical instability could reduce consumer spending and demand. (T1_OFFICIAL) | Pressure on pricing power and volume in discretionary-adjacent categories; brand premium may face elasticity testing during downturns. |
| Foreign Currency Exposure | Significant international operations create material exposure to exchange rate fluctuations that can affect reported revenues and earnings. (T1_OFFICIAL) | Geographic revenue mix strategy must balance growth-market expansion (higher FX risk) against margin stability. |
| Competition & Consumer Preferences | The beverage industry is highly competitive; changing consumer health preferences toward lower-sugar and functional beverages could reduce demand for core sparkling products. (T1_OFFICIAL) | Requires ongoing portfolio diversification into water, juice, coffee, sports, and energy categories to maintain category relevance. |
| Bottling Partner Dependency | Reliance on independent bottling partners means the company does not control all production, distribution quality, and customer service touchpoints. Partner financial difficulties could affect market execution. (T1_OFFICIAL) | Brand consistency risk exists where bottling partners underinvest in quality or coverage; reputational exposure not fully within company control. |
| Regulatory & Tax | Changes in tax laws, tariffs, and beverage-specific regulations (including sugar taxes) could increase operating costs or reduce demand in affected markets. (T1_OFFICIAL) | Sugar taxes in multiple jurisdictions directly affect pricing and volume, requiring product reformulation or portfolio shifts. |
| Water Availability | Operations depend heavily on water access; scarcity or quality deterioration in key production markets could disrupt manufacturing. (T1_OFFICIAL) | Water stewardship is a brand equity component; failure to demonstrate responsible usage could affect ESG-sensitive institutional investors and consumers. |

## 8. Brand-Finance Nexus

### 8.1 What Financial Data Reveals About Brand Strategy

| Financial Signal | Brand Strategy Interpretation |
|-----------------|------------------------------|
| ~59% gross margin sustained over 5 years | The concentrate model creates structural brand leverage: brand equity monetized through licensing, not manufacturing, preserving margin through cycles. (T4_INFERRED) |
| Dividends >$7.6B annually for 5 consecutive years | Capital allocation prioritizes income investors, consistent with a brand identity built on reliability and long-term institutional trust rather than growth speculation. (T4_INFERRED) |
| SG&A at ~30% of revenue | Sustained investment in marketing, sales, and distribution reflects a brand competing on ubiquity and emotional connection rather than product differentiation alone. (T4_INFERRED) |
| Revenue growth FY2023–FY2025 despite sector headwinds | Indicates pricing power: the ability to raise prices without proportionate volume loss — a hallmark of strong consumer brand equity. (T4_INFERRED) |
| Total assets >$100B with CapEx only ~4% of revenue | Asset base dominated by intangibles (brand value, franchise rights, goodwill) rather than physical manufacturing — a brand-driven, not production-driven, business model. (T4_INFERRED) |
| $10.24B total shareholder return in FY2025 | Capital returned exceeds net income in some years, financed partly through debt — management views the brand as a reliable cash engine supporting leveraged capital allocation. (T4_INFERRED) |
