## Core Personas

### Persona 1 — The Suburban Family Shopper ("The Target Run Mom")

- **Age / Occupation**: 36, working parent, household income ~$85,000 (estimated), two children under 10
- **Nickname internally recognized**: The "Target run" shopper — enters for toothpaste, exits with $150 in basket
- **Core pain**: Time-scarce; needs a single store that covers grocery, household, kids' clothing, and seasonal items without sacrificing the pleasure of finding something nice. Does not want the parking lot anxiety of Walmart or the price anxiety of specialty retail.
- **Target relationship**: Weekly or bi-weekly in-store visits; Drive Up user for repeat purchases; Target Circle member for the 1% earning and surprise birthday offer. The store is a known-quantity pleasure — predictable quality, reliable availability, occasional design discovery.
- **Brand language**: "I only went in for one thing." "It's just so much nicer than Walmart." "Good & Gather is genuinely good." (T4_INFERRED from widely reported consumer sentiment; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- **Churn risk**: Low for core household categories; moderate when a nearby Costco or Amazon Subscribe & Save offers better economics on bulk staples.
- **Media touchpoints**: Target Circle app push notifications; Instagram (Target's visual brand performs well on feed); Sunday circular (digital); Pinterest for home décor discovery

---

### Persona 2 — The Design-Conscious Millennial ("Tarzhay Devotee")

- **Age / Occupation**: 29, urban or near-suburban, renter turning first-time homeowner, household income ~$70,000 (estimated)
- **Core cultural identity**: Uses "Tarzhay" (faux-French pronunciation of Target) as affectionate marker of the brand's aspirational-but-accessible position. Aware of the irony; embraces it.
- **Core pain**: Wants a well-designed home and wardrobe but cannot justify Restoration Hardware or Madewell prices. Needs the Threshold throw blanket that photographs like a $200 item and costs $35.
- **Target relationship**: Discovery-forward; responds strongly to designer collaboration launches (Lilly Pulitzer, Hunter, Vineyard Vines for Target). Follows @target on Instagram. Shares hauls on TikTok with "#targetrun" content.
- **Purchase trigger**: Limited-edition collab launch; seasonal home refresh; a viral TikTok showing a Target find. Scarcity is real for designer collabs — the Lilly Pulitzer 2015 sellout and website crash are cultural memory for this persona. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE)
- **Churn risk**: Higher than Persona 1 — if Target's design quality slips or a competitor (e.g., IKEA for home, H&M for fashion) offers equivalent aesthetic at lower price, this persona migrates.
- **Media touchpoints**: Instagram (@target has 4M+ followers, estimated); TikTok (#targetrun has billions of views, estimated); Pinterest home boards; Target Circle app

---

### Persona 3 — The College Student ("Dorm Outfitter")

- **Age / Occupation**: 19, full-time student, budget ~$500–$1,500 for dorm setup (estimated); often shopping with parental card
- **Core pain**: Needs to furnish an 11×12-foot room, buy toiletries, bedding, school supplies, and a Keurig in a single shopping trip, on a budget, from one store.
- **Target relationship**: High-frequency during back-to-school season (July–September); loyalty formed early and often retained through post-college household formation. Target explicitly targets this lifecycle inflection point with college registry programs and back-to-school campaigns. (official, Target back-to-school marketing; target.com/college)
- **Purchase trigger**: Move-in week; semester start supplies; late-night snack and household runs during semester. Target's proximity to college campuses (often the nearest large-format general merchandise retailer) is a structural advantage.
- **Churn risk**: Low during college years (captive market); moderate after graduation as income rises and Costco / Amazon become more attractive for some categories.
- **Media touchpoints**: TikTok; Instagram; Target app; email promotions

---

### Persona 4 — The Teacher ("Classroom Shopper")

- **Age / Occupation**: 33, K–12 teacher, household income ~$55,000 (estimated); often spends personal income on classroom supplies
- **Cultural salience**: Target has cultivated a specific affinity with the teacher community through its Teacher Appreciation programs, back-to-school assortment, and Target Circle educational discounts. The Teacher persona is a documented loyalty segment. (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE; official, Target educational discount program)
- **Core pain**: Classroom budget is inadequate; buys supplies out of pocket; needs the best value per dollar on construction paper, markers, organizational bins, and snacks for class parties.
- **Target relationship**: Back-to-school season is the peak purchase window; relies on Target Circle teacher discount (historically 15% off select school supplies during back-to-school, subject to change). Dollar Spot (now Bullseye's Playground) is a key destination for inexpensive classroom items.
- **Churn risk**: Low — strong emotional affinity, loyalty-reinforced by teacher-specific programming.
- **Media touchpoints**: Target app; Instagram (educator community active on platform); email; in-store seasonal end-caps

## Target Circle — Loyalty Program Architecture

Target Circle, launched in October 2019 (replacing Cartwheel and the previous loyalty beta), is Target's primary loyalty mechanism. Key features as disclosed in official communications:

| Feature | Detail | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **Earnings rate** | 1% on every eligible purchase, redeemable as Target Circle earnings on future purchases | (official, target.com/circle) |
| **Birthday offer** | Personalized discount delivered on member's birthday | (official, target.com/circle) |
| **Circle Deals** | Personalized and universal offers on specific products; activated in app or at checkout | (official, Target app) |
| **Target Circle Card** | Co-branded credit/debit card offering 5% savings on Target purchases (formerly REDcard) | (official, target.com; renamed as part of Circle rebrand) |
| **Target Circle 360** | Paid membership tier (~$99/year, estimated based on public pricing) bundling free same-day delivery via Shipt and additional benefits | (official, target.com/circle360) |
| **Membership scale** | Over 100 million Target Circle members (estimated; not formally disclosed as of FY2023 10-K, but widely reported) | (T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE) |

The Target Circle Card (formerly REDcard) carries strategic significance beyond discount: it enables direct debit from checking accounts (debit version), increasing purchase frequency and average basket size. (T4_INFERRED from observable retail loyalty economics; T3_SECONDARY_RELIABLE, retail industry analysis)

## Anti-Persona — Who Target Is Not For

| Type | Characteristics | Why Target Is Not the Match |
|------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Pure price maximizer** | Makes purchase decisions on lowest unit price alone; shops multiple stores to optimize every line item; no aesthetic consideration | Walmart, Aldi, and Dollar General offer lower prices on many commodity items. Target's value proposition requires the guest to value the experience and aesthetic, not only the price. |
| **Luxury buyer** | Purchases signal status; brand exclusivity is required; price is evidence of quality, not a concern | Target's accessible price point is incompatible with luxury status signaling. The Lilly Pulitzer collab was appealing precisely because it was accessible — the opposite of the luxury buyer's goal. |
| **Bulk/warehouse buyer** | Buys in quantities suited to a household of 8 or a small business; prioritizes unit economics over unit experience | Costco and Sam's Club own this segment. Target's store format and SKU depth are not suited to bulk purchasing. |
| **Pure digital native** | Prefers to never enter a physical store; views shopping as a logistics problem, not an experience | Amazon dominates this segment. Target's brand equity is substantially anchored in the physical store experience. The "Target run" phenomenon does not translate to a pure-digital context. |

## Steal Sheet — 3 Transferable Principles

**1. Build the loyalty program around behavior change, not points accumulation.**
Target Circle's most powerful retention mechanism is not the 1% earning — it is the behavioral shift from in-store checkout to app engagement, and from standard checkout to Drive Up use. Each new behavior adopted increases switching cost. Transferable structure: design your loyalty program to reward the behaviors that deepen platform integration (mobile app use, recurring order setup, review writing) rather than simply spending volume.

**2. Design for the life-stage inflection point, then retain through the transition.**
Target's college back-to-school program is not profitable in the short term — a college student buying $400 of dorm supplies at minimal margin is not Target's best customer. But the brand relationship formed at that inflection point carries through household formation, first apartment furnishing, and young family shopping. Transferable structure: identify the entry-point life stage for your category and design a specific, high-value experience for it, even at reduced margin, because the lifetime customer relationship begins there.

**3. Let the audience name the brand.**
"Tarzhay" was not invented by Target's marketing department. It emerged organically from customers who wanted a signal for the brand's aspirational-but-accessible identity. Target's response was to embrace it rather than correct it — the faux-French pronunciation is now used in advertising and media coverage without irony. Transferable principle: monitor what your most loyal customers call you or say about you informally, and consider whether adopting that language in your own communications would close the authenticity gap between brand voice and customer voice.
